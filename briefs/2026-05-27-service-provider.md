## HB 815 is a roofing-revenue rebalance, not a loss

The story your homeowner customers are reading frames House Bill 815 (effective **July 1, 2026**) as "carriers can't drop you for an old roof." For roofers, it's more complicated — but the net is favorable if you reposition fast.

**What changes for your work mix on July 1:**

- The classic "replace the whole roof to save the policy" job — driven by a non-renewal letter on a 12-18 year-old roof — largely goes away. Some of that revenue evaporates.
- In its place: **roof inspection becomes a recurring billable service** for every 15+ year-old roof in your service area. The carrier is legally required to honor an authorized inspector's certification. That's a $400-$600 line item on tens of thousands of South Florida roofs over the next 24 months.
- **Coating systems on low-slope roofs become a statutorily-blessed alternative to replacement.** If your inspector certifies that a coating extends useful life by 5+ years, the carrier can't deny coverage. Elastomeric and silicone coating installation just got handed a structural tailwind.

**Cross-sell stack to build this month:**

1. **Roof inspector certification** — if you don't have a Florida-licensed inspector on staff, that's the highest-leverage hire or contract in your 2026 plan.
2. **Coating system capability** — silicone and elastomeric, with proper substrate prep. If you're not already quoting it, get one product line approved and trained on by end of June.
3. **Bundle pricing** for the inspection-plus-coating sequence. "$500 inspection credited toward a $4-7K coating job if you proceed within 60 days" pulls homeowners off the fence.

Don't undersell the inspections as a loss leader — they're the relationship entry point for the much larger coating or partial-replacement work.

## The new wind-mit form (1802) is a 30-day credential window

OIR's updated **Uniform Mitigation Verification Inspection Form** took effect April 1. The change is more documentation per credit — specific product approval numbers, photos, permit references for each feature. Inspectors who haven't trained on the new form will produce work that gets rejected.

**Your operational read:**

- Roofers, garage door installers, and impact window contractors with a current-trained 1802 inspector on staff have a tighter cross-sell window in 2026 than ever. The pitch writes itself: *"We'll quote the upgrade and run the updated wind mitigation form — most homeowners recapture 20-30% of the upgrade cost in year-one premium savings."*
- If your inspector isn't current on the April 2026 revision, fix that **this month**. After June 1, every quote that doesn't include the 1802 component is leaving 20-30% of the value on the table.

**Lead-gen language that lands** with homeowners: "Are you currently claiming the wind mitigation credits you're entitled to? On a $4,500 premium, the full credit set runs $800-$1,500 a year off your bill. We can quote the upgrade and run the new form together." That single line moves more pre-season cold-traffic than any seasonal urgency play.

Source: [FLOIR — Wind Mitigation Resources](https://floir.gov/consumers/wind-mitigation-resources)

The state regulator approved three more property carriers last week: **Builder Reciprocal** (Texas-based, focused on newer-home communities), **Frontline Reciprocal** (Lake Mary FL, statewide), **Wingsail** (Arizona, Spinnaker-owned). Total post-reform new entrants now stands at **20**, with combined fresh capital over **$850 million**.

For your business this means **three new approved-vendor panels** standing up in Florida over the next 6-12 months. Restoration firms, roofers, plumbers, water mitigation, HVAC — all of them get on approved-vendor lists by application.

**This-month action:**

- Pull the OIR vendor postings for the three new carriers.
- Get your COI, license copies, BBB rating, and any manufacturer certifications into a single pre-application packet that you can submit in under 30 minutes per carrier.
- Apply early. Approved-vendor positions get harder to win once the panel is full.

The historical data is clear: the firms that landed on Heritage and Citizens panels in the first 18 months captured a disproportionate share of post-storm direct-repair volume for years. Same dynamic with the new entrants — but the window for first-mover positioning is the next two quarters.

## "Below-normal" forecast is a demand-suppression headline you have to fight

NOAA's 2026 outlook: **8-14 named storms**, 3-6 hurricanes, 1-3 major. The mechanism is a developing El Niño. The media headline that's already landing in your customers' phones is "quieter season ahead."

That's the opposite of what your June calendar needs. Pre-season roof, shutter, generator, and tree-trim bookings drop when the headline reads "below normal."

**Counter-messaging that works in homeowner conversations:**

1. **"It only takes one."** NOAA Administrator Neil Jacobs led with that line. So should you. Andrew (1992) was a Category 5 in a below-normal season. Below-normal is *probability*, not protection.
2. **Pricing math.** The work doesn't get cheaper after the first cone enters the Florida basin. Pre-season pricing is the floor for the year — every named storm tightens trade capacity and pushes prices up.
3. **Inspection backlog quadruples within 48 hours of any landfall.** A pre-season inspection is on your calendar inside a week; a post-storm inspection is 3-6 weeks out. Frame the same job as "now, by appointment" vs. "after the storm, in line."

If your June outreach hasn't been written, lead with those three points and the June 1 binding-window tightening. That's the message homeowners need to hear this week.
