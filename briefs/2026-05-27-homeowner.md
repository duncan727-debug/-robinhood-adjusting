# South Florida Homeowner Brief
**May 27, 2026** | Palm Beach, Martin, Miami-Dade, St. Lucie, Broward

> Hurricane season opens in **5 days** (June 1). NOAA's outlook says below-normal — but the practical posture is the same: get your prep done this week, before the carriers tighten their binding windows.

---

## Your roof age just stopped being a renewal-killer

If you've ever opened a renewal letter that said "we're not renewing because your roof is too old," **that letter becomes much harder for a carrier to send after July 1**. House Bill 815 takes effect that day and rewrites the rules. The short version:

- Carriers **cannot** refuse to issue or renew a policy solely because the roof is **less than 15 years old**. That kills the recent drift toward 12- and 10-year cutoffs that crept in on some carriers.
- If your roof **is** 15+ years old, you now have an enforceable right to a licensed inspection **before** any non-renewal. If the inspector certifies 5+ years of useful life remaining (on a sloped roof) or that a coating system can extend life by 5+ years (on a flat roof), the carrier cannot drop you on age alone.

**What to do this week:** if your roof is between 12 and 18 years old, get the year-of-install number off your closing documents or the original permit. If you've already received a "concerned about your roof" letter this cycle, a $400-$600 inspection in late June is almost always cheaper than the "just replace it" route, and after July 1 it's enforceable. Don't pay for a full roof replacement on age-pressure alone before you've had the inspection conversation.

Source: [Florida Senate — HB 815 (2026)](https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2026/815)

## Wind-mit credits got tighter — and probably better

Florida's updated **wind mitigation form (OIR-B1-1802)** took effect April 1. It changes both what credits you can claim and what documentation your inspector needs to claim them. The headline points for you as a homeowner:

- Your **existing** 1802 is still valid through its 5-year window. You don't need to re-inspect immediately just because the form changed.
- If you've upgraded your roof, garage door, or windows since your last inspection, a fresh 1802 under the new form is the cheapest way to capture credits you may not have claimed before. Some features (notably modern hip-roof geometry with documented strapping) qualify for **higher** discounts under the revised tables.
- Inspectors now have to provide specific product approval numbers, photo documentation, and permit references for each credit. The looser "visually inspected and consistent with" language no longer carries the same weight — which means a 2026 form is harder to argue with at renewal.

**The math:** on a $4,500 annual premium, a full credit set (hurricane straps + secondary water barrier + impact openings) can pull **$800-$1,500 a year** off the bill. That's $4,000-$7,500 over the 5-year validity window.

**What to do this week:** call your agent and ask "what wind mitigation credits am I currently getting?" If they can't answer it in 60 seconds, that's the signal to schedule an inspection.

Source: [FLOIR — Wind Mitigation Resources](https://floir.gov/consumers/wind-mitigation-resources)

## You may have more carrier choices than you realize

The state regulator confirmed last week that **three more carriers** have been approved to write property insurance in Florida — Builder Reciprocal, Frontline Reciprocal, and Wingsail. That brings the post-2023-reform total to **20 new carriers** with combined fresh capital over **$850 million**.

If your last quote came back as "Citizens or nothing," that answer is getting more out-of-date every quarter. Two of the new entrants (Builder Reciprocal and Frontline Reciprocal) are **reciprocal exchanges**, which means the policyholders are the member-owners — a structural difference that doesn't change anything visible at renewal time but does change the company's incentives.

**Don't dismiss an unfamiliar carrier name automatically — but do check:**
1. The AM Best rating (B+ or better is the usual minimum lenders accept)
2. The Demotech rating (most Florida domestic carriers use Demotech rather than AM Best — A or better is the floor)
3. Whether your mortgage servicer accepts the carrier (call them with the carrier name before binding)

**What to do this week:** if your renewal is coming up in June or July, ask an independent agent — not a captive agent of your current carrier — to quote two or three of the newer entrants alongside your incumbent.

## Citizens just filed for a small rate decrease — but the depopulation push continues

Two pieces of Citizens (state-run insurer) news worth noting together:

1. **Citizens filed for a 2.6% statewide rate decrease** for personal multi-peril policies. If approved, it takes effect later this year. This is the third small decrease in a row — the underlying signal is that Citizens' book is stabilizing.
2. Citizens has also **cleared 546,000 policies** through depopulation since the 2023 reforms — moved them off the state's books and onto private carriers. If you got a "your policy is being transferred to [Carrier X]" letter in the last 12 months, that's depopulation.

**For you:** if you're currently with Citizens, you may get a depopulation offer this summer. You **can opt out** and keep your Citizens policy — but if the private offer is within 20% of your Citizens premium, the private policy almost always comes with broader coverage and fewer assessment risk. Read the offer letter carefully and call your agent before the response deadline.

## Hurricane season opens June 1 — what to actually do this week

NOAA's 2026 outlook is **below-normal**: 8-14 named storms, 3-6 hurricanes, 1-3 major. The mechanism is a developing El Niño. **None of this changes your prep list.** It only takes one storm — Andrew (1992) was a below-normal-season Category 5.

Your 5-day list, in order of "do this first":

1. **Bind, don't drift.** If you're shopping a new policy or renewing in June/July, bind on day one of the cycle. Most carriers stop binding new wind policies **24-72 hours** before a named storm enters the Florida cone. "I'll get to it next week" is the most expensive sentence in coastal property.
2. **Photograph everything.** Inside and out, including serial numbers on appliances, A/C units, and any high-value contents. Upload to cloud storage. This is the single highest-leverage 30 minutes of hurricane prep.
3. **Confirm flood + wind coverage is actually active.** Two separate policies for most South Florida homeowners. Call your agent and ask "is my wind policy in force and what's the deductible? Is my flood policy in force and what's the coverage limit?" Get the answers in writing if you can.
4. **Exercise shutters or impact panels.** If they haven't been moved since last season, they often need adjustment or fastener replacement. Better to know now.
5. **Trim trees that overhang the roof or driveway.** Small jobs by June 1; very expensive jobs by June 15.
