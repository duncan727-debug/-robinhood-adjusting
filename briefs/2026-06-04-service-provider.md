## Job-Category Signal #1: SIRS Wave Is Here — Concrete, Envelope, and Restoration GCs Should Be Booked Through Q4

The Structural Integrity Reserve Study (SIRS) outer-pairing deadline is **December 31, 2026** — 210 days out — for buildings whose milestone inspections were tied to a SIRS-completion timeline. Across coastal Miami-Dade, Broward, and PBC, the buildings that have been kicking scope decisions down the road no longer can.

What this looks like as pipeline:
- **Concrete restoration.** Spalling, post-tension cable repair, balcony reconstruction. Per industry analyses of post-Surfside SIRS outcomes, special assessments on older mid-rises are landing in the **$134K–$400K per unit** range — much of that figure is concrete and envelope scope.
- **Waterproofing and coatings.** Above-grade and below-grade systems specified by the engineer of record. Specs typically run NACE Level II or equivalent inspector requirement; if you don't have that on staff, build the sub relationship now.
- **Window and door replacement.** Often pulled into the same scope when the envelope is open. Particularly true on HVHZ buildings (Miami-Dade, Broward), where the **HVHZ 30% rule** can drag the entire envelope into current-code compliance.
- **Restoration GCs.** Multi-trade coordination is the bottleneck. Boards want one number, one schedule, one accountable contractor.

**How to pitch into this in the next 60 days:**
1. Pull your county's condo-association mailing addresses (PBC, Broward, Miami-Dade clerks each publish association registries) and segment the list by building year — focus first on buildings constructed **before 1996**.
2. Time your outreach to land **the week before** the association's next board meeting (most condo boards meet monthly, often the second Tuesday or Wednesday — published in the association docs).
3. Lead with the **engineer's report**, not your services. Boards are drowning in unsolicited GC pitches; what they need is help reading a 60-page engineer's-of-record letter.

## Job-Category Signal #2: Roof Pipeline Splits in Two on July 1

**HB 815** takes effect July 1 — we covered it Monday. Short version for roofers and GCs working residential: the certified-inspector pathway closes the prior "roof age" hard cap on renewal. The practical effect is bifurcation:

- **Older asphalt-shingle roofs (15–25 years)** that previously would have been forced into replacement at renewal can now be **certified for an extension** by a licensed inspector based on condition, not age. Expect a meaningful share of homeowners who were resigned to a replacement to opt for inspection + targeted repair instead.
- **Standing-seam metal and tile** continue to capture share on new construction and re-roofs, partly because the **OIR-B1-1802 wind-mit form** (in effect since April 1, 2026) credits stronger opening protection and roof systems more generously than the prior version.

Material-cost reality has not changed: per industry trade coverage, roofing materials remain **15–25% higher than 2024 baselines** driven by Section 232 tariffs on steel and aluminum (50% rate) covering 400-plus derivative categories — drip edge, flashing, prefab panels included. Aluminum mill shapes are up **33%** YoY; steel mill products **20.7%**.

**Practical move for the next 30 days:**
1. Add a **certified-inspection product** to your menu at a clean price point ($350–$550 typical band) and market it side-by-side with replacement quotes. The inspection becomes the customer's "get a second opinion" before they accept the carrier's non-renewal letter.
2. **Material-escalator clauses in residential contracts.** If you wrote contracts in early 2026 that lock fixed material pricing through August, you are eating margin. Update template language now.
3. **Wind-mit form coordination.** If you don't have a wind-mit-certified inspector partner, find one this month. The OIR-B1-1802 form premium-relief number is the homeowner's primary motivation to call you.

## Job-Category Signal #3: SB 266 Is About PAs, But It's Also a Cross-Sell Door

**Senate Bill 266** (effective July 1, 2026 per filed text) overhauls Florida Statute §626.854 — the public-adjuster contracting statute. The big changes: open-ended rescission rights for vulnerable adults, mandatory **18-point font** for contract disclosures, and a new disciplinary ground for taking advantage of vulnerable insureds.

Why this matters for trades — even though you're not a PA:

1. **Storm and water-loss work flows through PAs.** A meaningful share of residential restoration, mold remediation, and emergency tarp work comes from PA-driven claims. Stronger consumer-protection rules on the PA side reshape who calls you and when.
2. **Vendor-relationship documentation matters more.** If you have a referral or AOB relationship with a PA, expect renewed scrutiny of those arrangements under the new disciplinary framework. Keep your paper clean.
3. **Direct homeowner channels are getting easier.** With clearer PA disclosure rules and tighter solicitation oversight, the in-person homeowner conversation is more durable than the PA-broker handoff. Lean into branded direct outreach.

For Duncan and other Florida PAs working in this market, the operational lift is: refresh every contract template by July 1, add a vulnerable-adult screening step at intake, and document the affirmation.

## CE Deadline at 88 Days: Don't Get Caught

Florida construction-trades license renewals require continuing-education completion by **midnight EST on August 31, 2026** for the current cycle. Roofing, HVAC, plumbing, and general-contractor licenses are all covered. Per Florida DBPR Construction Industry guidance, the hours and topic requirements depend on license type — the **roofing CE** typically includes wind-mitigation and code-update modules.

CE is the kind of thing every owner-operator says they'll handle in August. Block 4 hours next week. If your license lapses you are uninsured on the next job, which means a single liability claim during the lapse is on your personal balance sheet.

For trades the practical signal:
