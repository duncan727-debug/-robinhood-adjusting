## TOP STORIES

### Citizens' $400K Flood Mandate Is Live — Add It to Your Disclosure Conversation

Effective **January 1, 2026**, Citizens Property Insurance Corporation requires every policyholder with **Coverage A of $400,000 or more** to carry flood insurance — **regardless of FEMA flood zone, including Zone X**. Without proof of an active National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) policy or an approved private flood policy by the next renewal date, Citizens non-renews the underlying homeowners policy. Phase 2 on **January 1, 2027** extends the requirement to **all** single-family Citizens policies.

The driver, per Citizens' flood page: Helene and Milton pushed surge and inland flooding into Zone X across the Tampa Bay and Treasure Coast corridors. Most non-condo housing west of I-95 in Palm Beach County, Broward, and Miami-Dade crosses the $400K Coverage A threshold once current rebuild costs are factored in.

**What it means at the closing table.** If your buyer is taking over a Citizens policy at $400K+ Coverage A, flood proof needs to be in place by the renewal date — and the financing contingency conversation should reflect it. If your seller has a recent non-renewal that referenced flood, that is now a material item to surface pre-listing, not at inspection. Two practical scripts:

- **For listing intake:** "Is your homeowners with Citizens? If so, what's the Coverage A on the declarations page, and do you carry flood?"
- **For buyer-side due diligence:** "Will the buyer be assuming a Citizens policy? If so, has flood quoting started on both NFIP and private?"

*Source: Citizens Flood page; Flood Insurance Guru summary; Statewide Insurance summary.*

### Florida Is the Largest Private Flood Market in the Country — Shop Both Sides

Private flood insurance now represents an estimated **35% of Florida's flood market**, with **600,000+ privately insured properties** — the largest private flood book in the country. Versus NFIP:

- **Dwelling limits** up to $10M (private) vs. NFIP's $250K cap
- **Replacement-cost contents** vs. NFIP's actual-cash-value contents
- **Loss-of-use** covered by some private policies; NFIP excludes it entirely
- **Price** runs 10–30% less than NFIP for favorable-risk homes (newer construction, above base flood elevation); NFIP remains the safer bet for repetitive-loss and high-risk properties

Backdrop: **NFIP reauthorization expires September 30, 2026.** Since 2017, the program has been reauthorized via 30+ short-term extensions; the political risk of a lapse remains real.

**What it means.** "Did you shop NFIP versus private?" is now a load-bearing question on every closing in or near a flood zone, and on every Citizens-policy renewal at $400K+ Coverage A. For listings priced for newer construction or homes elevated above base flood elevation, lining up a private quote pre-listing can shave hundreds to low thousands off the buyer's annual carrying cost — a real factor in closing conversion.

**For September–early November closings:** include the NFIP-lapse scenario in the financing contingency. Lenders generally require flood coverage in Special Flood Hazard Areas. Confirm whether the listing carries assumable NFIP, a private policy, or both.

*Source: Harbour Insurance — Private vs. NFIP 2026; Comegys 2026 flood guide.*

### Florida Building Code 9th Edition — 211 Days to December 31

The Florida Building Code (FBC) 9th Edition takes effect **December 31, 2026**. Permits **pulled before** the effective date generally stay under the 8th Edition through completion; permits **pulled after** fall under the 9th Edition. The 8th Edition took effect December 31, 2023 — the regulatory cadence has held at three years.

Expected changes: updated wind-load tables, sealed-roof-deck refinements, revised energy-code provisions. For High-Velocity Hurricane Zone (HVHZ) counties — Miami-Dade and Broward only — the **HVHZ 30% rule** can pull the entire building envelope into current code when substantial alterations are made. A 9th-Edition roof replacement on an older HVHZ home can drag windows, doors, and attachments into the new code on the same job.

**What it means for listings and renovations.** For sellers with discretionary projects on the calendar (roof, addition, large remodel), permit-pull date is now a pricing variable. For buyers walking into homes with in-progress or recently-completed work in Miami-Dade or Broward, ask about the **permit-pull date** as part of due diligence — assemblies permitted before December 31 will not be required to meet 9th-Edition standards even if installed in 2027.

*Source: Revolution Roofing FBC 9th Edition timeline; Florida Building Commission.*

## REGULATORY & COMPLIANCE COUNTDOWN

### Condo Milestone Inspection + SIRS — December 31, 2026 (211 Days Out)

Buildings reaching the 30-year age threshold in 2026 must complete their milestone inspection — and may complete the Structural Integrity Reserve Study (SIRS) simultaneously — by **December 31, 2026**. SIRS reserve funding under the new statute began **January 1, 2026** for affected associations.

For any condo listing in this category, the buyer-side question is no longer hypothetical: "where is the building in the milestone process, and has the SIRS been completed?" An association without a signed engineering engagement by midsummer is signaling either neglect or impending special assessment — both material disclosures. Engineer capacity for the second half of 2026 is already tight in PBC, Broward, and Miami-Dade.

**At-closing language:** "Has the association completed the milestone inspection and SIRS, or scheduled them? If scheduled, when, and with which engineer? Has the board discussed assessment funding?" Get it in writing from the association before contract.

*Source: Florida Statute 553.899; DBPR Condo Inspections.*

## HB 459 — A Bill That Would Change How Disputed Claims Resolve

**House Bill 459**, proposed in the 2026 session, would create a mandatory Division of Administrative Hearings (DOAH) procedure for disputed property insurance claims: either side may file a petition, an Administrative Law Judge issues a **coverage decision within 60 days** and a **final dollar figure within 180 days**. The bill is **proposed — not yet law.** Carrier groups support; the trial bar opposes.

**What it means.** For listings where a current or recent claim is in dispute, today's expected resolution timeline is 12–24 months in circuit court. Under HB 459, it would compress to roughly six months. That changes how a pending-claim disclosure reads to a buyer and lender. Watch the committee calendar through the 2026 session.

*Source: HB 459 — Florida Senate; HB 459 — Florida House; G&G Law Group analysis.*
