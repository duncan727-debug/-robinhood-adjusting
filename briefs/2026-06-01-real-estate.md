## The insurance binder conversation is materially friendlier this week

Two rate decreases land in the same window:

- **Citizens Property Insurance** — statewide **2.6% personal-lines cut** is **effective today**. Roughly **3 in 5** Citizens policyholders see an average reduction of **11.5%, or about $359**. This is the first Citizens rate cut since 2015.
- **Heritage Property & Casualty** — announced an **up to 5% reduction** for **tri-county policyholders (Miami-Dade, Broward, Palm Beach)**. Company-wide average decrease is roughly 3.3%; the tri-county zone gets the deepest cut. The 26,000-home / 11.9% number circulating in some coverage is a Governor-office figure for the broader market-relief package across multiple carriers — **not Heritage alone**.

Three additional carriers (**Builder Reciprocal Insurance Exchange**, **Frontline Reciprocal Exchange** — June 18 new-business start, **Wingsail Insurance**) were also approved on May 20, bringing the total to **20 new entrants** and **$850M+ of fresh capital** in the Florida market since the 2022-2023 legislative reforms.

**What this changes for a buyer-rep conversation:** The first thing a buyer asks at the contract stage is what the binder will cost. For Citizens-eligible properties in moderate-risk PBC zip codes, today's quote is materially better than the quote you would have referenced a month ago. For buyers being routed to Heritage, the PBC-specific decrease is a positive datapoint to surface in the financing-contingency conversation. Recommend that the buyer's independent agent re-shop the binder if the original quote came in before today.

**What this changes for a listing presentation:** "Insurability" has gone from a five-minute objection to handle to a near-zero objection in most PBC and Broward inland geographies. The narrative shifts from "Florida insurance is broken" to "the post-reform market is settling, and your buyer has more carrier paper to choose from than a year ago." Update your seller-prep deck.

## HB 815 — what every buyer will ask you starting July 1

Your buyers are reading the same coverage you are. In 30 days, **House Bill 815** takes effect, and the question *"how old is the roof?"* moves from a deal-killer to a manageable diligence item.

The mechanics, in language usable on a showing:

- **Under 15 years old.** No carrier can refuse to issue or renew solely on roof age. Full stop.
- **15 years or older, steep-slope.** Carrier must allow an authorized inspector to certify condition. If the inspector certifies **≥5 years of useful life**, the carrier cannot deny on roof-age grounds.
- **15 years or older, low-slope.** Same test, with a separate **coating-system pathway** — if a coating can extend useful life by 5 years, the roof stays insurable without a tear-off.

The bill applies to policies **issued or renewed on or after July 1, 2026**. June closings still operate under the old rules.

**What to put in the disclosure conversation:** For listings with a 15-to-20-year-old roof, a useful-life inspection completed before closing is now the cleanest path to a binder. Recommend the seller order an authorized inspector report **with closing scheduled in July or later** — it survives the financing-contingency window and removes a major buyer objection. For listings with a 12-to-14-year-old roof, the July 1 floor handles it automatically — no inspection needed.

**What to write into the contract for July-and-later closings:** A new contingency line should reference the **roof-age verification under HB 815** rather than a flat replacement requirement. Your title or attorney partner can draft the standard language. Brokerages should expect to circulate a revised contingency template by mid-June.

## The federal flood watchlist — closings scheduled for Q4 need this on the radar

The **National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP)** is authorized through **September 30, 2026**. A federally appointed council has recommended structural changes — shifting more disaster costs to state and local government, reducing FEMA's workforce, and moving flood policies toward private-market oversight. None of this affects today's binder. All of this is in play for the reauthorization vote due before September 30.

Florida holds **~1.8 million NFIP policies** — more than any other state.

**What to do this week:** For any flood-zone listing under contract with a Q4 2026 close, add a one-line note to the buyer-rep file: *"Watch NFIP reauthorization status as Sept 30 approaches; confirm flood binder availability 30 days before close."* A lapse is unlikely but not impossible, and a Q4 closing that gets caught in an NFIP gap is a real transactional risk.

A related Florida-specific deadline: **January 1, 2027** is when **Citizens** universal phase mandates flood insurance on **all wind policies**, regardless of home value. If your seller is a Citizens policyholder, the buyer will inherit that obligation on transfer.
