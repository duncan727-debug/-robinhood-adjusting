### Wellington's Land-Use Land-Grab Crystallizes — Arden Annexation Talks Plus Kolter's 579-Home Approval Plus Related Ross's 605,000-Sq-Ft Mixed-Use Complex
What looked last fall like three separate Wellington land-use stories has resolved into a single arc: the Village is moving aggressively to absorb adjacent unincorporated parcels and the most consequential development pipeline in central Palm Beach County is being approved inside that perimeter.

Three threads, one map:
- **Arden annexation** — Wellington and Palm Beach County are negotiating an interlocal service boundary agreement that would move roughly **7,000 Arden residents** out of unincorporated PBC and into the Village. Discussions are public and active. Arden today is the closest large master-planned community to the proposed 200-acre data center site that surfaced two weeks ago.
- **Artistry Lakes / Kolter** — The Village Council formally annexed the 446-acre Kolter Group parcel on **March 10, 2026**. Kolter has filed plans for **579 single-family homes** in a gated configuration; the multifamily and commercial components in the earlier proposal were dropped.
- **Related Ross / Stribling Way** — Stephen Ross's firm received final Wellington Village Council height approvals on **March 11, 2026** for an **86-foot extended-stay hotel, a 47-foot lakeside restaurant pad, and two 42-foot commercial buildings** at 10400 Stribling Way. The underlying rezoning permits up to **210,000 sq ft of retail, 105,000 sq ft of restaurant, a 180-room hotel, 75,000 sq ft of office, 215 residential units**, and a **405,000-sq-ft Wingrove Academy** private school for 1,750 students.

Net: Wellington's residential and commercial inventory is set to expand by an order of magnitude over the next 36–60 months, and the Village — not the County — is now the entitlement authority for the bulk of it.

**What it means for homeowners:** If your home sits inside current Wellington limits, the tax-base story is favorable in the medium term — more commercial assessed value spreads fixed services across more rooftops. The near-term cost is construction traffic on Stribling, Forest Hill, and the 441 corridor, and the loss of the green-buffer character on the western edge. For Arden residents specifically, annexation would change permit, code-enforcement, and (possibly) trash and utility billing — watch the boundary-agreement public hearings before they close.

**What it means for real estate professionals:** Three immediate effects. First, listings within a 2-mile radius of the Related Ross site should be repositioned around the future amenity set — hotel, restaurants, school — for second-home and family-buyer narratives. Second, the Kolter 579-home inventory will hit the resale market in waves over 24–36 months; the comp set for existing Wellington product needs to be benchmarked now while pre-inventory pricing power still holds. Third, the Arden boundary question is a closing-table disclosure: any buyer asking "is this Wellington or unincorporated?" needs a current answer, not a six-month-old one.

**What it means for service providers:** New construction at this scale is a 2027–2029 service-and-warranty pipeline — roofs, HVAC, plumbing, landscape maintenance, pool service. Two preparatory moves to make this quarter: (1) get on the approved-vendor list with Kolter's regional builder operations team, and (2) network with the Related Ross general-contractor side now — punch-list and post-CO service contracts route to whoever was in the room early. Don't wait for the dust on the construction fencing.

### SB 266 Specifics — Vulnerable-Adult Rescission Plus $5,000-Per-Act Solicitation Fines, Effective July 1
**Senate Bill 266 (2026)** is the Burton bill cleaning up public-adjuster contract law. It takes effect **July 1, 2026** and carries two provisions every property owner and every PA-adjacent professional should understand.

**Provision 1 — Unconditional rescission right for vulnerable adults.** The bill amends Florida Statute § 626.854 to allow vulnerable adults, adults who lack capacity to consent, and their legal representatives to **rescind a public-adjuster services contract at any time, without penalty**. The existing 30-day-after-loss / 10-day-after-signing rescission window remains the floor for everyone else, but for protected populations the window is permanent.

**Provision 2 — $5,000 per-act penalty for soliciting vulnerable adults.** The Department of Financial Services is empowered to suspend, deny, or revoke a public-adjuster or apprentice license, and to assess fines of **up to $5,000 per act**, for soliciting a vulnerable adult.

The bill applies to all contracts entered into on or after July 1, 2026. Pre-effective-date contracts retain the older rescission terms.

**What it means for homeowners:** If you are caring for an aging parent or a disabled family member who has signed a public-adjuster contract — or who is being approached by one — your legal-representative authority to cancel is now an explicit, permanent right. The 30-day post-loss clock no longer applies to that population. Two practical actions: (1) inventory any existing PA contracts in the household and date them against July 1; (2) for any storm loss this season, route PA-engagement decisions for vulnerable family members through the legal representative before signing.

**What it means for service providers (especially restoration and water-mit):** Restoration crews on the ground are frequently the first to recommend a PA to a distressed homeowner. After July 1, that recommendation carries new compliance weight when the homeowner meets the vulnerable-adult definition. The cleanest policy: hand the homeowner a written list of three licensed PAs and let them choose — do not bring a single PA to the door. The $5,000 fine attaches to the solicitor, but contractor referral patterns can read as steering.

**What it means for real estate professionals:** Less direct exposure, but two angles. First, listing presentations on storm-damaged inventory now should reference SB 266 — the seller's family may have rescission rights on any PA contract previously signed, which can change the timeline for claim resolution before closing. Second, on estate sales and guardianship transactions, confirm the status of any open claim and any PA contract attached to it before listing.

### Mortgage Rates Stay at 9-Month High — Middle-East Oil Premium Is the Driver, Not the Fed
Freddie Mac's Primary Mortgage Market Survey held the **30-year fixed at 6.51%** in the week ending May 21, the highest reading since August 2025. The **15-year fixed sits at 5.85%**. Daily-rate trackers show the average 30-year purchase rate drifting to **6.704%** by May 22.

The driver has shifted. Through most of Q1 2026, mortgage rates moved on Fed expectations and CPI prints. Since the U.S.–Iran conflict opened, oil has carried a war-risk premium that's pushing through to headline inflation — and 10-year Treasury yields, which drive mortgage pricing, are rising on the inflation print rather than on growth or Fed signals. The mechanism is unusual but not new: oil up → inflation up → long yields up → mortgage rates up. The Fed cannot directly counteract a supply-shock inflation pulse.

**What it means for homeowners:** If you're shopping a refinance and were waiting for 6.0%, the supply-shock path means the wait may be longer than your origination cost-recovery math supports. Re-run the break-even at 6.51% before locking — but also re-run it at 6.25% if your originator can offer a buydown structure, which several Florida wholesale shops are now quoting more aggressively to keep deals moving.

**What it means for real estate professionals:** Two-week posture: assume 6.5%–6.75% is the base case for at least 30 days. For buyer-side conversations, lead with the buydown options and the seller-paid concession structures — those are the levers, not rate predictions. For seller-side, the time-on-market math at $500K–$900K inventory needs to be recut: a 25-bp move in rates moves the qualifying-buyer pool meaningfully at the conforming-loan-limit boundary.

**What it means for service providers:** Rate stickiness keeps the renovation-in-place vs. sell-and-move ratio tilted toward renovation. Kitchen, bath, roof-and-windows replacement, and ADU-conversion work all see the tailwind. Lead generation should emphasize "the move you're not making this year" framing — homeowners delaying a sale will reallocate that home-equity intent into a project.

## CONTRACTORS & SERVICE INDUSTRIES

### Four Days to Season Open — Final Pre-Hurricane Vendor-Lockup Window for Commercial Properties
Industry guidance from the commercial-property trades is converging on a single message this week: **May is the last serviceable window** to finalize hurricane-season service agreements before the first cone-watch event scrambles every roofer's, restoration firm's, and tarping contractor's schedule. Once a named storm enters the Florida cone, mobilization queues stretch to 5–14 days for non-priority customers, even at firms with surplus capacity.

The practical scope for any commercial property manager this week:
- **Pre-storm tarping and dry-in agreement** — signed, with a defined response-time SLA.
- **Roof and rooftop-equipment inspection done** — HVAC fastening, solar/satellite mounts, vent caps, flashing, drainage.
- **Generator service current** — load-bank tested within 6 months, fuel topped, transfer-switch verified.
- **Restoration-firm priority-customer agreement** — with insurance carrier pre-approval where the firm participates in a managed-repair network.

**What it means for service providers:** This is your June marketing message verbatim. Lead with the **calendar pressure**, not with discount pricing. A signed priority-response agreement at standard rate is more valuable to a commercial property than a 10% discount with no SLA. For owner-operators with capacity remaining: take the priority-customer slots first, then sell on volume.

**What it means for homeowners:** Same logic, smaller scale. The two highest-leverage moves this week are (1) book a 30-minute pre-season roof inspection with a contractor you'd want to be #3 on the call list if the cone hits, and (2) photograph the entire interior and exterior of the home, push the album to cloud, and note serial numbers on major appliances and HVAC equipment.
