## HB 815 lands July 1 — and the inspector letter becomes a SKU

In 30 days, **House Bill 815** takes effect. If you write roofing, restoration, or you run a Florida-authorized inspector practice, this changes what you sell.

The mechanics:

- **For roofs <15 years old:** Carriers can no longer use age alone to refuse to issue or renew. The age-based replacement push goes away on that cohort.
- **For roofs ≥15 years old, steep-slope:** Homeowner can engage an authorized inspector. If the inspector certifies **≥5 years of useful life**, the carrier cannot deny on roof-age grounds.
- **For roofs ≥15 years old, low-slope:** Same test, with the additional pathway that an inspector can certify a **coating system** that extends useful life by ≥5 years — which keeps the roof insurable without a tear-off.

Where this leaves you depends on your shop:

**Roofers** — the pure tear-off-on-age sales pitch loses force on July 1. The replacement decision is now litigated through an inspector report, and you should be either (a) the inspector, (b) the contractor the inspector refers, or (c) the contractor with a turnkey "inspect-then-repair-or-replace" package. Pick one this week and price it.

**Authorized inspectors** — the certified-useful-life letter is your new flagship deliverable. It is tied directly to a carrier-acceptance decision, which means it is high-leverage and the homeowner will pay for it. Set your turnaround standard and your fee schedule before the July rush.

**Coatings applicators (low-slope / flat-roof restoration)** — your sales motion has a new opening line: "we can extend the useful life of this roof by five years and keep it insurable without a tear-off." That is now codified in state law as a recognized pathway. Update your one-pager.

**Restoration / water-mit** — the relevance is downstream. A roof that stays insurable under HB 815 still leaks under the right storm. Your post-Ian post-Milton call-volume math holds. But pay attention to the second July 1 bill below; it changes how you talk about PAs.

## The PA-referral motion changes the same day — SB 266

**Senate Bill 266** also takes effect **July 1, 2026**. The DFS now has explicit authority to fine **up to $5,000 per act** for soliciting vulnerable adults (adults who lack capacity to consent, adults under guardianship, certain elderly homeowners as defined in Florida Statute §415.102), and contracts signed by vulnerable adults can be rescinded at any time without penalty.

You are not the licensee on the hook for the fine. The PA is. But your **referral pattern** can read as steering if it routes a single PA repeatedly to vulnerable households. Clean protocol for the field crew:

1. When you suspect a vulnerable adult is involved, do not hand the homeowner a single PA card.
2. Hand them a written list of **three licensed Florida PAs**. Let them choose.
3. Document that you provided the list, not a recommendation.

This protects your business. It also protects the homeowner from being routed to a PA who will be the one paying a $5,000 fine.

Source: [Florida Senate — SB 266 (2026)](https://flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2026/266)

## The CILB renewal clock — 12 weeks to August 31

The CILB **certified-license biennial renewal closes August 31, 2026**. The CE requirement is **14 hours**: 1 hour each of workplace safety, workers' comp, and business practices, plus 11 hours of trade-specific or general construction.

If you have not started, you have roughly 12 weeks. Three CE blocks of one hour and an 11-hour weekend block clears it. Do not wait until late August — the CE seat-availability tightens in the final 30 days, and a missed deadline puts you on inactive status during peak season.

Source: [MyFloridaLicense — Construction Industry Hot Topics](https://www2.myfloridalicense.com/construction-industry/hot-topics/)
