## The roof-age underwriting fix is dead — older-roof listings keep their insurance problem

Florida Senate records confirm that **HB 815 — the roof-age reform bill — died in the Insurance & Banking Subcommittee on March 13** and will not take effect July 1. The bill would have prohibited carriers from refusing to issue or renew a policy solely because of roof age and given owners a certified-inspection pathway before any forced replacement.

**What this changes at the closing table: nothing — and that's the story.** If you've been telling buyers (or hearing from lenders) that the older-roof insurability problem eases this summer, correct the record now. Through at least the 2027 session:

- A 15-to-20-year-old roof remains a legitimate underwriting obstacle. Carriers may decline, surcharge, or demand replacement as a condition of binding.
- The practical workaround is unchanged: get the **roof condition report and wind-mitigation inspection** into the listing package *before* going active. A documented 5+ years of remaining useful life converts "uninsurable" conversations into "rated" conversations.
- For buyers, the insurance contingency timeline matters more on older-roof homes — a declined binder ten days before closing is still a live failure mode.

The companion public-adjuster contract bill (SB 266) died the same day, after a unanimous first-committee vote — expect both back next session.

Sources: [HB 815, Florida Senate](https://flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2026/815) · [SB 266](https://flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2026/266)

## Property-tax amendment: the buyer math just got real — and contested

The homestead-exemption overhaul is now concrete enough to model into buyer conversations. The version passed by lawmakers last week raises the exemption from **$50,000 to $150,000 in 2027** and up to **$250,000 in 2028** (inflation-indexed after that), with school levies carved out. Per the Governor's office, property taxes would be **fully eliminated on about 60% of Florida homes** at the $250,000 level. It requires **60% voter approval in November 2026**.

Two developments this week complicate the picture:

1. **S&P Global Ratings warned (June 12)** that Florida local governments — it rates 22 counties, 67 municipalities, and 22 school districts — face "greater credit pressure" and possibly "material" revenue challenges, with no replacement revenue identified. Localities are floating new service fees; Florida issuers carry **$175+ billion** in municipal debt.
2. **The state was sued (June 11)** over the amendment's ballot language, which plaintiffs call "biased and misleading."

**How to use it this week:** for a homesteaded primary-residence buyer, the amendment is a meaningful carrying-cost upside scenario for 2027–2028 — worth mentioning, *clearly labeled as contingent on a 60% vote*. Do not bake it into affordability math. For investors and second-home buyers: the exemption is homestead-only — non-homesteaded property gets nothing and could plausibly face new local fees instead.

Sources: [Insurance Journal / S&P](https://www.insurancejournal.com/news/southeast/2026/06/12/873577.htm) · [The Real Deal / lawsuit](https://therealdeal.com/miami/2026/06/11/florida-sued-over-property-tax-exemption-ballot-language/)

## Season's first disturbance — calibrate your closing timelines

The National Hurricane Center is tracking the first Atlantic-basin system of 2026: a Bay of Campeche low with a **10% formation chance** and no Florida threat. The transaction-relevant reminder: once a named storm approaches Florida, **carriers suspend new binders** until the threat passes. Any closing scheduled inside hurricane season should have its insurance bound *early*, not the week of closing — a binder suspension with a storm on the map is an avoidable way to blow a closing date. NOAA still forecasts a below-normal season.

Source: [NHC Atlantic outlook](https://www.nhc.noaa.gov/gtwo.php?basin=atlc&fdays=7)
