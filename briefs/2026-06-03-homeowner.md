## A Citizens policy with no flood proof is a non-renewal waiting to happen

Here is the renewal-letter story most South Florida homeowners are not hearing about yet, even though it is already in effect.

If your homeowners insurance is with **Citizens Property Insurance Corporation**, and your **dwelling coverage (Coverage A) is $400,000 or more**, you are now required to carry flood insurance. The rule kicked in **January 1, 2026**. It applies regardless of which FEMA flood zone you are in — even Zone X, which most of inland Wellington, Boca, Coral Springs, and Pinecrest sits in.

If you can't show Citizens proof of an active flood policy (either National Flood Insurance Program or an approved private carrier) by your next renewal date, Citizens can — and will — non-renew the underlying homeowners policy. That's not a rate hike. That's losing the policy entirely.

A few things to know right now:

- **The number to look at is Coverage A on your declarations page.** Not the market value of your home. Not the tax-assessed value. Coverage A — the dwelling line. Most non-condo South Florida homes west of I-95 cross the $400K Coverage A threshold once you factor in current rebuild costs.
- **The rule expands again on January 1, 2027** — at which point *every* single-family Citizens policy needs flood, regardless of dwelling value.
- **You can satisfy the requirement two ways.** NFIP (the federal program) or an approved private flood carrier. Florida is now the **largest private flood market in the country** — about 35% of flood policies in the state are private, covering 600,000+ properties.

### What to do this week

1. **Pull out your Citizens declarations page.** Find the Coverage A number. If it's $400,000 or more and your next renewal is within 90 days, you need flood proof on file.
2. **Get quotes from BOTH NFIP and a private flood carrier.** Private flood typically runs **10–30% less than NFIP** for newer construction and homes elevated above base flood elevation. NFIP is often the safer bet for older homes or homes with prior flood claims. Don't accept the first number you see.
3. **Ask whether your Citizens renewal date already passed.** If you got a non-renewal notice that referenced flood, you have appeal options — but the clock starts ticking the day the letter goes out.

## A second story worth knowing about: the building code resets December 31

If you are considering a roof replacement, an addition, or a major remodel in the next year, the calendar matters.

The **Florida Building Code 9th Edition** takes effect **December 31, 2026** — about 211 days from today. Permits pulled *before* that date are generally locked into the current rules through completion. Permits pulled *after* are under the new code.

What changes? The 9th Edition is expected to refine wind-load tables, sealed-roof-deck requirements, and energy provisions. For homes in Miami-Dade or Broward — the only two counties in the state's High-Velocity Hurricane Zone — there's an additional wrinkle: when a renovation crosses the **30% threshold of the building envelope**, the entire envelope can be pulled into current code. A new roof can end up requiring upgraded windows, doors, and attachments to match.

### What to do

- **If a roof or major project is on your list for 2027**, get the permit application moving this summer. Locking in 8th Edition rules before December 31 is real money on most jobs.
- **Ask your contractor for a written code-effective-date plan.** If materials are being ordered now for a job that won't ship until January, parts of the assembly may need to be re-specced.
- **For Miami-Dade and Broward homeowners**, the HVHZ 30% rule is the one to flag. Any significant project there needs a careful scope conversation.

## A bill that would change how disputes get resolved

Worth a heads-up because it could change how you handle a claim that doesn't go smoothly:

**House Bill 459**, currently in the 2026 Florida legislative session, would create a mandatory administrative track for disputed property insurance claims. Instead of going straight to civil court, disputed claims would be routed through the Division of Administrative Hearings — coverage decision within 60 days, dollar figure within 180 days.

The bill is **proposed, not law**. Trial lawyers oppose it; insurance carriers largely support it. The concern from policyholder advocates is that the bill narrows access to civil court. The pitch from supporters is that it gets claims resolved faster and cheaper.

If you have a current dispute with your carrier, nothing changes right now. But if HB 459 passes in some form, the playbook for resolving claim disagreements gets very different — and worth understanding before signing anything that mentions DOAH or "administrative resolution."

[Florida Senate — HB 459 (2026)](https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2026/459)
