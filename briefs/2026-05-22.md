## TOP STORIES

### NOAA's 2026 Atlantic Outlook is Officially Below-Normal — 8–14 Named Storms, 3–6 Hurricanes, 1–3 Majors
The National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration's Climate Prediction Center released its 2026 Atlantic Hurricane Season outlook yesterday in Lakeland. The official ranges:

| Category | 2026 Range | 30-year average |
|---|---|---|
| Named storms | 8–14 | 14.4 |
| Hurricanes | 3–6 | 7.2 |
| Major hurricanes (Cat 3+) | 1–3 | 3.2 |

The probability of a **below-normal** season is **55%**, near-normal 35%, above-normal 10%. The dominant driver is a developing — and possibly "super" — **El Niño** by late summer, which historically raises tropical Atlantic wind shear and suppresses storm organization. This is the lowest named-storm range issued for an Atlantic season since 2014–2015.

**What it means for homeowners:** "Below-normal" is a *probabilistic* call, not a landfall forecast. The forecasters were explicit about that. 1992 was officially a "below-normal" season — and it produced Hurricane Andrew. A quieter overall count does not lower the risk on any individual home. Treat preparation, documentation, and insurance review the same as you would in any other year.

**What it means for real estate professionals:** Inland buyers asking "is it a bad year to close?" should be told the truth: probabilities are favorable for the overall season, but a single landfall changes everything. Don't lean on the forecast as a closing argument.

**What it means for service providers:** A quieter forecast historically pulls forward margin pressure as homeowners delay non-emergency work. The window to sell **June–July off-season inspection and maintenance** is open now, before the first named storm sets expectations for the rest of the season.

### Three More Homeowners Insurance Carriers Enter Florida — Market Count Now ~20 Post-Reform, $850M Fresh Capital
A new industry report covered by Insurance Journal yesterday confirms **three additional homeowners insurance carriers** have entered the Florida market this spring, bringing the post-reform total to **20 new entrants**. Combined fresh capital across the new carriers exceeds **$850 million**. Florida-domiciled personal property insurers have swung from a **$132 million underwriting loss two years ago** to nearly **$1 billion in underwriting gains** in the most recent reporting period.

**What it means for homeowners:** Capacity is no longer the binding constraint. New carriers tend to write conservatively in the coastal high-wind zones of Miami-Dade and Broward, and more aggressively in lower-risk inland zips. For Wellington and inland Palm Beach County, shopping a renewal this year is more likely to surface a meaningful price difference than it has been since 2022. For coastal Martin and St. Lucie, the place to read carefully is **wind-only sublimits** — that's where new entrants will try to thread the needle.

**What it means for real estate professionals:** "Insurance not available" objections that killed deals in 2023 are now mostly outdated. Buyers in non-coastal areas should be encouraged to request quotes from at least three of the new carriers before closing — many will come in below the legacy incumbent.

Source: https://www.insurancejournal.com/news/southeast/2026/05/21/870846.htm

### Florida's Condo Insurance Market is Healing — Highest Writer Count in 15 Years; April Sales Up 7% Year-Over-Year
The same report flags the **condo association** segment is recovering: the number of carriers writing condo policies is at a **15-year high**, and statewide condominium sales in April 2026 rose **7%** over April 2025. This is the first credible "thaw" data point since the post-Surfside disclosure crunch and HB 1021 milestone-inspection requirements.

**What it means for homeowners (condo unit owners):** Associations with completed milestone inspections and clean structural reports now have more carrier options at the master-policy renewal. Unit owners should ask their board for the master policy declarations page and their building's most recent milestone inspection summary — the two documents together determine whether your personal HO-6 policy will price well or get non-renewed.

**What it means for real estate professionals:** Condo listings that have been sitting due to financing-tied insurance issues are about to get easier to move. The buyer's broker should be asking the listing side for the milestone inspection status before showing — it's the new gating document.

Source: https://www.insurancejournal.com/news/southeast/2026/05/21/870846.htm

## INSURANCE & REGULATION

### 30-Day Florida Homeowners Insurance Rate Filing Average is Now NEGATIVE 2.3%
The 30-day moving average of homeowners insurance filings with Florida's Office of Insurance Regulation is **−2.3%**, versus **+0.5%** one year ago. Since January 2024, **39 carriers have filed for decreases** and **48 have filed flat or 0% changes** — a structural inflection, not a one-off.

**What it means for homeowners:** Between now and September 1, the default expectation for a renewal should be "**flat or lower**, not up." A non-renewal letter or a material rate increase is now an outlier — and an outlier is a signal worth reviewing. Ask the carrier for the underlying inspection or scoring data they used to make the decision; you have a right to it.

**What it means for real estate professionals:** Quoted premiums on listings from six months ago are likely already stale on the high side. Re-quote before sharing premium estimates with buyers.

Source: Florida OIR rate filings summary — https://irfssearch.floir.gov/

## CONTRACTORS & SERVICE INDUSTRIES

### 2026 Florida Building Code High-Wind Zone Updates — Miami-Dade Roofing Standards Now Performance-Based
**Miami-Dade County has updated its Roofing Application Standards 118, 119, 120, and 127, along with related testing protocols**, to remove many prescriptive methods and insert **performance-based standards**, including expanded structured tables for required underlayment uplift resistance.

**What it means for homeowners (Miami-Dade and Broward):** A "passed inspection 5+ years ago" wind mitigation form is increasingly a paper credential, not a current compliance statement. This is the second wind-mitigation revisit signal in two weeks. If your last wind-mitigation inspection is from before 2023, request a fresh one before renewal — the discount may be larger, and the documentation will hold up at claim time.

**What it means for service providers (roofers):** Roof systems that passed prior code cycles may **no longer comply** under the new performance-based reads, especially older tile underlayments. The updated tables also open legitimate scope expansion on re-roofs — but only with current Miami-Dade Notice of Acceptance documentation in hand. Update your standard estimating templates now.

Source: https://www.floridaroof.com/2026-Tile-Code-Update
