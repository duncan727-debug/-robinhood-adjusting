## Condo Listings: SIRS Is Now the #1 Deal-Killer in Coastal Submarkets

The Florida Structural Integrity Reserve Study (SIRS) outer-pairing deadline is **December 31, 2026** — **210 days away**. The hard-cost reality has settled in over the last 12 months: per industry analyses of completed SIRS, **special assessments are landing in the $134,000 to $400,000 per unit range** on older mid- and high-rises that deferred concrete and envelope work.

For listing agents and buyer's agents in coastal Miami-Dade, Broward, and PBC, this is now the most common reason a deal collapses between contract and closing on pre-1996 condo stock.

**Add this to your standard listing intake checklist for any 3+ story condo:**
1. **Milestone inspection status.** Has the building's milestone inspection been completed? Date of completion? Outstanding scope?
2. **SIRS completion date.** Most boards now have draft SIRS reports — even if not finalized.
3. **Reserve adequacy vs. SIRS recommendation.** The gap between current reserves and the SIRS-recommended reserve number is the assessment-risk number. A board that has the gap funded is a green light. A board that has not voted on it yet is yellow. A board with a vote pending in the next 90 days is red.
4. **Special-assessment history.** Last 36 months of approved assessments and any pending.
5. **Last two board meeting minutes.** Look for the words "structural," "concrete," "spalling," "engineer's letter," or "Phase 2." Each is a flag.

**Disclosure conversation script — listing side:**
> *"Before we list, I want to walk through the building's milestone and SIRS status with you. This is the #1 thing a buyer's agent will ask for, and it's the disclosure most likely to get challenged at the inspection period. If we don't have a clean answer, I'd rather we surface it in the listing notes than discover it on day 14 of contract."*

**Disclosure conversation script — buy side:**
> *"Before we go under contract, I need the building's milestone inspection report, the current SIRS draft or final, the last 36 months of board meeting minutes, and the reserve study. If the seller's agent can't produce all four, that's part of our inspection contingency."*

## The Hazard-Insurance Binder Just Got More Complicated

You should already be reading the buyer's hazard-insurance binder before closing — every conforming Fannie / Freddie loan requires it. What changed: in late December 2025, three U.S. Senators opened a probe into **Demotech's** rating practices for Florida property insurers, citing research that **nearly 20% of Demotech-rated Florida insurers became insolvent between 2009 and 2022 while holding an "A" rating** — roughly 30 times the failure rate of insurers rated by other agencies. The Senators are asking why the GSEs continue to accept Demotech ratings as proof of financial strength.

For your closing this month, the binder review now includes one additional question: **what is the carrier's current Demotech tier, and is the lender currently accepting that tier under its hazard-insurance requirements?** Most lenders still are. But the question is no longer paranoid — it is part of due diligence.

**Practical move:** add a one-line item to your buyer's insurance-binder checklist: *"Confirm carrier rating with lender's hazard-insurance team within first 7 days of contract — flag if Demotech tier is below current GSE acceptance threshold."*

## Public-Adjuster Contract Reform — Why This Matters to Buyers and Sellers With Open Claims

**Senate Bill 266** carries an effective date of **July 1, 2026** per its filed text. The bill amends Florida Statute §626.854 and substantially rewrites the contracting framework for public adjusters:

- **Vulnerable-adult open-ended rescission.** A vulnerable adult or one lacking capacity (or their legal representative) may rescind a PA contract **at any time**, without penalty.
- **18-point font disclosures** required on the fee disclosure section.
- **New disciplinary ground** for soliciting or taking advantage of vulnerable adults.

**Why this lands at the closing table:** in any transaction where the seller has an **open property-insurance claim** — common after a storm-loss year, common on older roofs being replaced under claim — the PA contract is part of the claim's economic structure. If the seller is a senior or has a legal representative on file, the new rescission right could change the assignment math at closing. Buyer's-agent practical question to add to the disclosure conversation when an open claim exists:

> *"Is there a public-adjuster contract attached to this claim? If yes, who signed it, when, and under what fee structure? After July 1 there are additional cancellation pathways available to certain insureds."*

The bill's signing status was not yet in the public record at the time of this brief; the July 1 effective date holds if signed.

## The Hurricane Window — Quiet, But Use It

National Hurricane Center shows **no Atlantic tropical formation expected through at least June 11**, per the most recent 7-day outlook. NOAA's 2026 seasonal outlook calls for a below-normal season (55% probability of below-normal, 35% near-normal, 10% above-normal; 8-14 named storms, 3-6 hurricanes, 1-3 major). El Niño is developing.

For your active listings, the calm window is the right time to:
- Confirm carriers and binders on every active contract
- Get any deferred SIRS / milestone documentation ordered now, not in August
- Talk to sellers about **HB 815** (roof-age reform, effective July 1) — the certified-inspector path can salvage some older asphalt roofs that have been pricing themselves out of buyer financing contingencies
