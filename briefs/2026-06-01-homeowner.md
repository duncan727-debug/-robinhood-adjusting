## The renewal-letter math just changed

Two carriers cut rates this week, and the cut on the larger one — **Citizens** — is effective **today**.

If your homeowners policy is with Citizens, the rate filing approved earlier this year takes effect on June 1 renewals going forward. The statewide average cut is 2.6%, but the distribution is uneven and almost certainly more friendly to you than that number suggests:

- **Roughly 3 out of every 5 Citizens customers** see an average premium reduction of about **11.5%, or $359**.
- A smaller group in the highest-risk coastal geographies see a flat or slightly higher renewal.
- This is the **first Citizens rate decrease since 2015**. Eleven years.

The second cut: **Heritage Property & Casualty** — one of the larger private Florida homeowners carriers — announced a rate reduction of **up to 5%** for **tri-county policyholders (Miami-Dade, Broward, Palm Beach)**. Separately, the Governor's office has highlighted that roughly **26,000 PBC homes** are seeing an **average reduction of about 11.9%** across the broader market-relief package — that figure spans multiple carriers, not Heritage alone. If you have Heritage and you live in PBC, your renewal envelope should look meaningfully better than last year's.

**What to do this week:**
1. Pull last summer's declarations page. Put it next to the renewal that arrives this month. Look for two things: the *premium* line and the *roof age* line — both are about to matter.
2. If you've been quoted out of Citizens by a takeout offer in the past 90 days, run the math again with today's new Citizens number. The spread narrows.
3. If you're on a non-Citizens carrier and your renewal lands flat or up, ask an independent agent to quote against the **three new carriers approved in May**. There are now 20 new entrants in the Florida market and ~$850 million in fresh capital — your agent has more paper to shop than a year ago.

## July 1 is the day the roof-age trap closes

For 30 days, you are still living under the old rules. On **July 1, 2026**, **House Bill 815** takes effect, and three things change at once for the roof on your house:

- **The 15-year floor.** No insurer can refuse to issue or renew your policy solely because your roof is *less than 15 years old*, regardless of material. If a renewal letter cites "roof age" on a 12-year-old shingle roof, that letter is no longer enforceable after July 1.
- **The inspector pathway for older roofs.** If your roof is 15+ years old, the carrier must let you bring in an **authorized inspector** to certify the roof's condition before they can mandate replacement. The inspector's findings become the new pricing surface.
- **The 5-year useful-life test.** For a steep-slope roof (most pitched shingle and metal roofs in South Florida), if the inspector certifies **5 years of useful life remaining**, the carrier cannot deny you on roof age. For a low-slope roof (most flat or near-flat residential), the same test applies if a **coating system** can extend useful life by 5 years.

The bill applies to policies **issued or renewed on or after July 1, 2026**. If your renewal lands in June, it falls under the old rules. If your renewal lands in July, the new rules apply.

**What to do this week if your roof is 15+:** Don't pay for a full replacement out of pocket between now and July 1 without first getting an authorized inspector to evaluate. Booking that inspection now — before the rush — costs less than booking it in July.

## The Citizens flood mandate is still coming on January 1

A related deadline that has not moved: starting **January 1, 2027**, Citizens will require **all wind policies** — every dollar of home value — to carry flood insurance. If you are with Citizens and you are not already in NFIP or a private flood policy, you have seven months to add it.

Note also that the **federal NFIP authorization expires September 30, 2026**, and Congress is in the early stages of reauthorizing it. A federally appointed council has floated structural changes that could move flood policies toward the private market over time. None of that affects your policy this summer — but if you're closing on a flood-zone home in Q4 2026, the financing-contingency conversation now includes the reauthorization question.

## Hurricane prep — the un-glamorous version

The Atlantic basin is clean. NHC shows **zero formations expected** for the next 7 days. NOAA's seasonal outlook calls for **8–14 named storms, 3–6 hurricanes, 1–3 major hurricanes** — below-normal counts driven by a developing El Niño.

Below-normal is a count statement, not a Florida-landfall statement. The last below-normal year that came through the Florida peninsula still came through the Florida peninsula.

The three highest-leverage moves for this week, before the cone-watch cycle starts:

1. **Photograph the house.** Every room, every elevation, every serial number on a major appliance. Push the album to cloud. The album you don't take is the album you wish you had at the claim stage.
2. **Book a 30-minute roof inspection** with a contractor you would want to be #3 on the call list if a storm hits. Their slot today is open; their slot in September isn't.
3. **Pull the policy out of the drawer.** Read the deductible page. Read the wind-vs-flood split. Note whether your contents limit reflects what's actually in your house in 2026.

The 2025 tax-relief package made most hurricane-prep supplies — batteries, tarps, generators, anchor kits, smoke detectors, gas cans — **permanently sales-tax exempt year-round**. There is no two-week shopping window to plan around. The savings are baked in to today's price.
