What Is a Public Adjuster, and Why Does It Matter?

The difference between a carrier adjuster and a public adjuster is not technical. It is who they represent.

A public adjuster represents the policyholder. The insurance company's adjuster represents the insurance company. Both may inspect the same loss, but their obligations, incentives, and role in the claim are different.

What a public adjuster does

A public adjuster helps prepare, document, present, and negotiate a property insurance claim on behalf of the policyholder. That can include inspecting the damage, reviewing the policy, estimating the scope of covered repairs, organizing evidence, responding to carrier questions, and negotiating the claim value.

The practical value is simple: your claim is not just about what happened to the property. It is about what can be proven under the policy.

How a public adjuster differs from the carrier's adjuster

  • Carrier adjuster: assigned by the insurance company to evaluate the claim for the carrier.
  • Independent adjuster: often hired by the carrier as an outside vendor, but still working on the carrier's assignment.
  • Public adjuster: hired by the policyholder to represent the policyholder's interest.

That distinction matters most when the claim involves hidden damage, disputed scope, policy exclusions, depreciation, code upgrades, or a low initial estimate.

When it is worth talking to one

  • The damage is significant or affects multiple areas of the property.
  • The insurer's estimate seems too low to restore the property properly.
  • The claim was denied or partially denied.
  • You are dealing with roof, water, wind, mold-related, or business interruption issues.
  • You do not understand the policy language or the carrier's coverage position.

When you may not need one

Not every claim needs a public adjuster. If the loss is small, coverage is clear, and the carrier's estimate is fair, you may be able to handle it yourself. A good public adjuster should be willing to say that.
