Why Contractors Should Never Negotiate Insurance Scope Directly

When your estimate conflicts with the carrier's estimate, the smartest move is to keep the roles clean.

Contractors should build and repair. Public adjusters should handle claim presentation and negotiation. That division protects the homeowner, the contractor, and the claim.

The problem with arguing scope yourself

Contractors often spot under-scoped carrier estimates immediately. Missing line items, wrong materials, code issues, access problems, and unrealistic labor assumptions are common. But when the contractor becomes the person arguing claim value, the conversation can get messy fast.

  • The carrier may treat the contractor as financially biased.
  • The homeowner can get caught between competing estimates.
  • The contractor risks spending unpaid time on insurance arguments instead of paid work.
  • The relationship can sour if the customer thinks the contractor is driving the claim rather than solving the repair problem.

Where a public adjuster fits

A licensed public adjuster can review the carrier estimate, inspect the loss, prepare the policyholder's claim position, and negotiate with the insurer. The contractor still provides real repair expertise, photos, measurements, and pricing, but the PA handles the insurance claim lane.

What contractors should do instead

  • Document what you see with clear photos, measurements, and notes.
  • Write a repair estimate based on the work actually required.
  • Flag obvious scope gaps to the homeowner in plain language.
  • Recommend that the homeowner speak with a licensed public adjuster if the carrier estimate is incomplete, denied, delayed, or far below the repair scope.

Why this helps the homeowner

The homeowner gets a cleaner process. The contractor stays focused on the repair. The public adjuster organizes the claim argument under the policy. Everyone stays in the lane where they add the most value.
