Lead Story

26 Days to Fill Your Pre-Season Pipeline — The Most Efficient Window of the Year

Hurricane Season opens June 1. The stretch between now and May 31 is historically the most productive period for insurance-adjacent mitigation work: homeowners are motivated, inspection contingencies from spring real estate transactions are closing, and carrier scrutiny on repair scopes hasn't spiked yet. This is the window where the most work gets scheduled, started, and completed before demand turns reactive.

Current conditions favor scheduling. Highs are in the mid-to-upper 90s with minimal rain forecast through mid-May — ideal for roofing, exterior work, impact window installation, and any other outdoor scope. Afternoon storms begin arriving daily once summer sets in. If your crews aren't booked through late May, you're leaving the easiest part of the year unaddressed.

Colorado State's 2026 forecast projects a below-average season — 13 named storms vs. the 19-storm average — but Florida-specific probability remains significant: 74% chance a named storm passes within 50 miles of the state. Below-average means fewer storms, not no storms. One activated event is enough to flip a quiet May backlog into a six-month emergency queue.

Workforce

90% of Roofers Cite Labor as #1 Constraint — and It's Not Getting Better Before June

Industry data from the Florida Roof Association and Roofing Contractor Magazine confirms what most shop owners already know: nine out of ten Florida roofing contractors identify finding skilled labor as their single biggest business problem heading into the 2026 season. This isn't a regional anomaly — it's a structural condition that's been compressing capacity for three consecutive years.

The firms pulling ahead aren't solving this with more job postings. They're treating it as a systems problem:

  • Structured training paths that retain crew members rather than cycling through new hires
  • AI-assisted scheduling tools that prevent crew overbooking and surface fatigue-risk alerts before productivity drops
  • Wage competitiveness built into every contract — not offered reactively when workers threaten to leave
  • Apprenticeship pipeline investment to build 12–18 month lead time on skilled capacity

The offseason — right now, before June 1 — is the last low-pressure window to make structural changes. Once storm demand surges, there's no time for systems upgrades. If you're still running on reactive hiring when the first named storm activates, you'll be watching jobs go to whoever has crews, not whoever does the best work.
