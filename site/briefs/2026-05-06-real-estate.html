Lead Story

Miami + Broward Realtor Merger Takes Effect May 11 — What Changes for Agents

In five days, the Miami Association of Realtors and Broward, Palm Beaches & St. Lucie Realtors officially merge into Miami and South Florida REALTORS® — the largest local Realtor association in the world with 93,000 members. MLS systems, transaction reporting, marketing infrastructure, and member services consolidate across Palm Beach, Broward, Miami-Dade, St. Lucie, and surrounding counties into a single operating platform (subject to NAR approval, with full integration expected by year-end).

The practical implications for your business: cross-county listing data becomes unified, which simplifies pricing comparables for agents working multi-county territory. Transaction reporting standardizes, which should reduce the administrative friction on deals that cross county lines. For market intelligence purposes — particularly when analyzing insurance-sensitive inventory — consolidated data produces cleaner signals on pricing trends, days on market, and inventory by price tier.

If your systems, workflows, or MLS subscriptions need updates before May 11, now is the time. The merger does not change your existing client relationships or pending contracts, but the backend data environment will shift.

Market Alert

Martin County: Strong Demand + Volatile Insurance = Deals at Risk

Martin County's transaction volume is surging. Single-family pending sales jumped 35.3% year-over-year in March 2026. But the insurance picture directly contradicts the statewide rate relief narrative — Martin County is projected to see the highest homeowner premium increases in Florida for 2026, running counter to the declining rates seen in Miami-Dade, Broward, and Palm Beach.

This divergence creates a specific risk in Martin County transactions: buyers arriving with assumptions about Florida's improving insurance market may get a county-specific surprise when they shop for coverage. If premium affordability is baked into their qualification math — and for most buyers it is — a Martin County insurance quote that comes in significantly higher than expected can stall or kill a deal that looked clean on paper.

Regulatory

Legislature Pausing Major Insurance Reform — Use This as a Buyer Confidence Signal

Florida Senate President Ben Albritton has confirmed: no major property insurance overhaul is expected during the 2026 legislative session. The 2022–2023 reform package is producing measurable results — litigation is down 35% since 2021, reinsurance costs are normalizing, and carriers that exited the Florida market are beginning to return. The Legislature is choosing stability over further intervention.

Two narrow measures are still moving: HB 815 (prohibits non-renewal solely on roof age without physical inspection) and SB 1452 (expands My Safe Florida Home wind mitigation grant eligibility to more residential categories). Both benefit property owners and create mitigation incentives — neither represents destabilizing change.

For transactions: buyers who remain hesitant about Florida's insurance environment based on 2022–2024 headlines are working from outdated data. The rate relief is real, the legislative environment is stable, and carriers are adding capacity. This is a concrete, factual counter-narrative you can deploy with confidence.

Hurricane Season in 26 Days — The Closing Timeline Conversation

Any transaction scheduled to close in June or July should have the insurance conversation now. Carrier binding windows tighten when named storms are active — new policy applications face delays or holds mid-season. Deals closing before June 1 have the most flexibility. Colorado State projects 74% probability that a named storm passes within 50 miles of Florida this season. Below-average forecast ≠ no risk. Start the conversation early.
