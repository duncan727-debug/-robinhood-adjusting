TOP STORIES

Drought Severity Escalates to Worst Level in 15 Years; Wildfire Risk Remains Critical

Florida is experiencing its most severe drought in nearly 15 years, with 98% of the state now experiencing conditions worse than moderate drought. This marks a critical escalation from the "extreme drought" reported just days ago. The combination of sustained low humidity (20-30%), elevated temperatures, and near-zero precipitation has created unprecedented wildfire risk across the region.

Current Wildfire Status (May 14):

  • Max Road Fire (Broward/Everglades): 11,339 acres burned, 70% contained (mop-up operations ongoing)
  • 172nd Avenue Fire (Miami-Dade/Florida City): 300 acres burned, 50% contained
  • Statewide fires: 2,000+ wildfires reported in 2026 year-to-date
Why it matters: The 15-year worst-drought designation signals this is not a typical seasonal dry period—it's structural. Property owners in Broward, Miami-Dade, and western Palm Beach should expect continued evacuation risk through early June. Insurance carriers may issue additional coverage alerts or temporary deductible restrictions on fire-exposed properties. Mold remediation contractors should maintain rapid-response capacity; heavy rain post-fire suppression will trigger 24-48 hour mold mitigation windows.

Actionable for service providers: Wildfire underinsurance is now endemic—most standard policies do not adequately cover wildfire exposure. Public adjusters should conduct proactive wildfire-risk assessments for property owners in high-fire-risk zones (western Broward, south Miami-Dade) and advise on coverage gaps and additional endorsements.

National Hurricane Center Begins Regular Tropical Weather Outlooks Tomorrow (May 15); 18-Day Official Season Countdown

The National Hurricane Center will formally begin issuing regular Tropical Weather Outlooks on May 15, 2026—marking the shift from pre-season readiness to active seasonal monitoring. Official hurricane season begins June 1 (18 days from now). This shift signals the end of the final pre-season window for property inspections, wind mitigation, and flood insurance verification.

Critical Pre-Season Actions (Next 18 Days):

  • Finalize roof and wind-mitigation inspections
  • Complete flood insurance enrollment (moratoriums begin once the NHC names a tropical storm)
  • Verify property coverage limits and exclusions
  • Update emergency supplies and evacuation plans
  • Schedule final contractor consultations and repairs
Why it matters: The transition to NHC outlooks will trigger a surge in property owner inquiries. Service providers should expect peak demand for:
  • Rapid-turnaround roof inspections and assessments
  • Wind mitigation consulting and retrofit work
  • Flood insurance gap analysis and recommendations
  • Loss-control advisory services (multi-hazard property reviews)

Property owners who delay until June 1 will face capacity constraints and rushed turnarounds. Public adjusters should launch pre-season advisory outreach NOW—positioning as loss-control consultants rather than claim handlers.

CONTRACTORS & SERVICE INDUSTRIES

2026 Contractor Market Outlook: Workforce Challenges, Roofing & HVAC Lead Growth Demand

Florida's contractor market continues to expand on strong population inflow and major infrastructure investment, but labor constraints are emerging as the limiting factor for growth.

2026 Market Fundamentals:

  • Top 50 Florida contractors: $10.38B combined revenue in 2025; HVAC, roofing, electrical, and general contracting lead growth sectors
  • Strongest demand sectors: Roofing (wind mitigation, pre-season inspections), HVAC (installation and emergency service), electrical, specialized MEP trades
  • Labor shortage: Contractors report difficulty finding installers and licensed technicians; shortage expected to widen through 2026
  • Market pricing: More predictable in 2026 vs. 2024-2025; roofing consolidation continues with larger firms acquiring regional players

Regulatory Context (Effective July 1):

  • SB 808 expands roofing contractor scope: Roof-to-wall connection evaluation and enhancement now licensed contractor work
  • Roofing inspector qualifications broaden: home inspectors, building code inspectors, general contractors, roofing contractors now authorized
Why it matters: The labor shortage creates capacity constraints and justifies premium service fees and expedited rates. Contractors who emphasize training and employee retention (like Advanced Roofing Inc., now 100% employee-owned) will outcompete those relying on day-labor pools. Public adjusters should understand these constraints when recommending repairs and managing contractor timelines—18-day pre-season window will expose capacity limits.

Opportunity for Duncan: The 93,000-member Realtor network (post-merger) represents primary referral channel for contractor recommendations and joint loss-control consulting opportunities. Positioning as a "relationship connector" between adjusters, Realtors, and contractors differentiates service.
