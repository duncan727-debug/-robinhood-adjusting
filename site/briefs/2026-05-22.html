NOAA's 2026 Atlantic Outlook is Officially Below-Normal — 8–14 Named Storms, 3–6 Hurricanes, 1–3 Majors

[Headline number] NOAA's Climate Prediction Center released its 2026 Atlantic Hurricane Season outlook yesterday (May 21) in Lakeland. The official ranges:

Category 2026 Range 30-year average
Named storms 8–14 14.4
Hurricanes 3–6 7.2
Major hurricanes (Cat 3+) 1–3 3.2

NOAA puts the probability of a below-normal season at 55%, near-normal at 35%, and above-normal at 10%. The dominant driver is a developing — and possibly "super" — El Niño by late summer, which historically raises tropical Atlantic wind shear and suppresses storm organization.

What's actually new vs. yesterday's preview: The May 21 brief flagged the outlook drop; today we have the numbers. The headline shift from the May 14–18 forecaster split is that the official call has landed on the lower side of the range, not the middle. This is the lowest-named-storm range NOAA has issued for an Atlantic season since 2014–2015.

What it means for Duncan & PA practice: "Below-normal" is a probabilistic call, not a landfall forecast — NOAA was explicit about that yesterday. The on-the-ground messaging risk is real: homeowners will under-prepare. The 2018 ("near-normal") and 1992 ("below-normal," produced Andrew) seasons are the standard cautionary tales. Lead client conversations with "lower count, same risk per storm." For service providers (roofers, water mitigation, A/C), a quieter season historically pushes margin pressure and tighter inspection/maintenance volume — position the June–July off-season inspection offer now while pipelines are open.

Three More HO Carriers Enter Florida — Market Count Now ~20 Post-Reform, $850M Fresh Capital

A new industry report covered by Insurance Journal yesterday confirms three additional homeowners insurance carriers have entered the Florida market this spring, bringing the post-2022/2023 reform total to 20 new entrants. Combined fresh capital across the new carriers exceeds $850 million. Florida-domiciled personal property insurers have swung from a $132M underwriting loss two years ago to nearly $1 billion in underwriting gains in the most recent reporting period.

What's actually new vs. May 18–20 coverage: The May 18 brief noted "17 new carriers" as the headline market-structure number. The new report has revised that count up to 20 and added the underwriting-profitability swing — a number we hadn't quantified.

Why it matters: Capacity is no longer the binding constraint. The next 60 days are about pricing discipline and territorial appetite — new carriers tend to write conservatively in HVHZ (Miami-Dade, Broward) and aggressively in lower-risk inland zips. For Wellington and inland PBC, the "shop the policy" advisory is real money. For coastal Martin/St. Lucie, watch carriers' wind-only sublimits — that's where new entrants will try to thread the needle.

Source: https://www.insurancejournal.com/news/southeast/2026/05/21/870846.htm

Florida's Condo Insurance Market is Healing — Highest Writer Count in 15 Years; April Sales Up 7% YoY

The same report flags the condo association segment is recovering: the number of condo writers is at a 15-year high, and statewide condominium sales in April 2026 rose 7% over April 2025. This is the first credible "thaw" data point since the post-Surfside / HB 1021 disclosure crunch.

Why it matters for PA practice: Condo owners and associations with active milestone inspection findings (Dec 31, 2026 deadline, ~7 months out per yesterday's brief) now have more carrier options — but only if their structural reports are clean. The PA opening here is helping unit owners read their association's loss-assessment language before the next master policy renewal — that gap is where personal claims fall through.

30-Day Florida HO Rate Filing Average is Now NEGATIVE 2.3%

A new data point worth surfacing: the 30-day moving average of homeowners filings with OIR is −2.3%, versus +0.5% one year ago. Since January 2024, 39 carriers have filed for decreases and 48 have filed flat or 0% changes — a structural inflection.

Operational read: For renewal conversations between now and Sep 1, the default consumer expectation should be "flat or lower, not up." If a household is getting a non-renewal or material increase letter, that is now an outlier — and an outlier is a PA-advisable trigger to review the underlying inspection/scoring data driving the carrier's decision.

CONTRACTORS & SERVICE INDUSTRIES

2026 FBC HVHZ Updates — Miami-Dade RAS 118/119/120/127 Now Performance-Based

[Expanded angle vs. May 20] Yesterday's brief flagged the Florida Building Code Ninth Edition reset. The new detail: Miami-Dade County has updated RAS 118, 119, 120, 127 and related TAS protocols to remove many prescriptive methods and insert performance-based standards, including expanded structured tables for required underlayment uplift resistance.

What it means for roofers and homeowners:

  • Roof systems that passed inspection in prior code cycles may no longer comply under performance-based reads, especially older tile underlayments.
  • For homeowners: a "passed inspection 5+ years ago" wind-mitigation form is increasingly a paper credential, not a current compliance statement. This is the second wind-mitigation revisit signal in two weeks (see May 18 OIR-B1-1802 revision note).
  • For contractors: the new tables open up legitimate scope expansion on re-roofs — but only with current Miami-Dade Notice of Acceptance (NOA) documentation in hand.
