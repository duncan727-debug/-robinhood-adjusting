Heat Dome Concludes; Severe Thunderstorm and Wildfire Risks Emerge

South Florida's multi-day heat event concludes today as temperatures begin to moderate. The conclusion of dangerous heat brings new property risks: the National Weather Service is tracking a marginally elevated severe weather risk (Level 1/5) across portions of Florida, with potential for organized thunderstorm activity, gusty winds (40–60 mph), small hail, and locally heavy downpours (1–2 inches). Relative humidity has dropped to near-critical thresholds (30–40%) across interior South Florida, promoting elevated wildfire conditions—a new and growing concern for the 2026 season.

Florida is currently experiencing its most severe drought in more than 25 years, with hundreds of wildfires already reported in 2026, including in areas not typically prone to fire. This drought-fire cycle represents a novel exposure for property owners and a coverage gap many insurers have not adequately addressed.

Why it matters for service providers: Thunderstorm damage (hail, wind, water intrusion) will spike claim volume this week. Wildfire risk creates new demand for property inspections, risk assessment, and mitigation consulting. Water damage claims from heavy rain will trigger urgent mold remediation work—critical to execute within 24–48 hours before mold colonization occurs.

New Roof Inspection Law (SB 808) Takes Effect July 1; Expands Inspector Qualifications & Contractor Scope

Effective July 1, 2026, Florida Senate Bill 808 significantly expands who can perform roof inspections for insurance purposes. In addition to traditional professional engineers and architects, the following professionals are now authorized:

  • Home inspectors
  • Building code inspectors
  • General contractors
  • Roofing contractors

For roofs at least 15 years old, insurers must now differentiate between low-slope and steep-slope roofs in coverage determinations. The law also expands the permissible scope for licensed roofing contractors to include evaluation and enhancement of roof-to-wall connections for structures with wood roof decking—enhancements now required during any roof covering replacement or repair work.

Why it matters: This law reduces insurance friction around roof inspections and creates new revenue opportunities for roofing and general contractors. The roof-to-wall enhancement requirement opens retrofit and upgrade work previously outside standard contract scope. Public adjusters should familiarize themselves with these standards, as they will affect damage claims involving roof systems.

Sources: Florida Senate Bill 808 Text | Boggs Law Group—2026 Insurance Law Summary

Flood Insurance Alert: Critical Gap Before Hurricane Season

With hurricane season beginning in 20 days (June 1), insurance analysts are flagging a critical coverage gap: standard homeowners policies exclude flood damage entirely. Many property owners mistakenly believe that their wind/hail coverage extends to water damage from storm surge or heavy rainfall. Flood insurance requires a separate NFIP policy or private flood rider, with a separate, often substantial deductible. Once the National Hurricane Center names a tropical storm, insurers stop writing new flood policies and wind-only endorsements until the threat passes.

Why it matters: Public adjusters and service providers should proactively educate property owners on flood coverage gaps NOW, before moratoriums kick in.

Sources: Bridgeway Insurance—Hurricane Deductibles & Coverage | Florida Realtors—Storm Season Coverage

CONTRACTORS & SERVICE INDUSTRIES

Water Damage & Mold Claims: 24–48 Hour Window is Non-Negotiable

As Florida enters storm season facing elevated wildfire-related water damage, mold remediation will spike. A critical operational detail: wet or damp materials must be dried within 24–48 hours to prevent mold colonization. Under Florida Statute § 627.70131, insurers must acknowledge claims within 14 days and make coverage decisions within 90 days. Standard mold coverage is capped at $10,000 unless the policyholder purchases an optional endorsement ($25,000–$100,000+).

Why it matters: Contractors should budget for rapid-response operations during storm season. Public adjusters should understand mold coverage limits and endorsements, as hidden mold claims can quickly exceed standard coverage.

Sources: Louis Law Group—Mold Coverage | Florida Statute § 627.70131
