Florida Building Code 9th Edition — 211 Days Out, and the Permit-Date Rule Matters

The Florida Building Code (FBC) 9th Edition takes effect December 31, 2026. Per industry guidance, permits pulled before the effective date generally stay locked into the 8th Edition rules through completion; permits pulled after fall under the 9th Edition. The 8th Edition itself took effect December 31, 2023 — three-year cadence holding.

What the 9th Edition is expected to touch: updated wind-load tables, sealed-roof-deck refinements, and revised energy-code provisions. For High-Velocity Hurricane Zone (HVHZ) counties — Miami-Dade and Broward only — the HVHZ 30% rule can pull the entire building envelope into current code when substantial alterations are made. Translation: a 9th-Edition roof replacement on an older HVHZ home can drag windows, doors, and attachments into the new code on the same job.

What it means for the trades. Two distinct windows are opening at once:

  1. Spring/summer pull-forward. Owners with discretionary projects (roofs, additions, large remodels) have a clear incentive to permit before late December. Expect a permit-application bulge across PBC, Broward, and Miami-Dade through Q3.
  2. Stockpile-vs-spec. Material orders placed before December 31 that ship after may end up out of spec on certain assemblies. Put a written code-effective-date plan in every proposal you send between now and November — it answers the question before the homeowner asks it, and it protects you on a delayed ship date.

HVHZ contractors specifically: request the FBC update bulletins from your distributors now. Attachment and substrate requirements are the most likely points of change.

Source: Revolution Roofing FBC 9th Edition timeline; Florida Building Commission.

CILB Certified Renewal Closes August 31 — 89 Days

The Construction Industry Licensing Board's biennial renewal window for certified contractors (CGC, CRC, CCC, CMC, CFC, CPC) closes August 31, 2026. Late renewals trigger delinquent fees; longer lapses trigger license-void status. Continuing-education hours must be on file with the Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR) before renewal — not concurrent with it.

What it means. You have roughly 13 weeks. The CE providers get jammed in August and the DBPR portal slows down. A non-renewed license at midnight 8/31 takes your permits, your bonding, and your right to advertise as a licensed contractor down with it. If you haven't logged CE for this cycle, this week is the right week to schedule it.

Source: DBPR / CILB renewal schedule.

Citizens' $400K Flood Mandate Is Live — Inspection and Retrofit Demand Just Shifted

Effective January 1, 2026, Citizens Property Insurance Corporation requires every policyholder with Coverage A of $400,000 or more to carry flood insurance — regardless of FEMA flood zone, including Zone X. Without proof of an active National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) or approved private flood policy by the next renewal date, Citizens non-renews the underlying homeowners policy. Phase 2 on January 1, 2027 extends the requirement to all single-family Citizens policies.

What it means for service providers. A wave of South Florida homeowners is shopping flood coverage right now. That generates two things you can sell into:

  • Elevation certificates and flood-mitigation inspections. Private flood underwriters reward newer construction and homes above base flood elevation with 10–30% lower premium versus NFIP. Anything you can do to document elevation, foundation type, and mitigation features helps the homeowner's quote.
  • Drainage, grading, and dry-floodproofing scope. Owners pulling flood quotes for the first time are getting their first hard read on water exposure. Have a one-page "what I'd address first" leave-behind ready.

Source: Citizens Flood page; Flood Insurance Guru summary; Statewide Insurance summary.

Condo Milestone Inspection + SIRS — December 31, 2026 (211 Days Out)

Buildings reaching the 30-year age threshold in 2026 must complete their milestone inspection — and may complete the Structural Integrity Reserve Study (SIRS) simultaneously — by December 31, 2026. SIRS reserve funding under the new statute began January 1, 2026 for affected associations. Non-compliance consequences include daily fines exceeding $500/day, code-compliance referrals, special-magistrate hearings and liens, unsafe-building referrals to the Construction Board of Adjustment and Appeals (potential vacate order), and reporting to the state Division of Condominiums.

Pipeline math. Engineer capacity for the second half of 2026 is already tight in PBC, Broward, and Miami-Dade. The studies will identify scope; the scope drives RFPs to roofing, waterproofing, plumbing, electrical, and structural trades. The work window is 2027–2030. The bid window is now through Q4 2026. Build your pre-construction file (license, insurance, references, OSHA records) once and send it many times.

Source: Florida Statute 553.899; DBPR Condo Inspections.

Trade Activity — HVHZ Ratings, Broward License Fees

HVHZ wind ratings still 150–180+ mph for Broward and Miami-Dade. Wind ratings of 150–180+ mph remain the floor for permitted assemblies in Broward and Miami-Dade. Window, door, and roof attachments outside that envelope will not pass plan review or inspection. Code changes coming with the 9th Edition on December 31 may tighten attachment and substrate requirements further — get the bulletins now.

Broward contractor license fees. Contractor license application and renewal fee is $450, journeyman application $225 (renewal $120), change-of-status $350, letter of reciprocity $50.

HB 459 — The Disputed-Claim Procedure to Watch in Tallahassee

House Bill 459, proposed in the 2026 session, would create a mandatory Division of Administrative Hearings (DOAH) procedure for disputed property insurance claims: either side may file a petition, an Administrative Law Judge issues a coverage decision within 60 days and a final dollar figure within 180 days. The bill is proposed — not yet law. Carrier groups are broadly in favor; the trial bar opposes.

What it means for restoration, roofing, and water-mitigation contractors. Today's claim disputes that drag 12–24 months through circuit court would, under HB 459, resolve in roughly six months. That compresses the back-end timeline on supplemental claims, scope disputes, and code-upgrade arguments. If any version passes, the supplement playbook reshapes — start tracking the bill's committee calendar this month.

Source: HB 459 — Florida Senate; G&G Law Group analysis.
