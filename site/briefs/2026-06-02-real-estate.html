Cover the Bases at the Closing Table: Three Items Worth Putting in Writing

1. Condo Milestone + SIRS Deadline — December 31, 2026

For residential condominium buildings three or more habitable stories in height that reach the 30-year age threshold in 2026 (25 years in coastal areas, which captures most of Miami-Dade, Broward, and PBC east of I-95), Florida statute s. 553.899 requires the milestone inspection to be complete by December 31, 2026. The companion Structural Integrity Reserve Study (SIRS) is due on the same date.

For any condo listing in this category, the buyer-side question is no longer hypothetical: "where is the building in the milestone process, and has the SIRS been completed?" An association without a signed engineering engagement by midsummer is signaling either neglect or impending special assessment — both material disclosures.

The eight SIRS components for which reserve waivers are no longer permitted (budgets adopted after Dec 31, 2024): roof, load-bearing walls, primary structural members, fire protection, plumbing, electrical, waterproofing, windows and exterior doors. Any of these flagged in a SIRS draft becomes a near-certain assessment.

At-closing language to add to your disclosure conversation: "Has the association completed the milestone inspection and SIRS, or scheduled them? If scheduled, when, and with which engineer? Has the board discussed assessment funding?" Get the answers in writing from the association before contract.

2. National Flood Insurance Program — Authorization Expires September 30, 2026

NFIP's current authorization expires September 30, 2026. Congressional reauthorization before that date is required to avoid a lapse. A federally appointed council has recommended structural reforms — shifting more cost to states, moving the flood book toward the private market — which raises the political tape into the Q3 votes. Florida holds roughly 1.8 million NFIP policies, the most of any state.

For any closing scheduled in September, October, or early November in a designated flood zone: the financing contingency conversation should include the NFIP-lapse scenario. Lenders generally require flood coverage in SFHA zones. A clean private-market option may be available; confirm with the listing agent before contract.

3. HB 815 Takes Effect July 1, 2026

House Bill 815 changes the math on older roofs:

  • Under 15 years: carrier cannot refuse to issue or renew based on roof age alone, regardless of material.
  • 15+ years: carrier must allow an authorized inspector to evaluate. For steep-slope (>2-inch pitch), if the inspector certifies 5+ years of useful life remaining, the carrier cannot deny on age. For low-slope (≤2-inch pitch), if the inspector certifies the roof can be restored with a coating system that adds 5 years, the same protection applies.

Buyer-side application: A listing with a 17-year-old roof and a clean inspection letter is no longer auto-uninsurable. Encourage sellers in this position to commission the inspection pre-listing — it removes the most common financing-killer at appraisal.

The Insurance Backdrop Is Improving — Tell Your Clients

The June 1 reinsurance renewal cycle for Florida-writing carriers closed this weekend with property-cat pricing down 15-20% on a risk-adjusted basis and capacity at the highest level in six years. That is the wholesale read. Retail follows:

  • Citizens cut personal-lines rates by 2.6% statewide effective yesterday (first decrease since 2015); about 60% of policyholders see roughly $359 in annual savings.
  • Heritage Property & Casualty filed a tri-county reduction of up to 5% for PBC, Broward, and Miami-Dade.
  • Twenty new carriers have entered the Florida market since the 2022-23 reforms, with $850 million in fresh capital deployed.
  • Frontline Insurance Reciprocal Exchange begins writing new HO multi-peril business June 18, 2026 out of Lake Mary.

Expect additional primary-rate filings into Q3. For buyer clients who walked away from a PBC, Martin, or St. Lucie property over insurance cost in 2025, this is the cycle to revisit the spreadsheet. The combined effect of HB 815 (roof age), the new wind-mit form (effective April 1), and softer reinsurance pricing puts current-renewal premiums on a different trajectory than 2024-25.
