NOAA Calls for Below-Normal 2026 Atlantic Hurricane Season — 8–14 Named Storms, El Niño Is the Brake

NOAA's Climate Prediction Center 2026 Atlantic Hurricane Outlook, released last week, forecasts a below-normal season: 8 to 14 named storms, 3 to 6 hurricanes, and 1 to 3 major hurricanes (Category 3+). The probability assignment is 55% below-normal / 35% near-normal / 10% above-normal.

The mechanism is a developing El Niño, which historically elevates wind shear across the Atlantic basin and creates broader subsidence — both of which suppress tropical cyclone formation and intensification. Atlantic sea-surface temperatures are still slightly above normal and trade winds slightly weaker than average, but those tailwinds are not expected to outweigh the El Niño signal.

NOAA Administrator Neil Jacobs repeated the line that should anchor every conversation around this number: "It only takes one." Category 5 storms have made U.S. landfall in below-normal seasons before — Andrew (1992) is the textbook South Florida example.

What it means for service providers: A quieter season forecast is the opposite of what your booking calendar needs. Homeowner urgency for pre-season roof, shutter, generator, and tree-trim work falls when the headline is "below normal." Counter the noise in your June outreach: lead with the "it only takes one" framing and the Andrew comp. The work doesn't get cheaper after the first cone — and the inspection backlog quadruples within 48 hours of any landfall.

Florida's New Wind Mitigation Form (OIR-B1-1802) Took Effect April 1 — Tighter Documentation, Re-Inspect Before You Renew

The Office of Insurance Regulation's updated Uniform Mitigation Verification Inspection Form (OIR-B1-1802) took effect April 1, 2026. The revision incorporates findings from the 2024 Residential Wind-Loss Mitigation Study and changes both the credits available and the documentation required to claim them.

Headline changes:

  • More evidence per feature. Inspectors must now provide specific product approval numbers, photo documentation, and permit references for each mitigation feature claimed. "Visually inspected and consistent with" language no longer carries the same weight.
  • Updated discount tables. Several features qualify for higher discounts under the revised tables (notably modern hip-roof geometry with documented strapping); others move under stricter criteria.
  • Five-year validity holds. A passing 1802 is still good for five years provided no material change to the structure.

Citizens explicitly reserves the right to order an independent re-inspection to verify any 1802, and will reject any form where the inspector's Florida license is not active. That part is not new — but the audit volume on the updated form is expected to be heavier in the first year.

What it means for service providers: Roofers, garage door installers, and impact-window contractors with a licensed wind-mit inspector on staff have a tighter cross-sell window in 2026 than ever. Lead with: "We'll quote the upgrade and run the updated 1802 — most homeowners recapture 20–30% of the upgrade cost in year-one premium savings." Inspector training on the new form is the prerequisite — confirm your inspector is current on the April 2026 revision before quoting credits.

HB 815 Goes Live July 1 — Carriers Can No Longer Drop a Policy Solely Because the Roof is Old

House Bill 815 — the "Roofing Requirements for Property Insurance" bill — becomes effective July 1, 2026 and rewrites the rules around roof age as a basis for non-renewal. The bill closes the long-running "age-only" loophole that carriers have used to drop otherwise good policies over a roof that was simply over a threshold age.

Key provisions, as enacted:

  • Sole-age denial bar: Carriers cannot refuse to issue or renew a policy solely because the roof is less than 15 years old. This formalizes what was largely informal industry practice and prevents the recent drift toward 12- and 10-year cutoffs on certain carriers.
  • 15-year+ inspection right: For roofs 15 years or older, the homeowner has the right to have an authorized inspector evaluate the roof's actual condition before any non-renewal decision.
  • Steep-slope roofs (>2" pitch): If an inspector certifies the roof has at least 5 years of useful life remaining, the carrier cannot deny coverage on age alone.
  • Low-slope roofs (≤2" pitch): Carrier cannot refuse coverage if an inspector certifies the roof can be restored with a coating system that extends useful life by at least 5 years.

Applies to all property insurance policies issued or renewed on or after July 1, 2026 — homeowners, dwelling fire, and condo HO-6 forms included.

What it means for service providers (roofers especially): Two-edged. Some "replace the whole thing" jobs that historically came in under age-based pressure will now route to "inspect, certify, defer, and possibly coat" instead. That's revenue lost on full replacements. But: coating system installation on low-slope roofs becomes a newly statutorily-blessed category. Roofing contractors with elastomeric and silicone coating capabilities should be building that line item into 2026 estimates and marketing. Roof inspector certification is the high-leverage credential: every 15+ year-old roof in the South Florida book becomes a billable inspection over the next 24 months.

CARRIER & MARKET ACTIVITY

Three New Carriers Approved — Builder Reciprocal, Frontline Reciprocal, Wingsail; Total Reaches 20 New Entrants Since Reform

Insurance Commissioner Mike Yaworsky announced last week that three additional property and casualty carriers have been approved to write in Florida, bringing the post-reform total to 20 new entrants since the 2022–2023 legislative changes. Combined fresh capital across the 20 carriers is over $850 million.

Carrier Domicile Focus
**Builder Reciprocal Insurance Exchange** Texas Homeowners multi-peril, new/newer-home communities; $100M+ capital
**Frontline Insurance Reciprocal Exchange** Lake Mary, FL Multi-line including HO multi-peril; statewide all 67 counties
**Wingsail Insurance Company** Arizona (Spinnaker-owned) HO multi-peril, expanding into FL

The underlying market data continues to improve: Florida domestic property companies reported an 83% pooled combined ratio at year-end 2025, down from 94% at year-end 2024. The 30-day moving average of homeowners filings is −1.2% versus −0.3% a year ago — the negative drift continues.

What it means for service providers: New carriers means new approved-vendor lists for direct repair, mitigation, and inspection networks. Restoration firms, roofers, and water-mit contractors should monitor the OIR vendor postings and apply to the panels at Builder Reciprocal, Frontline Reciprocal, and Wingsail as they stand up Florida operations.

Port of Palm Beach Completes $30 Million Infrastructure Program

The Port of Palm Beach held a ribbon-cutting marking completion of its $30 million Port Infrastructure Development Program (PIDP). The upgrades expand cargo-handling capacity and harden waterside infrastructure — important for storm resilience on a working port that serves the Caribbean trade.

What it means for service providers: Hardened port infrastructure means a more reliable post-storm restart for cargo flow — which matters for any contractor whose materials supply chain runs through the Port. Worth flagging to vendors who run building-product imports.
