Lead Story

26 Days Until Hurricane Season — Your Action Window Is Closing

Atlantic Hurricane Season opens June 1. That's 26 days. Insurance companies pay attention to what's documented before a storm, not after — and the time to get that documentation done is now, while it's dry, while contractors are available, and while no one is rushing.

South Florida is in the middle of its best pre-season inspection window of the year. Highs are in the mid-to-upper 90s with no meaningful rain expected through mid-May. Roof inspections, impact shutter tests, gutter clearance, and exterior repairs can all be scheduled and completed before June 1 with minimal friction. That changes once the season opens and a single tropical system puts every mitigation contractor on backorder.

This year's Colorado State University forecast calls for a below-average season — 13 named storms vs. the 19-storm average, and 2 major hurricanes vs. the historical average of 3. Below-average is not the same as no risk. Florida-specific modeling shows a 74% probability that a named storm passes within 50 miles of the state this season, and a 15% chance of a major hurricane making East Coast landfall. "Below average" means fewer storms, not zero storms.

Regulatory Update

HB 815: New Protections If Your Insurer Cites Your Roof

A bill advancing through the Florida Legislature — House Bill 815 — would prohibit insurance carriers from refusing to renew your homeowner's policy solely because of your roof's age, without first conducting an actual physical inspection. It also establishes your right as a homeowner to initiate an independent inspection before a carrier-mandated replacement is required.

Why this matters now: carriers that are aware of pending legislation sometimes accelerate non-renewal decisions before new consumer protections take effect. If you've received a non-renewal notice or a conditional renewal citing roof age, don't treat it as final. An independent inspection — often ordered through a licensed public adjuster or a licensed roofing contractor — frequently changes the carrier's position.

If your insurer cited roof age on your last renewal: Request the specific inspection criteria they used. You are entitled to know. If no physical inspection was conducted, that's relevant to a dispute. Reply to this email or call 561-772-7528 — this is a conversation worth having before June 1.

Contractor Window Is Closing — Book Pre-Season Work This Month

Ninety percent of Florida roofing contractors report that finding skilled labor is their single biggest business constraint in 2026. Crews that will be completely booked in June are still accepting May appointments. If you need roof repairs, impact window installation, gutter work, or mitigation improvements, the week of May 6–13 is the optimal window. Dry weather, available crews, and no storm urgency creating price pressure. After June 1, all of those conditions change simultaneously.

Workforce

90% of Roofers Cite Labor as #1 Constraint — and It's Not Getting Better Before June

Industry data from the Florida Roof Association and Roofing Contractor Magazine confirms what most shop owners already know: nine out of ten Florida roofing contractors identify finding skilled labor as their single biggest business problem heading into the 2026 season.

The firms pulling ahead aren't solving this with more job postings. They're treating it as a systems problem:

  • Structured training paths that retain crew members rather than cycling through new hires
  • AI-assisted scheduling tools that prevent crew overbooking and surface fatigue-risk alerts
  • Wage competitiveness built into every contract — not offered reactively when workers threaten to leave
  • Apprenticeship pipeline investment to build 12–18 month lead time on skilled capacity

The offseason — right now, before June 1 — is the last low-pressure window to make structural changes. If you're still running on reactive hiring when the first named storm activates, you'll be watching jobs go to whoever has crews.
