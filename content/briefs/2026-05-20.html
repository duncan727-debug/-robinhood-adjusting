INSURANCE & REGULATION

Fannie/Freddie 15% Reserve Requirement — Florida-Specific PERS Retirement Already in Effect

[Newly Surfaced — Material for Condo-Heavy SF Market] On March 18, 2026, Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac issued updated condominium and HOA project eligibility standards with two changes Florida should care about:

1. Reserve funding minimum raised from 10% to 15% of total annual budgeted assessment income — effective for loan applications dated January 4, 2027 and later. Many Florida associations are not even satisfying the prior 10% threshold today.

2. Florida-specific PERS retirement (already in effect): New or newly converted attached condo projects in Florida no longer need to go through the Project Eligibility Review Service. They can now be reviewed under the same lender-delegated Full Review process used in other states. This is immediate compliance relief for new project lending in PBC, Broward, and Miami-Dade.

The compound pressure point for unit owners: South Florida condo associations face a stacking sequence:

  • 2024 reserve-funding mandate (post-Surfside)
  • Special assessments peaking in 2026–2028
  • HB 1021 mandatory document disclosure (effective Jan 1, 2026)
  • New: 15% Fannie/Freddie reserve floor (effective Jan 4, 2027) — gives associations ~7 months to reach compliance or face mortgage lending obstacles for unit sales

PA / advisor angle: When a unit owner gets a special-assessment notice in 2026, the conversation is no longer just "your master policy vs. your HO-6." It's now also: "if your association can't hit 15% reserves by January 2027, your unit becomes harder to sell to buyers using conforming financing." That's a coverage-and-resale conversation, not a claim conversation.

SB 266 Public Adjuster Standards — 60-Day Written Estimate Rule Is the Operational Change

[Skipped: Already covered May 18-19] [New Angle: The 60-day estimate clock]

SB 266 (effective July 1, 2026) is usually discussed for its vulnerable-adult rescission rights. The under-discussed operational piece worth surfacing: PAs must provide clients and their insurers a written estimate within 60 days of contract execution, unless failure is caused by factors beyond the adjuster's control. The 60-day cancellation window ceases when the estimate is provided — so timely estimates also lock in the engagement faster.

Practical workflow change for Duncan and any PA practice:

  • Every signed contract starting July 1 has a calendar-tracked 60-day estimate deadline.
  • Build a CRM/calendar reminder rule: Day +50 = "estimate due in 10 days" trigger.
  • Document any factors-beyond-control delays in writing immediately (carrier delays, inspector access, etc.) — that's the only legal defense if the 60 days slip.

CONTRACTORS & SERVICE INDUSTRIES

Florida Building Code Ninth Edition (FBC 2026) — HVHZ Roofing Approval Reset

[Quietly Material — Verify Before June 1] The Ninth Edition Florida Building Code (FBC 2026) has compliance triggers that contractors and PAs should verify before storm season:

  • In High Velocity Hurricane Zones (Miami-Dade and Broward counties): self-adhering underlayment is mandatory.
  • Roofing systems approved for use on lightweight concrete over steel decks that were qualified solely by 2 ft × 2 ft uplift testing must be updated to comply with the new code.

Why this is an active issue this week: Properties that completed roof work in late 2025 or early 2026 may have been installed under older system approvals. If an underwriter or carrier asks for documentation post-storm, stale product approval notices could become a coverage friction point. Worth pulling Miami-Dade Product Approval Notices for any client claim involving a recent re-roof.

Operational follow-up:

  • Roofing contractors: confirm your current product approvals are FBC 2026-compliant; refresh any documentation referencing older approvals.
  • PAs: when reviewing a roof claim in HVHZ counties, verify underlayment specification in addition to wind-mitigation form OIR-B1-1802. Self-adhering underlayment is a code item, not a discretionary feature.

Mold Remediation Licensing — Chapter 468 Reminder Heading Into Wet Season

[Operational Reminder — Not New, But Newly Relevant] Under Florida Statutes Chapter 468, Part XVI, any mold-related service on an area larger than 10 square feet requires an active DBPR mold assessor or mold remediator license.

Why call this out today: As the rainy season ramps and water-intrusion calls surge over the next 4–8 weeks, expect unlicensed operators to surface chasing volume. Two practical screens:

  • Always pull DBPR license verification before referring or accepting a mitigation referral.
  • Pricing benchmark: $10–$25 per square foot for standard remediation; black mold (Stachybotrys chartarum) drives a 15–25% premium due to containment protocols.

PA implication: Carriers are increasingly scrutinizing remediation invoices for licensure documentation. An unlicensed remediator's invoice can be partially or fully denied — which becomes the policyholder's out-of-pocket problem. Verify upfront, not after the claim is filed.
