Florida's 1% Insurance Surcharge Ends Two Years Early — Oct 1, 2026; ~$650M in Consumer Savings

The Florida Insurance Guaranty Association's 1% emergency assessment — the line item that has been appearing on every property insurance policy since October 1, 2023 — will end on October 1, 2026, two years earlier than originally scheduled. The assessment will still appear on policies that renew through Sept. 30, 2026, then drop off.

The state estimates the early termination will save Florida homeowners and businesses up to $650 million over the next two years through Sept. 30, 2028. The surcharge was put in place to repay bonds that funded unpaid claims and unearned-premium refunds from a wave of insurer insolvencies in 2021–2022. Florida Insurance Commissioner Mike Yaworsky cited the early end as "another indicator that the insurance market has stabilized."

What it means for homeowners: On a typical $4,500 annual homeowners premium, the 1% surcharge is about $45/year — modest in isolation, but it disappears on every policy in Florida starting Oct 1. If you renew between now and Sept 30, 2026, the surcharge is still on the bill. After that date, it should be gone. Check the line items on your renewal declarations to confirm.

What it means for real estate professionals: This is a tailwind talking point for buyers nervous about insurance costs. The market-stabilization message is no longer just a forecast — it now has a regulator-confirmed action behind it.

The combined West Palm Beach and Boca Raton metro now ranks fifth in the United States for office space currently under construction, with nearly 1.6 million square feet in the active development pipeline. The driver is the now well-documented "Wall Street South" relocation wave — hedge funds, private equity firms, family offices, and major financial institutions opening satellite headquarters or moving operations to Florida to capture the no-state-income-tax advantage and post-2020 talent migration.

What it means for real estate professionals: The class-A office absorption story is durable for at least the next 24 months — there is real, contracted demand behind the construction. Commercial brokers should be tracking pre-leasing percentages on the top three projects; that's the leading indicator for whether the pipeline holds or wobbles.

What it means for service providers: A 1.6 million square foot construction pipeline = a multi-year revenue band for site work, mechanical, electrical, finishes, life safety, and tenant build-out trades. Subcontractors not yet on these projects' approved-vendor lists should be applying now while general contractors are still building out their bench. Once shells top off, the build-out window favors firms already pre-qualified.

WEATHER

Tropical Wave at 23°W in the Far Eastern Atlantic — First Genuine Signal of the Season

The National Hurricane Center's 7-day Atlantic outlook still shows no tropical cyclone formation expected in the North Atlantic, Caribbean, or Gulf — but a tropical wave with axis near 23°W is moving westward at about 10 knots, from roughly 0°N to 10°N latitude. This is the first organized tropical wave to show up in the eastern Atlantic this pre-season window. It is not a formation threat today, but it is the kind of signal that, if it survives a 2–3 week westward transit, gets watched closely as a "Cape Verde precursor."

The previously active Caribbean Gale Warning offshore Colombia has been diminishing.

What it means for homeowners and service providers: No action needed today. Note for context only — a quiet outlook can still produce an active first storm if the eastern Atlantic stays organized. Hurricane season opens June 1 (9 days).
