Lead Story

26 Days Until Hurricane Season — Your Action Window Is Closing

Atlantic Hurricane Season opens June 1. That's 26 days. Insurance companies pay attention to what's documented before a storm, not after — and the time to get that documentation done is now, while it's dry, while contractors are available, and while no one is rushing.

South Florida is in the middle of its best pre-season inspection window of the year. Highs are in the mid-to-upper 90s with no meaningful rain expected through mid-May. Roof inspections, impact shutter tests, gutter clearance, and exterior repairs can all be scheduled and completed before June 1 with minimal friction. That changes once the season opens and a single tropical system puts every mitigation contractor on backorder.

This year's Colorado State University forecast calls for a below-average season — 13 named storms vs. the 19-storm average, and 2 major hurricanes vs. the historical average of 3. Below-average is not the same as no risk. Florida-specific modeling shows a 74% probability that a named storm passes within 50 miles of the state this season, and a 15% chance of a major hurricane making East Coast landfall. "Below average" means fewer storms, not zero storms.

What often gets buried: rate adjustments frequently come with quiet changes to exclusions, sublimits, and wind deductible structures in the same renewal documents. Before you celebrate the lower premium, read what changed.

If you're receiving multiple renewal notifications this season, that's the market working in your favor. Use the window to review what each policy actually covers — wind vs. water damage, debris removal limits, ALE (additional living expenses) in case your home is uninhabitable after a storm. Those line items matter more than the premium when a claim gets filed.

Regulatory Update

HB 815: New Protections If Your Insurer Cites Your Roof

A bill advancing through the Florida Legislature — House Bill 815 — would prohibit insurance carriers from refusing to renew your homeowner's policy solely because of your roof's age, without first conducting an actual physical inspection. It also establishes your right as a homeowner to initiate an independent inspection before a carrier-mandated replacement is required.

Why this matters now: carriers that are aware of pending legislation sometimes accelerate non-renewal decisions before new consumer protections take effect. If you've received a non-renewal notice or a conditional renewal citing roof age, don't treat it as final. An independent inspection — often ordered through a licensed public adjuster or a licensed roofing contractor — frequently changes the carrier's position. A roof that an insurer flags as "aging" may pass a physical inspection with no deficiencies noted, at which point the coverage grounds dissolve.

If your insurer cited roof age on your last renewal: Request the specific inspection criteria they used. You are entitled to know. If no physical inspection was conducted, that's relevant to a dispute. Reply to this email or call 561-772-7528 — this is a conversation worth having before June 1.

Action Item

Contractor Window Is Closing — Book Pre-Season Work This Month

Ninety percent of Florida roofing contractors report that finding skilled labor is their single biggest business constraint in 2026. Crews that will be completely booked in June are still accepting May appointments. The math is straightforward: pre-season scheduling is easier, faster, and less expensive than post-storm emergency scheduling under a declared disaster.

If you need roof repairs, impact window installation, gutter work, or mitigation improvements, the week of May 6–13 is the optimal window. Dry weather, available crews, and no storm urgency creating price pressure. After June 1, all of those conditions change simultaneously.
