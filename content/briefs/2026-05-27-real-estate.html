South Florida Real Estate & Insurance Brief

May 27, 2026 | Palm Beach, Martin, Miami-Dade, St. Lucie, Broward

> Five days to hurricane season. Two regulatory changes (HB 815 + the new wind mitigation form) and the post-reform carrier expansion materially shift what's negotiable, what's disclosable, and what buyers will ask you between now and August. Below: what to know, what to say, and what to put in the contract.

HB 815 turns roof-age listings from problem to differentiator

If you have inventory with a 12-18 year-old roof, July 1, 2026 is the date your listing strategy changes. House Bill 815 takes effect that day and reframes the insurance conversation around roof age:

  • A carrier cannot refuse to issue or renew a policy solely because the roof is less than 15 years old. This closes the cutoff drift toward 12- and 10-year thresholds.
  • For 15+ year-old roofs, the homeowner has an enforceable right to a licensed inspection before any non-renewal. A certification of 5+ years useful life (sloped) or coating-extendable life (flat) blocks an age-only denial.

At the closing table: the long-standing "we'd love this house but our carrier wants a new roof" buyer objection becomes much weaker after July 1. The fix moves from a $25-40K capital expenditure (full replacement) to a $400-600 inspection and, where applicable, a coating treatment.

Listing language that closes deals in June-July:

> "The roof on this property is [X] years old. Under Florida HB 815 (effective July 1), buyers retain the right to a licensed roof inspection before any non-renewal — and a coating system is a statutorily-recognized extension option for low-slope roofs."

That sentence in a property description or in your buyer-agent counter neutralizes the "but the insurance" objection on 60-70% of the inventory in PBC and Broward built between 2008 and 2014.

What to put in the disclosure conversation now: roof age + last inspection date + whether the seller has done any of (a) recent shingle/tile repair, (b) underlayment work, (c) coating treatment. Get those into the seller's disclosure even if buyer financing is conventional, not FHA — the insurance conversation runs in parallel to the lender's conversation.

The new wind mitigation form (1802) is a pricing tool on every closing

OIR's updated Uniform Mitigation Verification Inspection Form took effect April 1. The change matters at the closing table because the form is now what carriers actually price off — and the credits available under the revised tables run higher in some cases than the pre-April version.

For your listings (especially anything with a roof, garage door, or window upgrade in the last 5 years):

  • A fresh 1802 under the new form is a closing-table differentiator. Instead of "your insurance estimate," buyers can see a current, post-revision form showing the actual credits the new carrier will apply.
  • On a $4,500 typical premium, a full credit set (hurricane straps + secondary water barrier + impact openings) pulls $800-$1,500 per year off the bill. Over a typical 7-year hold, that's $5,600-$10,500 in carrying-cost savings — a real number to put in front of a buyer who's pencil-sharpening on monthly cost.

For your buyer-side clients in PBC and Broward (where wind mitigation matters most):

  • Make a current 1802 a contract contingency on any closing in the next 6 months. Cost: $150-$300. Value: a defensible number on monthly insurance carrying cost before they sign.
  • On any property with NO recent mitigation upgrade, the same form serves as a "here's the realistic insurance number" anchor — useful when sellers are unrealistic on price.

Cocktail-conversation framing that lands with prospective sellers: "the new wind mitigation form means buyers are getting much sharper insurance quotes earlier in the process — listings with current credits documented are clearing faster."

Three new carriers expand the quote shelf — keep an independent-agent short list current

The state regulator approved three more property carriers last week: Builder Reciprocal (Texas-based, newer-home focus), Frontline Reciprocal (Lake Mary FL, statewide), Wingsail (Arizona, Spinnaker-owned). Post-reform new entrants now stands at 20 with combined fresh capital over $850 million.

What it changes for your transactions:

  • "I can only get Citizens" buyers are getting rarer every quarter. Buyers in the $400K-$900K PBC and Broward inventory band benefit most from competitive quoting against the new entrants — that's the price band where premium differences between Citizens, the legacy private carriers, and the new reciprocal exchanges can be $1,200-$3,000 per year.
  • The reciprocal-exchange structure (Builder + Frontline) is a member-owned ownership model. It looks different on the corporate slide but doesn't change anything visible to your buyer at policy time. Don't let an unfamiliar carrier name kill an otherwise-clean quote — verify the Demotech or AM Best rating and the mortgage servicer's approved-carrier list, then proceed.

Operational play:

  • Maintain a current short list of two or three independent agents (not captive agents) who actively quote the new entrants. Refresh the list quarterly — the carrier mix is moving fast enough that an agent who was the right referral 12 months ago may not be today.
  • For buyers in transit (under contract, pre-closing), point them at an independent agent the moment the inspection contingency clears. The 10-14 day window between inspection and final loan approval is when insurance pricing should get firmed up. Waiting until 48 hours before close is the most common reason a deal hits a financing snag.

Citizens depopulation: 546K policies moved — watch for the offer letters in your closings

Two pieces of Citizens news worth tracking together:

1. Citizens filed for a 2.6% statewide rate decrease for personal multi-peril. Third consecutive small decrease — the underlying signal is that the Citizens book is stabilizing.

2. Since the 2023 reforms, Citizens has cleared 546,000 policies through depopulation — transferred them to private carriers.

At the closing table: if your buyer is taking over a Citizens policy at close, or your seller has been with Citizens for the last 3 years, the depopulation conversation is alive on that file. A depopulation offer can land in the seller's mailbox during the listing period — and the buyer's name on the new policy gets the offer 60-90 days post-close.

The buyer can opt out and keep Citizens, but the private carrier offers usually come with broader coverage and zero assessment-risk exposure. Tell your buyer to expect a transfer offer letter and to read it carefully before responding — and to call you with the carrier name so you can route them to your independent agent for a second opinion.

This is a small detail, but the buyers who feel best-served are the ones who weren't surprised by the letter. The buyers who feel worst-served are the ones who threw it out as "junk mail" and only discovered the issue at renewal.

Pre-season window closes June 1 — bind-on-day-one is the new normal

NOAA's 2026 outlook: 8-14 named storms, 3-6 hurricanes. Below-normal forecast, El Niño the mechanism.

The detail that matters at the closing table: most carriers stop binding new wind policies 24-72 hours before a named storm enters the Florida cone. For June and July closings, "I'll handle the insurance the day of funding" is the most expensive sentence a buyer can say.

Standing rule for your June-September closings:

> Insurance is bound on day one of contract, not day-of-funding. Add it to your buyer-checklist email at executed-contract.

For sellers, the parallel rule: do not let a wind policy lapse during the listing period. A property with an active wind binder is materially easier to transact than one where coverage has been allowed to lapse and the new owner has to qualify from cold. Confirm policy status before signing the listing agreement.

Port of Palm Beach completes $30M infrastructure program — Riviera Beach industrial pricing tailwind

The Port of Palm Beach held a ribbon cutting marking completion of its $30 million Port Infrastructure Development Program. Hardened waterside infrastructure, expanded cargo capacity.

For your industrial and warehouse listings in Riviera Beach and the Port adjacency: the completed upgrades are a tailwind for asking-rent stability on port-served logistics product. Investor interest in this segment has been steady but constrained by older infrastructure — the completed PIDP removes one of the long-standing underwriting questions. Worth flagging on any pitch deck in this submarket for the next 12 months.
