## The Countdown Begins

It's May 4th, and the Atlantic Hurricane Season officially opens in exactly 30 days. 

For contractors, public adjusters, property managers, and insurance professionals in South Florida, this isn't a date on the calendar—it's a dividing line. After June 1st, everything changes. Phone lines get busier. Schedules get tighter. Property owners shift from planning mode to panic mode. Insurance companies buckle down. The market that was calm just becomes... frantic.

But right now? You have something most professionals waste: **time.**

And time, in May, is worth far more than it will be in July.

## The Current Market Picture

Let's be clear about what's happening in South Florida right now:

- **Insurance rates are dropping.** USAA, Allstate, Citizens Property Insurance—all announcing rate relief. That's not just good news for homeowners; it's opportunity for you. When rates stabilize, homeowners think about optimization, not just cost. They're open to conversations about mitigation, coverage audits, and risk management.
- **Contractor schedules are still open.** Labor constraints persist in roofing, HVAC, and water mitigation, but scheduling friction hasn't hit peak yet. You can still book wind mitigation projects, roof inspections, and hurricane-hardening work without waiting six weeks.
- **The forecast is favorable.** Colorado State University is projecting a below-average hurricane season (13 named storms vs. 19 average; only 2 major hurricanes vs. 3 average). That's not permission to relax—it's permission to be strategic instead of reactive.
- **Wealth is flowing into South Florida.** Palm Beach is in its 7th consecutive month of sales growth. Miami-Dade has 28% of homes now priced above $1 million. High-net-worth relocations mean high-net-worth clients who are new to Florida's insurance landscape, often unfamiliar with wind mitigation requirements, and actively thinking about risk management.

Separately, these are nice market signals. Together, they create a **specific window that closes June 1st**.

## Why May Matters (And June Doesn't)

Think about the difference between May and September.

**In May**, a property manager calls you about wind mitigation because she's being proactive. She's thinking about next season. The homeowner is calm. No claims are on the table. You can take time to assess, recommend, and build a relationship. Insurance carriers approve mitigation investments readily. There's no time pressure—you have time to do the work right.

**In September**, a contractor calls you because a thunderstorm damaged half a roof and the owner is panicking. They want the fastest estimate, not the best contractor. Insurance companies are slammed with claims. Your crew is booked solid. You're replacing a roof, not building a relationship. The homeowner signs up because they have no choice, not because they trust you.

One is consultative. The other is transactional.

If you spend May being strategic—meeting with property managers, offering inspections, documenting mitigation needs—you're positioning yourself to be the advisor, not the vendor. You're the one who knows the property. You're the one who's already been inside and diagnosed the problems. When a storm hits in August and the owner needs emergency work, you're the call they make.

This matters because advisors get better contracts, better relationships, and better margins.

## What to Do With Your 30 Days

### 1. **Inspect and Document**

If you're a contractor: Offer proactive inspections. Bundle them. Make it a package—"May Wind Mitigation Audit." Walk the property. Document everything. Take photos. Talk about roofing condition, impact windows, hurricane clips, water intrusion risks. Build a report. This isn't a sales pitch; it's data. But that data is also your inventory of work to propose.

If you're a property manager or adjuster: Same logic. Visit properties. Understand what's actually there. Too many properties have mitigation on paper that doesn't exist in practice. Be the person who knows.

### 2. **Talk About Rates (And How Mitigation Matters)**

Insurance rates are dropping, and homeowners are paying attention. But they shouldn't just drop their current carrier—they should review their coverage and their risk posture.

Position mitigation consulting as part of a broader optimization strategy. "Your rates are falling because the market is stabilizing and fraud is being prosecuted. But here's what protects your property: documented wind mitigation, regular inspections, and carriers who trust your risk profile. Let's make sure you've got this locked in."

This conversation keeps you in the relationship. It also builds a repeat service model—annual inspections, ongoing compliance, documented maintenance.

### 3. **Target High-Net-Worth Relocations**

Palm Beach is hot. Miami-Dade is concentrating wealth. If you're a public adjuster or contractor, target recently relocated affluent buyers. They're new to Florida. They have significant property values. They're thinking about risk. They don't yet have trusted contractors or adjusters.

This is your market. Reach out. Educate. Position yourself as the professional who helps them understand Florida-specific risks.

### 4. **Prepare Your Capacity**

Use May to staff up for June. Hire seasonal labor. Train crews. Stock materials. If you know you're going to be busy in hurricane season, prepare now while you can think clearly. Scrambling in July is expensive and low-quality.

## The Rate-Relief Advantage

Here's something important: The decline in insurance rates means carriers are recovering confidence in the Florida market. That confidence came from prosecuting fraud, reducing litigation, and seeing legitimate PA engagement. 

If you're a public adjuster, this is your opening. You're not the villain anymore—you're the professional who protects honest claims. Your licensing, bonding, and transparent fee structure are your brand. Use May to reinforce this positioning with property managers, carriers, and contractors.

If you're a contractor, it means insurance companies are more willing to approve mitigation investments because they trust the risk-reduction mathematics. Build this relationship. Become the contractor that insurance companies recommend for wind mitigation projects.

## The 30-Day Timeline

- **Week of May 4:** Schedule inspections. Reach out to recent relocations. Get your team planning capacity for June.
- **Week of May 11:** Complete inspections. Deliver reports. Start mitigation estimates.
- **Week of May 18:** Finalize contracts for May completion or early June scheduling. Prepare crews.
- **Week of May 25:** Finish mitigation work. Lock in relationships. Brief your teams on June protocol.

By June 1st, you're not scrambling to understand the market. You're ready.

## Looking Ahead

June 1st is not a crisis point—it's just when the market's attention shifts. Property owners who completed inspections in May won't need emergency consultations in June. Contractors who built relationships in May have a backlog already. Adjusters who positioned mitigation and risk management are ready to walk into claims with a clean property history and a documented relationship.

The properties that are unprepared in June will need you. But you'd rather be the advisor they've known since May than the emergency vendor they just found.
