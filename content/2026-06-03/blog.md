# 211 Days to the FBC 9th Edition: What South Florida Contractors Need to Decide This Summer

If you run a roofing, window, HVAC, or general contracting business in South Florida, there's one date on the calendar that's about to shape your next twelve months more than any other: **December 31, 2026** — the day the Florida Building Code 9th Edition takes effect.

That's 211 days from today.

Most of the contractors I talk to know the date is coming. Very few have a written plan for the transition. This post lays out the three decisions worth making in June — not November — so your pipeline, your distributor relationships, and your bids all land on the right side of the cutover.

## The Permit-Pull Rule Is Simpler Than You Think

The mechanics are straightforward, even if the implications aren't:

- **Permits pulled before December 31, 2026** are generally locked into 8th Edition rules through completion.
- **Permits pulled on or after December 31, 2026** fall under the 9th Edition.

This means the permit-pull date — not the contract date, not the start date, not the inspection date — is what governs the job. The 8th Edition itself took effect December 31, 2023, so this is a steady three-year cycle. The 9th Edition is expected to fold in updated wind-load tables, sealed-roof-deck refinements, and revised energy-code provisions.

For most discretionary projects with flexible scheduling, this creates a clear pull-forward window between now and late December.

## The HVHZ 30% Rule Is the Real Trap

If you operate in **Miami-Dade or Broward** — the only two High Velocity Hurricane Zone (HVHZ) counties — you need to be especially careful with the 30% rule when scoping any 9th Edition work.

In short: when substantial alterations are made to an existing building in an HVHZ county, the **entire building envelope** can be pulled into current-code compliance. A 9th-Edition roof replacement on an older HVHZ home can drag windows, doors, and attachments into the new code — turning what the homeowner thought was a $25,000 roof into a $60,000 envelope conversation.

This isn't new. The 30% rule has been on the books for years. But the moment the 9th Edition is in effect, "current code" means stricter assemblies, and the gap between what the homeowner expects and what the inspector requires gets wider.

Practical implication: every HVHZ bid you write for jobs that may permit in 2027 should include explicit envelope-cost ranges and a conversation about scope sequencing.

## The Material-Stockpile Problem Nobody Is Talking About

Here's the one that tends to catch contractors off guard. Material orders placed in fall 2026 that ship after December 31 may end up out of spec on certain assemblies under the new code. The product you ordered on the old spec is still good product — but it may not be code-compliant for a permit pulled in January.

Three things to do this month, not in October:

1. **Ask your distributors for a written code-effective-date plan.** Which SKUs change spec on January 1? Which are unaffected? Get it in writing.
2. **Map your Q4 material orders to a permit-pull date, not a ship date.** If a permit pulls in December but the panels arrive in January, you're potentially out of spec on attachments or substrate.
3. **Flag the 9th Edition explicitly in any contract you sign now for work that may permit after Dec 31.** A two-line clause about code-effective-date and material-spec adjustment protects both sides.

## The Two Pipeline Strategies Worth Considering

For most service providers, the strategic question is binary: **front-load or wait?**

### Front-Load Strategy
Push every discretionary project — roofs, additions, large remodels, window replacement programs — into the June-through-November permit-pull window. You get:

- Locked-in 8th Edition rules through completion
- Known material specs across the full job
- Stronger Q4 revenue
- A cleaner Q1 2027 (no scramble to re-quote anything)

Tradeoff: you're racing the permit office, and inspector availability in PBC, Broward, and Miami-Dade is already tight on the condo milestone work (the SIRS December 31 deadline is hitting at the same time).

### Wait Strategy
Hold off on bidding marginal jobs until 9th Edition specs are fully published and your distributors have clean replacement SKUs. You get:

- No transition risk
- Higher bid prices once everyone reprices to the new code
- Time to retrain crews on updated assemblies

Tradeoff: a slower Q4 and a guaranteed Q1 reprice on anything still open.

The right answer depends on your trade and your backlog. Roofers with full pipelines through November often default to front-load. GCs with discretionary remodels often default to wait. Window and door installers in HVHZ counties almost always benefit from front-load given the envelope drag.

## How a Public Adjuster Fits Into the 9th Edition Conversation

You may be wondering why a public adjuster is writing about building code transitions. Here's the connection: the same homeowners who are about to face a code-driven cost surprise in 2027 are the same homeowners filing storm and water claims today. When an insurance carrier writes a roof or envelope claim that gets repaired in 2027 under 9th Edition rules, the **Ordinance & Law** coverage on the policy is what bridges the gap between "what was there" and "what the new code requires."

Most homeowner policies in Florida carry between 10% and 25% Ordinance & Law coverage as standard, with higher endorsements available. A roof claim that triggers the HVHZ 30% rule with weak O&L coverage is a claim that leaves a homeowner — and the contractor doing the work — fighting over a shortfall that the policy should have covered.

Service providers who flag this in their initial scoping conversations tend to keep more jobs out of disputes. And when a dispute does happen, a PA can document the code-required scope in a way that pushes the carrier to honor the O&L limits properly.

## What to Do This Month

If you take one action from this post, make it this: **sit down with your distributor rep this month** and get a written list of which materials change spec on January 1, 2027, and which don't. Everything else — the pipeline strategy, the bid language, the homeowner conversations — flows from knowing what's actually going to be different on the shelf.

The contractors who plan the transition now win Q1 2027. The ones who don't are going to spend January re-quoting jobs they thought were closed.
