# HB 815 Takes Effect July 1: What South Florida Roofers, Inspectors, and Contractors Need Ready in 30 Days

Hurricane season opened this morning. The National Hurricane Center shows zero formations expected across the Atlantic basin for the next seven days, and NOAA's 2026 outlook calls for a below-normal season — 8 to 14 named storms, 3 to 6 hurricanes. Quiet on the tropical map.

That makes the next 30 days the most valuable runway service providers in South Florida have had in a long time. Because on **July 1, 2026, House Bill 815 takes effect** — and it quietly rewrites the carrier-to-roof relationship that has been driving non-renewals across Palm Beach, Broward, Martin, and St. Lucie counties for the last three years.

If you're a roofer, an authorized inspector, a general contractor, a property manager, or a restoration shop with PA referral relationships — this is your bill. Here is what changes, why it creates a new revenue surface, and what you should have built before July 1.

## What HB 815 Actually Says

The enacted text of HB 815 makes four operative changes for residential property policies issued or renewed on or after July 1, 2026:

1. **15-year floor.** Insurers may not refuse to issue or renew a residential property policy *solely* because the roof is less than 15 years old. Material is irrelevant — shingle, tile, metal, flat — the floor is the floor.

2. **Steep-slope inspection pathway.** For roofs 15 years or older with a pitch greater than 2 inches per foot, the homeowner has the right to engage an **authorized inspector**. If the inspector certifies the roof has **at least 5 years of useful life remaining**, the carrier cannot use roof age as the basis for denial.

3. **Low-slope coating pathway.** For roofs 15 years or older with a pitch of 2 inches or less, the inspector may certify that the roof can be **restored with a coating system that extends useful life by at least 5 years**. Same effect — carrier cannot use roof age as the basis for denial.

4. **Applies forward only.** Renewals issued before July 1 fall under the prior framework. So the next 30 days of policy paper still plays by the old rules.

The bill does not stop carriers from non-renewing for other reasons — claims history, structural condition, distance to coast, code-compliance gaps. What it stops is the lazy roof-age auto-decline that has been removing otherwise-insurable homes from the market.

## Why This Is a Business Opportunity, Not a Compliance Item

Read that again: the *carrier* cannot refuse based on roof age **if an authorized inspector certifies useful life.**

That sentence is a procurement event.

Every single renewal cycle from July 1 onward, every Florida homeowner with a 15-plus-year roof and a non-renewal notice will need an inspection-and-useful-life letter to stay insured. That's a recurring, predictable, statutory-driven service demand — and right now there is no standard pricing surface, no standard turnaround SLA, and no template that every carrier has agreed to accept on the first pass.

The trades that show up to July 1 with all three figured out are going to own this lane.

## What Service Providers Should Have Ready Before July 1

### For Roofers and Authorized Inspectors

**A defined inspection product.** Not "we can do that." A named, priced, time-bounded service: *HB 815 Certified Roof Life Inspection — $X — 5 business day turnaround — includes carrier-ready useful-life letter.* That product needs to live on your website, on your invoice template, and in your phone-script answer.

**A letter template.** Cover page, inspector credentials, photos, scope of inspection, methodology (visual + moisture + fastener pull where applicable), explicit useful-life finding tied to the statutory 5-year standard, signature, license number. The letter is the deliverable. Carriers will reject thin or vague letters and the homeowner will blame the inspector, not the carrier.

**A coating-system referral if you're a steep-slope shop.** Low-slope coating certifications are a separate pathway under the statute. If you don't do flat-roof coatings, line up the one shop in your county you trust to handle the low-slope half of the calls you'll get.

### For General Contractors and Property Managers

**A go-to inspector.** Your phone is about to ring with HOA boards, condo managers, and homeowner clients asking the same question for the next 18 months: *"Who does the HB 815 inspection?"* Pre-build that list. Two or three names per county, with current pricing and SLA.

**A renewal-window protocol.** Property managers should pull renewal-date reports and flag every unit with a 15-plus-year roof for proactive inspection scheduling — ideally 60 days before the renewal date. The carrier doesn't have to give the homeowner the inspection grace; the homeowner has to invoke the statute proactively.

### For Restoration Crews

**Written PA referral protocols, also by July 1.** Senate Bill 266 — the Burton bill on public-adjuster contracts — takes effect the same day. SB 266 adds $5,000-per-act fines for solicitation of vulnerable adults and unconditional rescission rights. If a restoration crew is recommending a PA at the loss site, get the written referral protocol in place and trained before the deadline. Two bills, one effective date, both touching how service providers interact with policyholders.

## The Carrier Side — What's Happening at the Same Time

Three context items frame why HB 815 lands now:

**Citizens cut personal-lines rates 2.6% effective today, June 1.** First Citizens decrease since 2015. Roughly 60 percent of policyholders see an average 11.5 percent premium drop. The spread between Citizens and the private market just narrowed.

**Heritage Property & Casualty announced up to a 5 percent reduction for Palm Beach County, affecting about 26,000 homes.** Average reported reduction 11.9 percent. Heritage is a top-10 private homeowners carrier in Florida by policy count.

**Three additional carriers were approved to write in Florida last month.** Builder Reciprocal (Texas), Frontline Reciprocal Exchange (Lake Mary, new-business start June 18), and Wingsail (Spinnaker subsidiary). That brings the total to 20 new market entrants and over $850 million of new capital since the 2022-2023 reforms.

Stack those three with HB 815, and the picture for service providers is clear: the South Florida residential property market is moving from "fragile and shrinking" to "stabilizing with more carrier paper to quote against." That means more renewals successfully closed, more roofs kept insured, more service work flowing through the inspection pathway — and more competition for the contractors who don't show up ready.
