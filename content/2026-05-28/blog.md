**Title:** The Last Week of May Is Worth More Than Any Discount You'll Offer in July

**Meta description:** Four days from hurricane season open, South Florida service providers have one last clean window to lock priority-customer agreements. Here's why calendar pressure beats price — and how to use it.

## The Atlantic Is Quiet. Use It.

As of this morning, the National Hurricane Center's 7-day Atlantic outlook shows zero disturbances and zero formation expected. For South Florida roofers, restoration crews, water-mitigation firms, and tarping contractors, that quiet is the most valuable piece of business intelligence on your desk this week.

June 1 is four days away. After that, every conversation you try to have about a priority-response agreement gets harder. Property managers stop returning calls. Owners go into wait-and-see. The first cone-watch event of the season scrambles every mobilization queue in the region, and your standing offer — *"we'll be there in 48 hours"* — turns into the same scramble everyone else is running.

This is your last week to sell on the calendar instead of on price. And the calendar is a much better sales tool than your discount column.

## Why Price Discounting Is the Wrong June Move

There's a reflex in this industry — when the phone slows down, drop 10% and run the email. It doesn't work in late May. Here's why.

The commercial property manager you're trying to sign is not deciding between you and a cheaper competitor. They're deciding between you and **nobody** — which is what they're going to have if they wait until week two of a named storm to start dialing.

The numbers in the trade press this month are consistent: once a tropical system enters the Florida cone, non-priority commercial accounts wait **5 to 14 days** for mobilization, even at firms running surplus capacity. That's not because contractors are gouging. It's because priority customers — the ones who signed in May — come first. Always.

So when you walk into a property manager's office on May 28 with a 10% discount, you are answering a question they aren't asking. The question they are asking is: *"Whose phone do I get to call on day one?"*

## The June Outreach Template That Works

Here's the structure I've seen convert this week. It's pulled from what commercial-trade publications are emphasizing, and it matches the language property managers are already using internally.

**Subject line:** *"Last week to add your property to our priority list before the first cone."*

**Opener:** Name the property manager's nightmare in one sentence. *"You don't want to be the PM explaining a week-two tarping wait to a board while the roof goes black."*

**Ask:** Twenty minutes to sign a no-cost priority agreement with a defined response-time SLA. The agreement doesn't obligate the property to a single dollar of work. It puts them on the call list.

**Close:** Scarcity, but real. *"We have [N] priority slots left this season. Want one?"* If you have ten, say ten. If you have three, say three. Don't fabricate the number — property managers compare notes.

That's the whole email. Three short paragraphs. The reason it converts in late May and stops converting in mid-June is that the calendar is the close. Once June 1 passes, the line *"before the first cone"* loses its weight.

## What to Build Into the SLA

If you're going to lead with response-time as your value, the agreement has to actually define one. Three components separate a real priority agreement from a marketing brochure:

1. **Response-time commitment.** Hours, not days. *"Onsite within 24 hours of a named-storm landfall in [county]."*
2. **Resource reservation.** State that the priority list is capped and identify the cap. This is what makes the slot worth signing for now.
3. **Reciprocal terms.** What the property owes you — typically a defined point of contact, site access during weather windows, and standard payment terms on emergency work.

If you don't have an SLA template, your insurance broker or a commercial real estate attorney can draft one in an afternoon. The investment is worth it. A signed priority agreement at standard rates beats an unsigned discount handshake every time.

## The Owner-Operator Strategy

If you're running an owner-operator crew — one or two trucks, no bench — the math changes slightly. You don't have surplus capacity to allocate, so the priority-list pitch needs to be honest about what you can deliver. Three to five priority slots per crew, season-long, is a defensible number.

Sell those slots first. They become your guaranteed June-through-November revenue floor. After they're signed, take volume work on top — same-week jobs, smaller properties, residential — at standard rates and standard scheduling. You've already locked your priority revenue; the rest is upside.

The owner-operators who get squeezed in October are the ones who took every job in June at a 10% discount and have nothing reserved for the customers who actually need them when the storm hits.

While you're working the priority-agreement outreach, do one other thing this week: position yourself for the Wellington vendor lists.

Three Wellington land-use decisions resolved this spring have set up the largest service-and-warranty pipeline in central Palm Beach County for 2027 — Kolter Group's 579-home Artistry Lakes annexation, Related Ross's Stribling Way height approval covering a hotel and a major commercial footprint, and the Arden annexation moving ~7,000 residents into Village service area.

The vendor approvals for that work are happening **now**, not next May. Roofing, HVAC, plumbing, landscape, pool, pest, painting, and garage-door contractors all route through regional builder-operations approved-vendor lists for new-construction warranty and post-CO service work. Kolter regional procurement is the entry point on the residential side. You will not get on those lists if you wait until the punch-lists land in 2027.
