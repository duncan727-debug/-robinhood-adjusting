# 14 Days to Hurricane Season: Why Your May Calendar Is Worth More Than Your June Pipeline

If you run a service business in Palm Beach, Broward, Miami-Dade, Martin, or St. Lucie, the next 14 days are going to set the tone for your entire summer. Hurricane season opens June 1. The National Hurricane Center activated formal Tropical Weather Outlooks on May 15. And on Thursday, May 21, NOAA holds its official 2026 Atlantic Hurricane Season Outlook press conference from the Aircraft Operations Center in Lakeland.

The moment that announcement hits the wires, the phones start ringing — and they don't stop until somebody names the first storm.

This post is about what to do this week to be on the right side of that surge.

## The Calendar Math

Today is Monday, May 18. Here's how the next two weeks shake out:

- **May 18–20:** Quiet days before NOAA. Use them.
- **May 21:** NOAA outlook drops. First demand spike begins within 24 hours.
- **May 22–31:** Compressed inspection, mitigation, and coverage-review window.
- **June 1:** Season opens. Capacity is now a luxury good.
- **First named storm:** Insurers impose moratoriums on new flood policies and wind-only endorsements. Inspection windows compress further.

You have 14 days. After that, every appointment you accept is one you have to deliver inside an active-season operating environment, with weather risk, supply-chain volatility, and customer panic baked in.

## What Homeowners Are Going to Call About

Based on the briefs I track every morning, here's the demand mix building right now:

**Roof inspections.** Insurance carriers are increasingly conditioning renewals on documented roof condition, especially for roofs 15+ years old. Senate Bill 808 — effective July 1 — expands the universe of who can perform insurance-purpose roof inspections (home inspectors, building code inspectors, general contractors, roofing contractors, professional engineers and architects). The bill also requires insurers to differentiate between low-slope and steep-slope coverage determinations on older roofs. Translation: roof inspections are about to become a more valuable, more standardized product, and homeowners are going to ask about them.

**Wind mitigation upgrades and re-inspections.** Citizens Property Insurance and several private carriers are issuing rate reductions at renewal — Citizens approved an 8.7% average homeowners cut last week, with county-specific reductions of -14.0% in Miami-Dade, -14.1% in Broward, and -11.9% in Palm Beach. That premium relief gives homeowners headroom to invest in mitigation that drops their premiums even further. Smart wind-mit pros are framing this as "redeploy your rate cut into better protection."

**HVAC service and replacement.** The heat dome a few weeks back blew out compressors and capacitors all over the region. The replacement cycle is still working through, and pre-season is when the next wave of marginal units fails. HVAC contractors with parts on the shelf and techs available are charging premium rates right now.

**Wildfire-adjacent water damage and smoke scoping.** Two active fires in our service area — the Max Road Fire in Broward/Everglades (11,339+ acres, 70% contained) and the 172nd Avenue Fire in Miami-Dade/Florida City (300 acres, 50% contained). Florida is in its worst drought in 15 years. Water-mitigation and mold-remediation contractors have a 24–48 hour window after firefighting-water exposure before colonization risks turn a small loss into a total loss. This is rapid-response money.

**Flood policy enrollment.** Once the NHC names a tropical system, flood-policy moratoriums kick in. Independent brokers who can quote and bind in 48 hours are about to be very busy.

## Why Capacity Beats Marketing Right Now

Here's the thing most service businesses get wrong about pre-season: they spend May trying to generate leads. The leads are coming. They don't need help. NOAA is going to generate them for you on Thursday.

What they need is somebody who can actually answer the phone, show up to the property within 48 hours, and put the work on the schedule. The differentiator isn't "we do great work." Everybody says that. The differentiator is "we can be there Wednesday."

Three practical moves for this week:

1. **Audit your May 18–31 schedule.** How many open slots do you actually have? Block out the ones you want to fill before NOAA airs.
2. **Pre-set your post-NOAA capacity.** Decide now what your service window is going to be when call volume doubles. Will you take new clients? Existing only? What's the cutoff?
3. **Build a referral relationship with one adjacent trade.** Roofers should know a wind-mit inspector. HVAC should know a water-mitigation crew. Inspectors should know a public adjuster. When you can't take the job, handing it to a trusted partner keeps the client in your ecosystem.

## The Trust Premium

One more thing worth flagging. Florida's CFO recently announced the arrest of a public adjuster for misappropriating over $700,000 in Hurricane Ian settlement funds. SB 266 — which adds vulnerable-adult protections and clearer PA discipline standards — takes effect July 1.

The reputational dust from cases like that lands on every service provider who knocks on a door wearing a uniform. Homeowners are more skeptical than they were a year ago. The providers who win this season are the ones who lead with documentation: licenses on the truck, written scopes, transparent pricing, signed agreements. Sloppy paperwork is going to cost market share.
