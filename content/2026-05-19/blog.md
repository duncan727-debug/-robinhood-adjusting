## 13 Days to Hurricane Season: The South Florida Service Provider's Playbook for the Last Two Weeks of May

We're 13 days from June 1. If you run a roofing crew, a water-mitigation truck, an HVAC service van, or a property management book in Palm Beach, Broward, or Miami-Dade County, the window you have to set up Q3 and Q4 is closing this week — not on June 1, and definitely not in July when the first system spins up.

I write a daily property and insurance brief for South Florida service providers, and three things landed in this morning's research that, taken together, materially change how the next two weeks should look on your calendar. Here's the operator's read.

---

### 1. The Rainy Season Started Yesterday. The Calls Start Tuesday.

The National Weather Service Miami office now has South Florida in its first full week of rainy season pattern. Forecasts through Friday:

- **Monday (yesterday):** ~50% rain chance — the wettest day this week
- **Tuesday–Friday:** 30–40% daily rain chances
- **Pattern:** Isolated morning showers, scattered afternoon thunderstorms
- **Beach risk:** High rip current threat through at least Tuesday

In isolation, that's a normal South Florida May. The reason this one isn't normal: the ground underneath the rain has been baked dry for six weeks. Broward and Miami-Dade are still inside the active fire perimeter footprint (Max Road Fire — 11,000+ acres at roughly 80% containment; 172nd Avenue Fire — 300 acres at ~50%). Drought-hardened soil doesn't absorb water. It sheds it.

**What that means in practical terms for the calls hitting your dispatch:**

- **Water mitigation:** Expect a first-wave spike Tuesday afternoon through Thursday morning. Yard runoff into garages and first-floor enclosures, foundation seepage in older slab homes, and overwhelmed gutters dumping at the wall line.
- **Roofing:** Deferred inspections you've been pushing through "intermittent showers" weather are about to get disrupted. Pre-call your scheduled customers Monday afternoon to confirm whether you can still get up Tuesday morning before the cells fire.
- **Plumbing:** Sewer-main backups in low-lying neighborhoods where stormwater and sanitary lines share aging infrastructure.
- **HVAC:** First humidity slam of the season — system failures from neglected pre-season service visits will start hitting Wednesday into the weekend.

**The opportunity in this isn't the calls themselves.** It's the question of who you partner with when the calls come faster than you can take them. This is the week to lock in two or three referral partners across complementary trades — the contractors you trust enough to send your overflow to and get reciprocal flow back when their dispatch board lights up. PAs are part of that ecosystem too, and the ones worth having on your call list are the ones who can walk a loss with you before the carrier-side adjuster shows up.

### 2. The Tax Holiday Is Permanently Gone. That's a Win, Not a Loss.

For the last decade, Florida ran two annual disaster-preparedness sales tax holidays — typically a window in late May/early June and a second in August. Generators, tarps, batteries, smoke and CO detectors, tie-down kits, ground anchors, life jackets, fire extinguishers, and a handful of other commonly stocked emergency supplies came off the tax rolls during those windows.

**Those windows are not coming back in 2026.** Legislation signed in August 2025 made the underlying exemptions **permanent**. Every day of the year, those items are tax-free at the register in Florida.

**Items now permanently exempt from Florida sales tax:**
- Portable generators (any wattage)
- Batteries, fire extinguishers, smoke and CO detectors
- Tarps, ground anchors, tie-down kits
- Portable gas/diesel cans, life jackets
- Insect repellent and sunscreen

**Why this matters more than it looks:**

On the procurement side, your stocking runs no longer have to compress into a two-week window. You can build inventory on your cash-flow schedule, not the legislature's. If you stock generators or sell tarps as a service add-on, the permanent exemption changes margin math on items that used to spike in late spring.

On the client-communication side, **anything in your marketing collateral that references "tax holiday dates" is now stale.** Email templates, blog posts, prep checklists, social posts pinned to your business page — if it says "this week only" or "May 24 through June 6," it's signaling out-of-date material to anyone savvy enough to read between the lines. That includes property managers vetting your professionalism before referring you internally.

The repair work is light. Replace "Tax Holiday Dates" headers with "Year-Round Tax Exemption" callouts. Update the dated copy. And — here's the value — that update gives you a credible, non-pitchy reason to drop a "your hurricane prep checklist for 2026 — here's what changed this year" note into your warm client list this week. Educational outreach lands. Sales outreach gets ignored.

### 3. Capacity Is Tightening Faster Than the Industry Is Talking About

This morning's brief flagged that Miami Best Roofing publicly announced a capacity expansion across all three counties focused on **high-velocity wind zone** systems. On its own, one mid-tier contractor expanding isn't a story. The pattern matters: when one operator expands publicly in May, it typically signals that the rest of the local field is tightening capacity quietly.

For property managers and PAs maintaining preferred-contractor lists, **this is the week to call every name on the list and verify availability through June 30.** Don't ask "can you handle a project in June?" Ask "what does your dispatch board actually look like Tuesday-Thursday this week, and what's your earliest install date on a 30-square asphalt re-roof?" The answers will tell you who's still real capacity and who's coasting on reputation.

For contractors, the inverse is true: **this is the week to publish your scheduling cutoff dates.** "Booking through June 14 — call now for July install" is a more credible message in May than it is in August. Customers who are watching capacity tighten in real time take action; customers who think there's slack don't.

### The PA Lens: One More Operational Note for Today

If you serve clients who own homes in flood-eligible zones — and in coastal South Florida that's nearly all of them — **today (May 19) is effectively the last day to bind a new NFIP flood policy and have it active before June 1.** The federal NFIP program has a hard 30-day waiting period from bind to coverage activation. Anyone enrolling June 1 will be uncovered for any named system through June 30.

This is the kind of detail that should be in every property manager's tenant orientation, every realtor's closing checklist, and every contractor's "what we found during the inspection" follow-up. It costs nothing to mention. It separates the operators who actually understand the property stack from the ones who just service it.

### What I'd Do With This Week If I Were You

Compressed checklist for the next five business days:

1. **Confirm dispatch capacity Tuesday-Thursday.** Hold or release slots for emergency water-intrusion calls. Don't get caught flat-footed Wednesday morning.
2. **Update any client-facing material that references the seasonal tax holiday.** Replace with "year-round exemption" framing.
3. **Drop a "what's new in 2026 hurricane prep" educational note to your warm list.** No pitch. Just the tax-holiday change, the NFIP cutoff, and one useful takeaway.
4. **Verify two or three cross-trade referral partners.** Roofers — who's your plumber and your water-mit? Water-mit — who's your roofer and your PA? Map the gaps.
5. **Publish a scheduling cutoff date.** Customers act when they see a real deadline.
