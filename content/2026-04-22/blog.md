## The News Every South Florida Contractor Should Have Seen

Earlier this month, a Miramar public adjuster named Francisco Chaparro-Araus was arrested and charged with fraud, grand theft, and misappropriation of funds. According to the Florida Department of Financial Services, he allegedly diverted more than $600,000 in Hurricane Ian insurance settlements that belonged to his clients. Florida DFS records show his PA license had been suspended — twice — before the arrest.

This story matters if you're a roofer, water mitigation company, plumber, or general contractor in South Florida. Not because it affects your license or your work directly. But because of what happens next: your clients are going to be more careful about who they work with, and that scrutiny extends to every professional in their network — including whoever referred them to their public adjuster.

## Your Referral Is Your Reputation

In South Florida's post-Ian, post-Idalia insurance environment, most property damage claims don't get resolved without someone in the trades recommending a PA. A roofer finds storm damage, finishes the inspection, and the homeowner asks: "What do I do now?" A water mitigation crew finishes a dry-out and the homeowner has a carrier lowballing the scope. The question gets asked all the time: *"Do you know a good PA?"*

That question — and your answer — creates a relationship. When the PA does right by the client, you look good. When the PA does what Chaparro allegedly did, the homeowner doesn't just blame the PA. They remember who sent them there.

The enforcement climate in Florida right now is the hottest it's been in years. DFS is active. The CBS News whistleblower investigation into a Florida insurer diverting reserves to investors is still running. Six people were charged earlier this year in a separate $1.7 million fraudulent damage claims scheme. This is a moment where credentials are being scrutinized — and where a bad referral has real consequences.

## The Clock Is Running on April Claims

There's a second story playing out this week that's more immediately actionable for a specific group of contractors.

On April 7, a Flood Watch covered Miami-Dade, Broward, and Palm Beach counties. A stalled frontal system and deep tropical moisture produced localized flooding across the region. If your crew took dry-out calls in the first two weeks of April, some of those homeowners are hitting critical claim deadlines right now.

Under Florida law:
- Insurers must **acknowledge** a claim within **14 days** of filing
- Insurers must **begin investigation** within **10 days** of proof of loss
- Insurers must **pay or deny** within **90 days** of receiving complete documentation

The April 7 claims that were filed in the first week are entering the acknowledgment and investigation phase this week. This is the window where slow-walked claims, lowball estimates, and improper denials start to surface — and where a PA can still enter the process cleanly, before the homeowner has already accepted a bad outcome.

**If you worked a property in that storm event and the homeowner hasn't mentioned their claim status, it's worth a follow-up.** They may be getting the runaround right now and not know they have options.

## What "Clean Credentials" Actually Looks Like

The Chaparro arrest is going to prompt a lot of contractors to say they'll vet PAs more carefully going forward. Here's what that actually means in practice:

**1. Look them up on DFS.** Every licensed PA in Florida is searchable at the Department of Financial Services website. You can see their license status, any disciplinary history, and suspension records. Chaparro had two prior suspensions — that's findable in under 60 seconds.

**2. Ask for their license number upfront.** A PA who's clean should hand you their license number before you even ask. If they're hesitant or vague about it, that's information.

**3. Ask how they handle the settlement check.** The most common fraud pattern in PA cases — including Chaparro's — involves the PA controlling the distribution of the settlement. Understand the payment flow before you refer anyone.

**4. Ask for references from contractors, not just homeowners.** A PA who has built genuine relationships with roofers, water mitigation companies, and GCs in your market has skin in the game. Those referral relationships are what they're protecting. A PA with no contractor relationships is operating without accountability.
