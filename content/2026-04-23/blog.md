## The Forecast Nobody Should Sleep On

Colorado State University released its April 2026 Atlantic hurricane season outlook this week. The headline: 13 named storms, 6 hurricanes, 2 major hurricanes — a slightly below-normal season, driven by forecast El Niño conditions that tend to increase wind shear across the tropics.

If your first instinct is relief, I understand it. But let me offer a quick history lesson.

Hurricane Ian struck Florida in 2022 during a season that opened with below-average expectations. Wilma — still the most intense Atlantic hurricane ever recorded — made landfall in Broward County in 2005 during a season that started quietly. Andrew hit in 1992.

Below-normal is a statistical category, not a protection. It tells you something about the aggregate probability distribution of the entire Atlantic basin. It tells you nothing about whether a storm with your address on it will form in August. CSU puts the odds of a major hurricane making U.S. landfall somewhere this year at **32%**. That's not a low number.

The more useful question is: what can you do between today and June 1?

---

## The 38-Day Window

June 1 is the official start of the Atlantic hurricane season. Between now and then, the South Florida insurance and property ecosystem is in its most actionable state of the year:

- Homeowners are (slightly) less anxious than they will be in August
- Contractors have marginally more availability than they will after the first named storm
- Conversations about documentation, policy review, and roof condition land better when they're preventative rather than urgent

For service providers — roofers, plumbers, HVAC technicians, general contractors, property managers — this window is business development opportunity, not just prep work.

---

## What "Pre-Loss Documentation" Actually Means for Your Clients

When a hurricane or severe weather event hits and a homeowner files a claim, the insurer's adjuster will reconstruct the pre-loss condition of the property from whatever evidence exists. If that evidence is thin, the carrier has more latitude to attribute damage to pre-existing wear, maintenance neglect, or excluded causes.

Pre-loss documentation closes that gap. It includes:

- **Timestamped photographs** of the roof, exterior walls, windows, doors, HVAC equipment, and any known vulnerabilities — taken before any storm event
- **Roof age records**: the installation date, contractor, permit number, and material type
- **A home inventory**: appliances, electronics, furniture, fixtures — anything that might need to be replaced after a loss
- **Policy review**: knowing what's actually covered, what the deductibles are (hurricane deductibles in Florida are often 2–5% of insured value, not a flat dollar amount), and whether the coverage limit reflects current replacement cost

This is not complicated work. But it almost never gets done proactively. Your clients know they should do it the same way they know they should floss daily.

Here's the practical piece for service providers: **you are already in these properties**. When a roofer does an inspection or a plumber clears a drain, they see conditions the homeowner hasn't looked at in years. That's a position of trust — and a position of knowledge. Knowing how to channel that into a PA referral relationship is one of the more durable business development moves available to trades contractors in South Florida right now.

## The Mold and Water Problem That's Already Here

While we're thinking about June 1, it's worth noting that the claims cycle from April 10's severe weather — a squall line with tornado warnings across Palm Beach, Broward, and Miami-Dade — is already live.

And it's landing in a difficult environment. South Florida has seen more than three times normal rainfall since April 1, and remediation firms are booked to full capacity into summer. That means homeowners with active mold or water damage right now are waiting longer for remediation, paying more when they get it ($2,500–$6,000 for modest jobs; $20,000+ for older single-family homes once you factor in insulation, cabinetry, and subfloor), and facing a claims environment that's actively contentious.

The core dispute is category: **is this a covered water event, or excluded long-term seepage?** That line is where insurers apply the most pressure, and it's where the largest claim reductions happen. An experienced public adjuster can often reframe the evidence, cite the policy language precisely, and recover amounts that would otherwise be written off.

For remediation contractors and plumbers, this matters for a specific reason: you are often the first professional on-site after a water loss. What you document, what you tell the homeowner, and whether you've built a working relationship with a public adjuster can be the difference between your client getting a fair settlement and getting a partial one — and whether they have the budget to pay your invoice in full.

## What New Carriers and Rate Cuts Actually Mean for Claims

Citizens Property Insurance policyholders across South Florida are seeing meaningful rate reductions at spring renewal — Miami-Dade and Broward down roughly 14%, Palm Beach down nearly 12%. That's genuinely good news for affordability.

But rate cuts and claim-settlement behavior are different things. Carriers entering the Florida market at competitive rates — and 17 new insurers have entered since the reform package passed — have every incentive to underwrite claims strictly. Expect more denials citing pre-existing damage, wear and tear, and wind-mitigation form discrepancies as these new carrier relationships mature into their first loss cycles.

The roughly 1.3 million policies that have moved from Citizens to private carriers since 2023 represent a population that is, in many cases, on their first claim with a carrier they don't know, under a policy they didn't closely read. That's a friction point — and it's exactly where a PA relationship (whether initiated by the homeowner directly or through a referral from their contractor or plumber) adds the most value.

## The Practical Ask

If you're a service provider working in South Florida, here's what the next 38 days look like in practice:

1. **Bring up documentation on your next 5 property visits.** You don't need a script. "Do you have photos of your roof from before this season?" is enough to start the conversation.
2. **Know who your public adjuster contact is.** Not for every job — but for the jobs where you can see the claim coming. The referral is worth something to your client and, over time, to your business.
3. **Note roof age when you're up there.** SB 808 (effective July 1) expands who can conduct insurer-accepted roof inspections — and with it, inspection quality variance is going to increase. Your documentation may be the most reliable record of a roof's actual condition.

The market is complex. The forecast is uncertain. But the window is clear, and it's 38 days wide.
