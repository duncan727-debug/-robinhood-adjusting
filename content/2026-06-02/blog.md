# The December 31 Condo Deadline Is a Six-Month Work Order for Eight South Florida Trades

If you run a trade business in Palm Beach, Broward, or Miami-Dade County, the most important date on your pipeline board this year isn't hurricane season's peak. It isn't the end of the federal fiscal year. It's **December 31, 2026** — the hard deadline for Florida's condominium milestone inspection and Structural Integrity Reserve Study (SIRS) requirements.

Two-hundred-twelve days from today, every residential condo and cooperative building that's three or more habitable stories tall and reaches the statutory age threshold in 2026 must have both reports complete. No extensions. Penalties start at $500 per day. And in the worst case, unsafe-building determinations can trigger vacate orders.

For service providers, that's not a regulatory headline. That's six months of mandatory, engineer-specified repair work landing on thousands of South Florida buildings — and the question of who gets the call when the report lands.

## What the Law Actually Requires

Florida statute s. 553.899 requires a **milestone inspection** for residential condominium and cooperative buildings three or more habitable stories in height. The age threshold is generally **30 years**, but it drops to **25 years in coastal areas** — which includes virtually all of Miami-Dade, Broward, and Palm Beach County east of I-95.

A parallel statute, enacted in the wake of the Surfside collapse, mandates a **Structural Integrity Reserve Study (SIRS)** for any building required to perform a milestone inspection. The SIRS specifically covers eight building components:

- Roof
- Load-bearing walls
- Primary structural members
- Fire protection systems
- Plumbing
- Electrical systems
- Waterproofing and exterior painting
- Windows and exterior doors

Reserves for these eight components **cannot be waived** for budgets adopted after December 31, 2024. That's the structural change. Boards that historically rolled deferred maintenance forward year after year no longer have that option. The funding has to be there. Which means the work has to get scheduled.

## Why This Is a Service-Provider Story, Not a Homeowner Story

Most coverage of milestone inspections speaks to unit owners and HOA boards. That's a one-way trade — the buyer is captive, the timeline is fixed, the work is mandated. The interesting position is the *seller* side of that transaction.

Here's the math from where I sit. South Florida has thousands of condo buildings constructed between 1991 and 2001 — the Surfside-era cohort hitting 25-year coastal threshold this year and next. A milestone inspection identifies repair scope. The SIRS prices it and forces the reserves. The board then has to put the work out for bid.

If you operate a roofing, waterproofing, plumbing, electrical, structural, fire-protection, window-installation, or general-contracting business in this corridor, you are looking at the largest scheduled work-order pipeline of the decade. Not "marketing-funnel maybe." Engineer-stamped, statutorily-required, calendar-dated.

The opening is *now*, not Q4. Boards that wait until October to start interviewing trades will be quoting against backed-up calendars. The boards talking to contractors in June and July are the ones who'll have crews on site when the engineer's report comes back.

## Five Plays That Are Working in This Window

**1. Become the board's pre-inspection trade reference.** When the engineer or special inspector visits a building, they identify scope but rarely refer trades — that's outside their lane. Boards that already have a trusted roofer or plumber on file will route work to them directly. Get on those phone trees now, before the reports start circulating.

**2. Lead with material-escalator language in every bid.** Roof material costs are running 15–25% above 2024 baselines. Section 232 tariffs at 50% on steel and aluminum are now hitting 400-plus derivative categories including drip edge, flashing, and prefabricated panels. Aluminum mill shapes are up 33% year-over-year; steel mill products up 20.7%. A bid you submitted in March without escalator language is a money-loser if installation slips to October. Build the language in. Be transparent with the board about why.

**3. Pre-schedule mobilization windows.** The bottleneck in Q4 won't be capital — boards will have the reserves funded by statute. The bottleneck will be crew availability. Offering a board a guaranteed September or October mobilization window in exchange for an earlier deposit will look conservative to them and lock your calendar for you. Both sides win.

**4. Bundle the wind-mit angle on roof work.** The new OIR-B1-1802 wind-mitigation form took effect April 1, 2026 — it credits opening protection more generously than the prior version. On any condo roof replacement, the unit owners can re-pull wind-mit inspections and see premium drops of $800–$1,500/year on $4,500-baseline policies. That's a board-meeting talking point that makes your bid easier to approve.

**5. Don't sleep on the CILB renewal.** Florida certified-contractor licenses (general, building, residential, roofing, mechanical, plumbing) renew on August 31, 2026 for even-year licenses — 90 days from today. Required CE is 14 hours, including 1 hour each of workplace safety, workers' comp, and business practices. If you're a GC working a sub-list, this is the right week to confirm every roofing, mechanical, and plumbing sub on file has CE booked and renewal payment queued. A non-renewed license at midnight August 31 is an instant unlicensed-contractor exposure problem — bonds, insurance, advertising claims, permit-pulling rights all evaporate.

## Where the Public Adjuster Fits In

A fair question for a tradesman reading this: why is a PA writing it?

Because milestone work and insured-loss work are about to bleed into each other in ways most operators haven't priced in.

When an inspection identifies waterproofing failure on a 28-year-old building, two things can be true at once: it's a reserve-funded repair, *and* it may also be evidence of a prior covered loss that was never properly adjusted. Roofs that get torn off for replacement frequently reveal latent storm damage — Idalia, Ian-band rain bands, even older Wilma residual. The unit-owner master policy or the association policy may owe money the board has never claimed.

I work the claims side of that equation. The trades work the build side. When we know each other, the homeowner — or board — gets a better outcome and we both get the next call. Referral-loop simple.

## The Calendar Through Year-End

- **June–July:** Engineers begin landing milestone reports on early-threshold buildings. Boards interview trades.
- **August 31:** CILB certified-license renewal deadline. Get CE done well before.
- **September–November:** Hurricane-season peak. Schedules will compress on weather days. Plan accordingly.
- **December 31:** Hard deadline. Inspections and SIRS complete. Reserves must be funded.

That's seven months from now to mobilize on what is, conservatively, the largest mandated repair cycle South Florida condos have ever scheduled simultaneously.

## What to Do This Week

If you run a trade business in one of the eight SIRS-affected categories, three concrete steps this week:

1. **Pull a list of every 25–30+ year condo building in your service radius.** Property appraiser records. Free. Half a day's work for a virtual assistant.
2. **Identify property managers, board presidents, and HOA management companies covering those buildings.** Most are public.
3. **Send one note. Soft. Not a pitch.** Something like: *"Saw your building falls inside the 2026 milestone cycle. If your engineer's report flags scope in [my trade], I keep a few late-summer slots open for boards we've worked with before. Happy to be a reference even if you go elsewhere — the trade community in this corridor isn't large and I'd rather know your board than not."*

That's it. That's the play.

The boards are about to spend the money. The work is statutorily required. The only open variable is who they hand the contract to. Be in the conversation.
