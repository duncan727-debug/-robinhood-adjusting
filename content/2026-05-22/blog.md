# Below-Normal Hurricane Season, Above-Normal Opportunity: The June Playbook for South Florida Contractors

NOAA's Climate Prediction Center released the official 2026 Atlantic Hurricane Season outlook on May 21, and the headline is a lower one than most forecasters were modeling two weeks ago: **8 to 14 named storms, 3 to 6 hurricanes, and 1 to 3 majors**. NOAA puts the probability of a below-normal season at 55%, with a developing — possibly "super" — El Niño doing most of the heavy lifting by suppressing tropical Atlantic organization.

If you run a service business in Palm Beach, Martin, or St. Lucie County, the temptation is to read that headline, plan for a slow summer, and tighten the belt. That's the wrong read. The right read is that the 2026 season has just handed you a 90-day window to reposition — and the contractors who use it will end the year stronger than the ones chasing storm calls.

Here's what's actually happening underneath the headline, and what to do about it before June 1.

## What "Below-Normal" Really Means (And What It Doesn't)

NOAA's outlook is a **probabilistic call about basin-wide activity**, not a landfall forecast. The agency was explicit about that yesterday. A below-normal season does not lower the per-storm risk to any individual property in South Florida.

Two reminders from history:

- **1992** was officially below-normal. It produced Hurricane Andrew.
- **2018** was officially near-normal. It produced Michael — a Category 5 at landfall in the Panhandle.

The headline number tells you how many storms might form. It tells you almost nothing about whether one of them will park itself over Wellington or Stuart in September. Anyone selling "quiet year" to a homeowner is setting themselves up — and their customer — for a bad October.

That's the messaging piece. Now the business piece.

## The Real Risk to Your Pipeline Is Margin, Not Volume

Quiet seasons don't just reduce storm-driven jobs. They compress prices. Mitigation crews who normally bill emergency rates settle for scheduled rates. Roofers who counted on insurance-paid replacements end up bidding cash-pay re-roofs against three competitors. A/C contractors who'd normally be triaging post-storm outages spend the summer fighting for tune-up volume against the big regional outfits.

In other words: a below-normal season pushes everyone back into the **maintenance and inspection economy**. The contractors who win in that economy are the ones who **own the calendar** — meaning they're scheduled into homeowner attention windows before competitors are.

The June 1 hurricane-prep window is the single biggest homeowner attention window of the year in South Florida. Right now, it's wide open.

## Why This June Is Different: Three Structural Tailwinds

This isn't a normal hurricane-prep cycle. Three things have shifted underneath the news:

### 1. Twenty new homeowners carriers are now writing in Florida

The post-reform new-entrant count just got revised up from 17 to 20, with more than $850M in fresh capital. Florida-domiciled personal-property insurers swung from a $132M underwriting loss two years ago to nearly $1 billion in underwriting gains last reporting period. Capacity is no longer the binding constraint — **pricing discipline and territorial appetite are**.

What that means on the ground: new carriers tend to write conservatively in HVHZ (Miami-Dade, Broward) and aggressively in inland PBC, Martin, and St. Lucie. Wellington, Palm Beach Gardens, Jupiter, Port St. Lucie — those zip codes are getting shopped right now.

### 2. The 30-day HO rate-filing average is officially negative

For the first time in this cycle, the rolling average of Florida homeowners insurance rate filings is **−2.3%**. One year ago it was +0.5%. Since January 2024, 39 carriers have filed for decreases and 48 have filed flat. The default homeowner expectation for a 2026 renewal is no longer "how much is it going up?" — it's "should I be paying less?"

### 3. Wind-mitigation paperwork is quietly becoming obsolete

Miami-Dade just updated **RAS 118, 119, 120, and 127** to performance-based standards. The OIR-B1-1802 wind-mitigation form is also under revision. A wind-mit inspection from 2020 is, increasingly, a paper credential — not a current compliance statement. Roof systems that passed five years ago may not comply under the new performance reads, especially older tile underlayments.

Stack those three together and you get a homeowner who:

- Has more carrier options than they did last June.
- Expects flat or lower premiums.
- Is sitting on inspection paperwork that may no longer hold up at renewal.

That's not a "quiet summer." That's a service-provider opportunity hiding in plain sight.

## The June Playbook: Three Plays for Three Trades

### Roofers

Don't lead with "we re-roof." Lead with **"we update your wind-mitigation paperwork."** A current Miami-Dade Notice of Acceptance (NOA) on the roof system, fresh photos of underlayment and uplift specs, and a clean OIR-B1-1802 in the homeowner's hand is a $300–$600 service call that converts to a six-figure re-roof at a meaningfully higher rate when a deficiency is found. The deficiency rate on roofs older than seven years in PBC is well into the double digits.

**Booking script:** "With the new Miami-Dade performance-based code and a wind-mit form revision in process, the inspection you had five years ago may not protect your premium at renewal. We'll re-photograph and re-document for the new standard — and if your roof needs work, you'll know before your carrier does."

### Water Mitigation

Pre-season moisture mapping. Run a thermal-imaging and moisture-meter pass on the highest-risk envelope areas — flat-roof transitions, sliding-door tracks, HVAC plenum penetrations, second-story exterior wall returns — before the first June storm. Sell it as a **prep service**, not a leak detection. Price it under the typical post-loss minimum so it lands as a no-brainer for property managers running multi-unit portfolios.

The play here is partnership volume, not single-home calls. Two or three property-management groups in PBC or condo associations under the milestone-inspection deadline can fill your June calendar.

### A/C and Mechanical

Pre-season coil cleaning + capacitor/contactor check is a commodity. The differentiator is **documentation**. Provide a single-page condition report with model, serial, refrigerant charge, and current efficiency — formatted so the homeowner can hand it to their carrier or their adjuster if a covered loss happens later in the season. You become the contractor of record on every future claim that touches the system. That's an enormous downstream asset.

## What to Do This Week

If you take nothing else from this brief, take these three:

1. **Decide on your June offer by Tuesday.** Inspection, moisture map, mechanical condition report — pick one and package it. If you don't define it, your competitors will define their version first.
2. **Build a list of 25 target properties or relationships.** Property managers, HOAs, condo associations near milestone inspection deadlines, and individual high-value homes you've already touched. Don't cold-prospect this; warm-list it.
3. **Have a referral partner.** Inspection findings convert better when the homeowner has a trusted resource for the carrier side of the conversation. That can be a public adjuster, an independent agent, or both — but build the relationship before you need it, not after.

The contractors who treat June as inspection season — not as a holding pattern before the first storm — will own the back half of 2026.
