**Meta Description:** Florida public adjuster arrested for $703K theft. What service providers need to know about vetting adjusters and protecting their reputation in claims work.

## The Case That Proves Trust Matters

On March 30, 2026, Florida's Chief Financial Officer announced the arrest of public adjuster Francisco Javier Chaparro-Araus on charges of grand theft, organized fraud, and financial exploitation of elderly persons. The accused allegedly stole over $703,000 in Hurricane Ian claim funds—money that belonged to homeowners who trusted him to represent their interests.

This isn't an abstract regulatory story. For contractors, roofers, plumbers, property managers, and other service providers, this case is a red flag attached to a fundamental business truth: **the professionals you partner with directly affect your reputation, your paycheck, and your legal exposure.**

If you've worked with a fraudulent adjuster—or worse, been burned by one—you know firsthand how quickly trust evaporates. One bad claim experience can damage relationships with clients, complicate future work, and invite scrutiny from insurers and attorneys.

---

## The Ripple Effect: How One Bad Actor Damages Everyone

When a public adjuster commits fraud, the damage spreads far beyond the immediate victims.

**Impact on Service Providers:**
- A tainted adjuster creates a tainted claim. If the adjuster's conduct is questioned, your invoices and work quality may come under the same microscope.
- Clients lose faith in the entire recovery process. If they were defrauded once, they're skeptical of everyone—including you.
- Legal complications multiply. You may be asked to testify, produce records, or defend your work if the claim becomes part of an investigation.

**Impact on the Industry:**
- Fraud cases invite regulatory scrutiny. Each case leads to stricter vetting rules, more paperwork, and tougher compliance requirements for honest adjusters.
- Public trust erodes. Homeowners become hesitant to file claims, insurers become more defensive, and the entire recovery ecosystem stalls.

In a profession built entirely on trust, one fraudulent adjuster can poison relationships for months or years.

## How to Vet a Public Adjuster: A Service Provider's Checklist

If you're choosing an adjuster to partner with—or if one approaches you—here's how to separate the professionals from the risky bets.

### 1. **Verify Their Credentials**
- Check their public adjuster license with the Florida Department of Financial Services (DFS).
- Confirm their license is active and in good standing.
- Search for any disciplinary history or complaints.
- Ask for proof of errors & omissions (E&O) insurance—legitimate adjusters carry it.

### 2. **Ask About Their Track Record**
- How long have they been adjusting claims?
- How many claims have they closed in the past two years?
- What's their average settlement rate and timeline?
- Can they provide references from contractors, property managers, or other service partners you know?

### 3. **Assess Their Business Practices**
- Do they have a physical office and a functioning website?
- Are they accessible—do they respond to calls and emails promptly?
- How transparent are they about their fee structure?
- Do they provide written engagement agreements with clear terms?

### 4. **Watch for Red Flags**
- Pressure to sign agreements quickly or without review.
- Reluctance to provide references or credentials.
- Claims of guaranteed settlements ("We always get 100% of the value").
- Working primarily with elderly clients or vulnerable populations.
- Handling claim funds directly instead of routing them through proper channels.

### 5. **Check for Industry Connections**
- Are they known and respected within the local contractor, restoration, and property management networks?
- Do they have relationships with reputable service providers, attorneys, and other professionals?
- Do other service providers actively recommend them?

Industry reputation matters because it's harder to maintain a fraud operation when you're embedded in a tight professional community. Isolated adjusters are easier targets for scrutiny.
