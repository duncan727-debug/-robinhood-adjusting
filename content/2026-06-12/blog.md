## They Paid $90,000 for Bonds That Never Existed

Two South Florida construction businesses — a roofing company and a construction firm — just learned the hardest lesson in risk management: paying for coverage and having coverage are not the same thing.

This week, a licensed property-casualty agent in Palm Beach Gardens — Checree Bryant, 51, of Innovative Bonding Services — was arrested and charged with embezzlement, fraud, and larceny. According to the Florida Department of Financial Services affidavit, she collected more than $90,000 from the two firms for three contractor surety bonds and never remitted the premiums to the surety company, Old Republic Surety Co. Investigators allege she used the funds personally and earned roughly $30,000 in commission along the way. Hearings are set for July 5 and July 30 in Palm Beach County Circuit Court.

The contractors believed they were bonded. They were not.

## Why This Should Worry Every Trade Business Owner

If you run a roofing company, a GC operation, a plumbing or HVAC shop, or any business that carries bonds and certificates of insurance, this story isn't a curiosity — it's a stress test of your own paperwork.

Think through what happens if a bonded job fails while the bond doesn't actually exist:

- **The exposure lands on you.** The whole point of a surety bond is to back your performance or payment obligations. If the bond was never placed, there's no surety standing behind the job — just your company's balance sheet.
- **Your licensing can be implicated.** Many Florida contractor license categories and many municipal and county jobs require active bonding. Operating unbonded — even unknowingly — can put your license status and your eligibility for public work at risk.
- **Your contracts may be in breach.** Most commercial contracts and many GC subcontract agreements require you to maintain bonding and insurance. "My agent stole the premium" is an explanation, not a defense, when the owner or GC asks for proof of coverage.

Here's the detail from this case that should change how you verify: **the agent's license still showed as valid this week.** A standard license lookup would have come back clean. The red flag was buried one level deeper — in the carrier appointments. Her appointment with Old Republic was inactive. That mismatch was the tell.

## The 15-Minute Verification Routine

You don't need a lawyer or a consultant for this. You need a phone, a list of your active bonds and policies, and 15 minutes.

### Step 1: Call the Surety Carrier Directly

Pull every active bond your business carries. For each one, call the surety company — not your agent — and confirm two things:

1. The bond is currently in force.
2. The premium was actually received.

Every major surety has a verification line or can route you to one. If the carrier has no record of your bond, you have a problem that gets worse every day you don't know about it.

### Step 2: Check Your Agent's Appointments, Not Just the License

Go to the Florida DFS licensee search at **licenseesearch.fldfs.com** and look up your agent. Don't stop at "license: valid." Click into the **carrier appointments** and confirm your agent holds an *active appointment* with the specific carrier your bond or policy is written through.

An agent with a valid license but an inactive appointment with "your" carrier cannot legitimately be placing business there. That's exactly the pattern in the Palm Beach Gardens case.

### Step 3: Change How You Buy Going Forward

On any new bond or policy, ask for confirmation **directly from the carrier** — a confirmation letter, a rider, or carrier-issued documentation — not just an agent-prepared certificate. Legitimate agents won't blink at this request. The ones who push back are telling you something.

### Step 4: Extend the Same Logic to Your COIs

Certificates of insurance are even easier to fake or let quietly lapse than bonds. If your business depends on a COI being real — and if you work for GCs, property managers, or HOAs, it does — periodically verify your own coverage with the carrier and encourage the businesses you work with to do the same. A culture of verification protects everyone in the chain.

## Why This Week, Specifically

Two calendar items make mid-June the right moment to run this checklist rather than filing it under "someday":

**1. The first disturbance of hurricane season is on the map.** The National Hurricane Center is tracking the season's first Atlantic-basin system — a Bay of Campeche low with only a 10% formation chance and no Florida threat. The system itself doesn't matter. The calendar does. Once the first named storm forms, everything accelerates: material lead times stretch, customer calls spike, and nobody has 15 minutes for paperwork. Your bonding, insurance, and licensing need to be verified *before* the season gets loud — because post-storm work is exactly when a bonded job is most likely to be scrutinized.

**2. The CE deadline is 11 weeks out.** Florida's construction-trades continuing-education deadline is **August 31, 2026**. While you're verifying bonds and appointments, check your CE hours. An expired license during the busiest claims season of the year is a self-inflicted wound.

While you're at it, push **pre-storm inspection and documentation packages** to your maintenance customers now. Dated roof and exterior condition reports taken before the first named storm are dramatically more valuable at claim time than anything taken after — and they keep your crews productive in the shoulder weeks.

## The Bigger Picture: Verification Is a Competitive Advantage

There's a positioning angle here that most contractors miss. The businesses that survive South Florida's claims environment long-term are the ones that treat documentation and verification as standard operating procedure, not paperwork drudgery.

When a GC, property manager, or public adjuster is deciding which roofer or restoration contractor to bring onto a loss, the ones with verified bonds, current COIs, active appointments, and clean licensing get the call. The ones with "I think my agent handled that" don't.

This case is a 15-minute reminder to be in the first group.
