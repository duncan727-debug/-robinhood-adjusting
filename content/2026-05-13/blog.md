**How to Choose an Adjuster You Can Actually Trust — And Why It Matters More Than Ever**

*Meta Description: After a $703K fraud case, here's how to vet a public adjuster, spot red flags, and protect your clients' claims. Plus: the 19-day pre-season advisory checklist.*

## The Wake-Up Call

This week, Florida's Chief Financial Officer announced a bombshell: Francisco Javier Chaparro-Araus, a public adjuster, had been arrested for misappropriating over $703,000 in Hurricane Ian insurance settlement funds. The details are damning.

He forged victim names on settlement checks. He concealed payments from 13 homeowners. When victims called asking where their money was, he ghosted them.

This isn't a one-off incident. It's a symptom of a larger industry challenge: **trust is broken, and it's affecting everyone in the claims ecosystem.**

If you're a contractor referring clients to adjusters, a property manager coordinating claims, or a business owner relying on an adjuster to recover after property damage, this case should make you pause. Your client relationships depend on working with someone who operates with integrity. One bad adjuster can tank your reputation.

## Why This Matters for Your Business

Let's be direct: **your clients are judging you based on who you recommend and partner with.**

When a homeowner or property owner gets referred to an adjuster through your firm, they're trusting you with their claim. They're expecting professionalism, transparency, and results. If that adjuster disappears, forges documents, or hides payments, your client doesn't blame the adjuster — they blame you for the referral.

This is especially critical for contractors, property managers, and insurance professionals in South Florida. We're 19 days away from hurricane season. Property owners are about to file a lot of claims. If those claims go sideways because of a bad adjuster, the ripple effects hit your network, your reputation, and your bottom line.

## Red Flags: What NOT to Do

Before referring a client to an adjuster or bringing one onto your team, watch for these warning signs:

**Vague Fee Structures**
A legitimate adjuster will clearly explain their fee upfront — typically a percentage of the settlement (10–20%, depending on claim complexity). If an adjuster is cagey about fees, uses complicated terms, or promises "flexible arrangements," keep walking.

**Lack of Documentation**
Every interaction should be documented: written agreements, fee disclosures, communication logs, settlement records. If an adjuster prefers verbal agreements or resists putting things in writing, that's a red flag.

**No Published Credentials or References**
A trustworthy adjuster will have verifiable licensing, professional references from contractors or property managers, case studies, and testimonials. If you can't find any of that, you don't know who you're working with.

**Promises of Inflated Settlements**
An adjuster who guarantees you a settlement 2x or 3x what the initial estimate claims is likely cutting corners or misleading clients. Honest adjusters work with insurers and contractors to get fair settlements — not fantasy numbers.

**Pressure to Rush**
Legitimate claims take time. If an adjuster is pushing you to sign immediately, skip documentation, or move faster than makes sense, they're prioritizing speed over accuracy. That's not in your clients' interests.

**Isolated Communication**
Does the adjuster avoid having your contractor, property manager, or broker in the conversation? Do they prefer one-on-one calls? That's a power-play setup. A transparent adjuster welcomes team communication and collaboration.

## Questions to Ask Before Partnering

Before bringing an adjuster into your referral network or recommending one to a client, vet them properly:

1. **What's your licensing status and history?** (Verify through Florida DFS)
2. **What's your typical fee structure, and how is it disclosed to clients in writing?**
3. **Can you provide references from contractors, property managers, or brokers I can contact?**
4. **How do you communicate with clients, contractors, and insurers? Is everything documented?**
5. **Have you ever had a complaint or disciplinary action?** (Check public records)
6. **What's your process for ensuring client funds are properly handled?** (Listen for escrow accounts, bank transparency, regular accounting)
7. **How do you handle disputes or client concerns?** (Professional adjusters have dispute resolution processes)

## The Pre-Season Advisory Opportunity

Here's the silver lining: with 19 days until hurricane season officially begins, property owners and businesses are actively seeking advisory services. This is your window to position your firm as a trusted, proactive partner.

**The Pre-Season Checklist for Your Clients:**

- [ ] **Property Inspections** — Roof condition, structural integrity, drainage systems
- [ ] **Wind Mitigation Upgrades** — Roof-to-wall connections, reinforced openings (SB 808 takes effect July 1)
- [ ] **Flood Insurance Verification** — Standard homeowners policies exclude flood damage; separate policies required
- [ ] **Insurance Policy Review** — Coverage limits, deductibles, gap analysis
- [ ] **Emergency Planning** — Evacuation routes, vehicle fuel reserves, emergency supplies, contents inventory
- [ ] **Wildfire Exposure Assessment** — Landscaping defensibility, evacuation route clarity, secondary property risks

When you help clients prepare, you're reducing claim frequency and building loyalty. And when claims do happen, you're positioned as the consultant they trust.

## What Transparency Looks Like

A trustworthy adjuster:

- ✓ Puts all fees and terms in writing **before** engaging
- ✓ Communicates regularly with clients, contractors, and brokers
- ✓ Documents every step of the claim process
- ✓ Explains settlement numbers and reasoning clearly
- ✓ Responds promptly to questions and concerns
- ✓ Operates with licensed professionals and verifiable credentials
- ✓ Prioritizes fiduciary accountability (your clients' interests first)
- ✓ Welcomes team involvement and collaboration
