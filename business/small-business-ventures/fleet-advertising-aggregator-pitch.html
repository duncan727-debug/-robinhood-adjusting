The Real Market Gap

What exists today

  • Carvertise / Wrapify: Brokers rideshare and personal-vehicle drivers (Uber/Lyft/Grubhub). Not commercial fleets.
  • Local wrap shops (CRD, GNS, Evolutions, Florida Car Wrap): Production houses — they sell the wrap, not the media spend behind it.
  • OOH brokers (AdQuick, Adomni): Sell billboards and digital screens, not truck-side inventory at the local level.
  • Direct sales (TruckSkin, etc.): One-off brand → one-off fleet matches; no aggregation, no measurement layer.

What's missing

  • Local-market aggregation of 10–60-truck commercial fleets (lawn, pool, HVAC, pest, delivery, junk-haul)
  • Sold like media with impressions, route data, and geo-targeting — not like a sign job
  • Bundled CapEx handling: advertiser pays for wrap amortized over 6-month minimum campaign
  • Multi-fleet campaign delivery: one buy across HVAC fleet + lawn fleet + pool fleet for category-isolated reach
Why no one's done it: Wrap CapEx is real, B2B fleet sales is slow, and OOH measurement is fragmented. Each is solvable individually — but solving all three at the same time has been the moat.

The Wellington / PBC Wedge

Unfair distribution advantages

  • Robin Hood Adjusting newsletter: Existing audience of homeowners, providers, real-estate pros = built-in advertiser demand
  • Provider directory: 62 PBC service businesses already listed = built-in fleet supply conversations
  • HOA + chamber + BNI relationships: Already opened threads with Wellington Expo (Garvey), Chamber Connections, BNI Pinnacle
  • Wellington / 33414 density: Equestrian-belt traffic patterns concentrate impressions on high-value eyeballs

Pilot-friendly market characteristics

  • 500+ mid-size commercial fleets in tri-county (lawn, pool, HVAC, pest, pool, delivery, junk)
  • Concentrated advertiser categories: Real estate, home services, dental, PI law, restaurants, auto dealers — all already-heavy local-ad spenders
  • Low competitive intensity: No regional fleet-media aggregator exists between Carvertise (national rideshare) and one-off wrap shops
  • Walkable proof: Duncan can drive every campaign route within 30 min — verify impressions, photograph deployment, debug in real time

Why This One Scales (vs. Service Ventures)

What service ventures cap at

  • Yard patrol / pressure washing / storm debris: Operator-labor bound. Each route adds linear cost.
  • Geographic radius constrained: Drive time defines TAM
  • Scale = more trucks, more operators, more keys
  • Exit value: 1.5–3× EBITDA at sale

Why fleet ad aggregation breaks the cap

  • Software + relationship moat — adding the 50th fleet costs less than adding the 5th
  • Geographic expansion is repeatable — same playbook works in Tampa, Orlando, Atlanta, Charlotte
  • Scale = same headcount, more inventory — operating leverage compounds
  • Exit value: 5–10× EBITDA at scale (ad-tech / OOH multiples)
Bottom line: A successful pressure-washing business at Year 3 = ~$150K owner net, route-bound. A successful fleet-ad aggregator at Year 3 = ~$735K owner net, geographically expandable, with a 5–10× exit multiple at sale. That's why the local-first prove-out matters: get to Day 120 cheap, then the scale path becomes a math problem instead of a hope problem.
