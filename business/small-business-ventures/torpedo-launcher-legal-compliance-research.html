Section 1 — Trademark (USPTO)

What changed January 18, 2025 — important

USPTO replaced the old two-track system (TEAS Plus $250/class + TEAS Standard $350/class) with a single application form plus surcharges. This means most older online guides are out of date — use 2025-2026 sources only. Source: Stinson LLP – USPTO's New Trademark Fees and WTR – Understanding the new 2025 US trademark filing system.

Surcharge if free-text description not in ID Manual +$200 / class same
Surcharge per additional 1,000 characters in goods/services description (over 1,000) +$200 same

Our scenario (single mark, single class, pre-approved description): $250 total at filing. Plus ~$100-150 later when we file the Statement of Use after the product is actually selling.

What class are we in

Toys fall under International Class 28 (toys, games, sporting goods). The Trademark ID Manual has many pre-approved descriptions in Class 28 — including ones for water toys and projectile launchers. Action item for Smith: before filing, search the ID Manual for the most-specific pre-approved description that fits our product. Using the Manual saves $200.

Pro se filing (DIY) — feasible?

Yes. USPTO explicitly accepts self-represented filers. What Smith does:

  1. TESS+ search (USPTO's trademark search tool at tmsearch.uspto.gov) — confirm chosen brand name has no confusingly-similar live marks in Class 28 (and possibly Class 25 / 41 for crossover risk).
  2. Common-law search — Google + state-business-name databases for unregistered use that could create rights.
  3. Domain check — already done (we have velodart, plumedive, glidewerks .com verified available).
  4. Specimen prep — at filing we need a "specimen" showing the mark in commerce; that's the prototype + Kickstarter page or product photo.
  5. Drawing — a black-and-white text logo or a designed mark file (we can file the word mark first, design mark later if/when logo finalized).

Where pro se gets risky: office actions. If a USPTO examiner pushes back ("your description is too broad," "your specimen doesn't show use in commerce"), responding correctly matters. Compromise approach: I prep the filing 100% — Duncan signs and submits — and if any office action comes back, we evaluate whether to respond pro se or hire a trademark attorney at that point (typical office-action response: $500-1,200 flat fee).

Timeline to registration

Filing → first examiner review: ~4-6 months Publication for opposition (30 days): + ~2 months Notice of Allowance: + ~2 months Statement of Use (after first commerce): + 6 months after Allowance Total to actual registration: 8-14 months typical, longer if any office action

Important: filing date establishes priority. Even before registration completes, we can use the ™ symbol and claim rights against later confusingly-similar filers. We do NOT use the ® symbol until registration completes.

Filing process (verified 2026-05-23 from Sunbiz LLC eFile portal)

  1. Name search at search.sunbiz.org — confirm chosen LLC name is "distinguishable" from existing FL entities. Name must end in "LLC", "L.L.C.", or "Limited Liability Company."
  2. File Articles of Organization online — Sunbiz e-file portal accepts credit card, processing 7-10 days typical, 3 weeks for mail.
  3. Receive Document Number — once filed, FL issues a Document Number; this is the entity ID we use for tax + bank purposes.
  4. EIN from IRS — separate step (free, online, immediate at irs.gov/ein). Single-member LLC defaults to disregarded entity for federal tax (passes through to Duncan's 1040 Schedule C unless we elect S-corp later).
  5. Operating Agreement — NOT required by Florida law and NOT filed with the state, but strongly recommended for legal clarity and bank account opening. I can draft a single-member-LLC template based on Florida Statutes Chapter 605.
  6. Florida Business Tax — register with floridarevenue.com for any applicable sales tax certificate (free).
  7. Local BTR — Wellington Village Business Tax Receipt, around $35 (verify with Wellington Village BTR office before filing).

Single-member-LLC tax treatment

Default: disregarded entity. Income/loss flows to owner's personal 1040. No separate federal tax return for the LLC. Optional later: elect S-corp treatment (Form 2553) if revenue grows enough that the self-employment-tax savings outweigh the added compliance cost. Generally not worth it until net profit ~$50K+/year. Decision can be made any time later; not Phase 1.

Registered agent — DIY vs paid service

Duncan can be his own registered agent at his Wellington address — saves $50-150/year that paid services charge. Trade-off: his address becomes public record on Sunbiz. For a one-product Kickstarter LLC this is normally fine. If preferred private, use Northwest Registered Agent ($125/yr) or similar.

Section 3 — CPSC + ASTM F963 toy compliance

This is the biggest compliance lift of the venture. A pool launcher fired by/at children under 14 is unambiguously a "children's product" under CPSC. We cannot legally sell it without compliance.

What's required (verified 2026-05-23)

Source: CPSC Toy Safety Business Guidance and CPSC ASTM F963 Requirements.

  1. Comply with ASTM F963-23 — the current Standard Consumer Safety Specification for Toy Safety. F963-23 became effective April 20, 2024, replacing F963-17. This is the federal mandatory toy-safety standard.
  2. Third-party test at a CPSC-accepted lab — independent test report on a representative production sample. Test must be at a CPSC-accepted laboratory; tests from any other lab are not valid. Lab list at cpsc.gov.
  3. Issue a Children's Product Certificate (CPC) based on the test report — a written statement identifying the product, applicable standards, testing lab, date/place of manufacture, date/place of testing, and contact info for whoever holds the test records.
  4. Tracking label on the product — manufacturer name, date and location of manufacture, batch or run identifier, contact info or batch source.
  5. Re-test on any material/design change — even small material changes can require fresh testing.

What ASTM F963-23 tests (relevant subset for our product)

  • Small parts hazard — anything that fits in the small-parts cylinder (~1.25" diameter × 2.25" long) for products intended for under-3s, with warning-label requirement for 3+. Our torpedoes will likely qualify as small-parts hazards.
  • Sharp edges / sharp points
  • Use & abuse cycling — simulated rough handling (drop, torque, tension, compression tests)
  • Projectile kinetic energy — F963 has specific KE limits for toys that launch projectiles. This is the test that matters most for our launcher. Limits are in joules; if our launcher exceeds the limit, design changes are mandatory.
  • Lead content in surface coatings (90 ppm limit)
  • Phthalates in any flexible plastic (0.1% / 1000 ppm limit on 6 specific phthalates)
  • Heavy metals migration (soluble lead, cadmium, etc.)
  • Flammability
  • Choking warning labels in proper format

Realistic budget for our product: $1,500-3,000 for first cert. Our launcher has multiple components (launcher body, projectiles, possibly elastic bands or seals) — each material may need separate testing. Expect to be near the top of that range, not the bottom.

Important: test only after design is locked. Re-testing on changes adds full cost again. This is why prototype + iterate happens BEFORE certification (Phase 5 in our order-of-ops, funded by Kickstarter $$$).

Cost-saving tactics (not corner-cutting — compliance is non-negotiable; this is just sequencing)

  1. Use a US-accepted lab in China for $500-1,200 first-cert — if our China manufacturer recommends one already accepted by CPSC. Saves 50%+ vs US labs. Verify any chosen lab is on the CPSC-accepted lab list before paying.
  2. Reduce phthalate-bearing materials — if no flexible plastic parts, the phthalate testing line item ($150-300) drops.
  3. Use a single elastomer formula across all flex parts — one material test instead of three.
  4. Lock the design before testing — no fresh tests on minor changes if avoidable.

Projectile KE — design constraint

ASTM F963 Section 4.21 covers projectile toys. The standard sets KE-per-area limits. Smith homework before final design: read F963-23 Section 4.21 specifically and target our launcher to come in under the limit by 30%+ margin. This means we may have to deliberately limit muzzle velocity for the safety/compliance win. The Aerobie / Toypedo "magic feel" should come from hydrodynamic glide, NOT raw launch velocity. This is actually a feature: a safer-feeling premium product is exactly the positioning.

Section 4 — Product liability insurance

What it covers

Pays defense costs + judgments if a kid is injured by our product. Required by every major retailer and almost every fulfillment partner before they'll carry a toy. We don't need it on day 1 (prototype, Kickstarter) but we need it before shipping to backers.

Cost (verified 2026-05-23)

Source Quote Notes
Insurance Canopy (small manufacturer) $800-$1,400 / year source
Broader product liability range $700-$3,000 / year Embroker – Product Liability Cost
Toy store (retailer, not us) $1,200-$4,000 / year Farmer Brown – Toy Store Insurance

Realistic Year-1 budget for our product: $1,000-2,000. We're a small manufacturer with a single SKU, a children's product (which increases the premium), but Kickstarter-scale volume (which lowers it). Mid-range.

Coverage targets (industry-typical for retail entry)

  • $1M / occurrence and $2M / aggregate — minimum for most retailer agreements
  • Product recall coverage — often a separate rider, ~$300-700/year add-on
  • Defense costs outside limits — preferable so claim defense doesn't eat coverage

When we buy

Not until production is funded and we have a CPC. Insurers will ask for the test report and CPC; without those, the quote is higher or coverage is refused. So order: 1. Kickstarter funds arrive 2. Production run committed 3. Lab test passes; CPC issued 4. Bind insurance policy 5. Ship to backers

Smith's deliverable, when CPC issued

  • Quotes from 3 carriers (Insurance Canopy, Hiscox, Embroker, or similar) using the actual CPC + product spec
  • Recommended limits + recall rider analysis
