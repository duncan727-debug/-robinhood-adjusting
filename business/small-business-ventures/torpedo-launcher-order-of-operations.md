# Torpedo launcher — order of operations (Kickstarter path)

**Why this doc exists:** Duncan asked on 2026-05-23 — "I thought if put a nice ad on kickstarter and raise funds, we could use those for our operations?" That's *partly* right and *partly* wrong, and the gap matters. This doc lays out the actual sequence so we don't burn capital out of order.

---

## The short version

**You're right that Kickstarter money pays for production.** Tooling, manufacturing runs, fulfillment, compliance testing — all funded by backer pledges.

**You're not right that the Kickstarter campaign happens first.** A campaign needs:
1. A *working* prototype (you can hold it on camera)
2. A *hero video* that makes someone click "Back this project"
3. A *pre-launch email list* of 500-2,000 people who said "notify me at launch"

That last item is what separates the 60% of Kickstarter projects that fail from the ~40% that succeed. **Marketing dollars are spent BEFORE the campaign**, building that list. Then on day 1, that list converts at 5-15%, hits the funding goal fast, which triggers Kickstarter's own algorithm to surface the project to its broader audience.

**So the order is: prototype → list → campaign → tooling.** Not campaign → everything.

| Day | Activity |

**Realistic funding target for a first-time premium pool toy:** $15,000-40,000. Above that, Kickstarter algorithmically favors you. Below ~$8,000 and you'll struggle to be taken seriously by retail buyers later.

**Gate:** If funding goal isn't 30% hit in the first 48 hours, the project will likely fail. Decide at hour 72 whether to ad-burst to recover or refund and re-launch later with a better foundation.

## The single biggest risk in this sequence

**Skipping the prelaunch list.** Almost every failed Kickstarter campaign failed at this step — they thought "post a great video, viewers will come." Kickstarter doesn't work that way. It rewards day-1 momentum, which requires a pre-built audience.

If we get to week 10 with only 100 emails on the list, the campaign almost certainly fails. The decision point is "did Phase 3 work?" — and if it didn't, we delay the launch by 4-8 weeks and try again, rather than launching a campaign that flops publicly.
