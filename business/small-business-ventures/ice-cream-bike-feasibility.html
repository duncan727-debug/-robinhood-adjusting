Ice-cream bike for the son — feasibility & numbers

Question: Buy a bicycle/tricycle with a freezer up front, son sells novelty ice cream at parks. Is this a real thing? What does it cost? Will Florida let us?

Short answer: Real and well-established (Icicle Tricycles, Worksman, generic Alibaba builds). The bike is the easy part. The hard part is where he can legally park it — Florida and Palm Beach County aggressively restrict mobile food vending in public parks. Numbers below assume we solve the venue problem with private events + apartment complexes + private property rentals — NOT public parks.

The regulatory wall (the actual problem)

Florida state level — mostly OK

  • Pre-packaged ice cream (Drumsticks, ice cream sandwiches, fruit bars from a distributor) is treated as a pre-packaged frozen food, NOT food service. This is the easy path.
  • Pre-packaged sellers need a Florida Sales Tax certificate (free, online at floridarevenue.com) and a DBPR Mobile Food Dispensing Vehicle license IF the bike crosses the "food service" line. Selling sealed novelties typically does NOT trigger MFDV. Confirm with DBPR before launch.
  • If we ever scoop, dispense, or open product, everything changes — full mobile food unit license, plan review, commissary requirement. Don't.

Palm Beach County / Wellington — the real choke point

  • PBC parks (county-operated, Okeeheelee, John Prince, etc.) prohibit vending without a concession agreement. These are not easy to get and typically go to established vendors via RFP.
  • Wellington Village parks (Village Park, Tiger Shark Cove, etc.) — same rule. Vendor permits required, granted sparingly.
  • State parks (Jonathan Dickinson, etc.) — concession-only.
  • Right-of-way / sidewalk vending — PBC requires a peddler's license; Wellington requires a Business Tax Receipt + zoning compliance. Mobile vending is prohibited on most public rights-of-way in residential zones.

What makes this work or kill it

Works because: - Son's hours of operation = his after-school + weekends. Zero opportunity cost for him. - Florida weather → 9-month season (Oct-Jun + sporadic summer). - Cash + Square: no AR, no collections. - Word-of-mouth in a planned community like Wellington compounds fast. - "Boy with an ice-cream bike at the HOA pool party" sells itself.

Kills it if: - He tries to park in a public park and gets a cease-and-desist (likely happens twice before he learns). - Lousy product (off-brand sticks-tasting-like-cardboard novelties). Pay for Blue Bell / Klondike / Talenti — quality drives repeat bookings. - Skipping insurance and a kid gets a tooth chipped — settle for $5K-$50K out of pocket. Get the rider. - Tries to scoop or hand-dip — instant regulatory escalation. Stay pre-packaged.

Bottom line

Yes, real thing. ~$3-4K startup. Y1 net $9-36K depending on hustle. Father-son skill match is excellent: dad does logistics/permits/insurance/CRM-sourced bookings; son does the on-bike work that's actually fun for a teenager.

Catch: the "ride it to the park" mental model doesn't work in PBC. The business is booked private events + farmers markets + HOA gigs, not roaming public parks. Once you accept that framing, it's a strong fit — and your existing network is built-in distribution.
