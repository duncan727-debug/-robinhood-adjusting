## The hierarchy of trust

For creators who are selling their judgment and _taste_ — advice, curation, analysis, a recommendation the audience will act on — trust is critical, completely foundational. 

After many conversations with creators and studying their businesses, I've come to see trust as built in a hierarchy from the bottom up.

View image: (https://media.beehiiv.com/cdn-cgi/image/fit=scale-down,format=auto,onerror=redirect,quality=80/uploads/asset/file/a51a3505-b296-485e-a3f9-4d004bb267f0/image__334_.png?t=1780625454)
Caption: Designed by [Laura Calle Puerta](https://www.linkedin.com/in/lauracallep/)

**For creators, trust is the currency earned by proving your values over time. Trust is what audiences give you in exchange for the feeling of belonging.**

* It starts with your **values**: what you actually stand for before anyone's watching. 

* Then **legibility**: your ability to make those values clear and evident.

* Then **consistency**: legibility over time. 

* Then **accountability**: proving those values even under pressure, when it costs you something. 

* And at the very top, **belonging**: the point where an audience starts treating you as part of who they are.

Notice the shift that happens at the top of the trust pyramid, as values made legible and and proven durable over time create belonging. The bottom four rungs are all things the _creator_ does — actions, broadcast outward. Belonging is what the _audience_ builds back, in response to everything below it. You can't manufacture belonging directly, but you can build the infrastructure for it by consciously stacking the rungs beneath it.

Audience belonging is most durable when it’s built on a shared reality others can clearly understand and participate in. Communities _can and do_ form without that foundation, but those relationships often look like parasocial attachment, one-way devotion untested by disagreement or change. 

When the hidden reality finally surfaces — like an undisclosed sponsor, revealing the creator’s values as disingenuous — the whole thing collapses at once. We've all seen it happen.

Ultimately, trust is about establishing a shared reality and making it legible to other people. In a fragmented media environment, trust becomes less about universal agreement and more about alignment. Creators thrive in this ecosystem because they offer audiences a coherent set of values, an interpretation of events that feels consistent and believable, and an approach to making sense of the world they can subscribe to.

## Trust in practice across creator archetypes

All creators depend on trust, but the stakes of that trust vary dramatically from one archetype to the next. 


* **There’s the advice creator**, who tells you how to _do_ something with stakes attached — the personal-finance explainer, the fitness coach, the career or parenting voice. Follow bad advice here and you lose money, time, or worse.

* **The curator**, who tells you what's worth your attention or money, the reviewer, the tastemaker, the "I bought it so you don't have to" voice. Their whole value lies in their calibrated taste and clean incentives.

* **The analyst**, who tells you what something _means_ — the markets commentator, the tech explainer, the one making sense of the news. You're trusting their read on reality, which is the most fragile thing to lend out and the easiest to bend.

* **The practitioner**, who teaches the craft they actually practice — the creator-journalist, the founder, the marketing director, or lawyer posting from inside the field. 

* **The guide**, who doesn't just inform but convenes — the community builder whose audience has become a place. Here, trust is already mutual, and belonging is doing the work.

Spectacle creators trade in attention — you don't necessarily need to _trust_ MrBeast to watch a video, you just need to be entertained. 

It helps to think of this less as two camps and more as a spectrum, sorted by a single question: how much time and/or money is the audience being asked to hand over? 

At one end sits pure spectacle — you watch, you're entertained, and nothing leaves your hands (other than, of course, your time). At the other end sits pure delegation, where you act on the creator's word at real cost to yourself. The further a creator moves toward that end, the more trust becomes central to the entire product.
