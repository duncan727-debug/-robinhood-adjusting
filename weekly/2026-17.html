This Week's Top Stories
⚡ Time-Sensitive · Active Claims Window

April 7 Flood Claims Entering the Acknowledgment Pipeline Now

Property owners affected by the April 7 flooding events across South Florida are now entering the statutory claims pipeline. Under Florida law, carriers must acknowledge a claim within 14 days of notice — meaning homeowners and business owners who filed in the days following April 7 are receiving their first formal carrier communications this week.

This is a critical window. Carrier adjusters are being assigned, initial inspection visits are being scheduled, and preliminary repair scopes are being drafted — often before the full extent of water damage is visible or documented. Structural moisture, mold precursors, and secondary damage to mechanical systems often don't manifest until weeks after the initial event.

What this means for you If you were affected by the April 7 event and have not yet had an independent assessment of your damage, now is the time — before the carrier's scope becomes the baseline for your settlement.
📊 Market Conditions

CSU Releases Slightly Below-Normal Hurricane Forecast — But Don't Read It That Way

Colorado State University's April 2026 hurricane season forecast calls for a slightly below-normal Atlantic season, citing cooler-than-expected sea surface temperatures in key development zones. "Below normal" in this context means fewer named storms than average — not zero, and not safe.

The most destructive seasons in recent Florida history were not the most active. A single landfalling storm in a below-normal year can cause more damage to South Florida than five storms that stay offshore in an active year. The CSU forecast affects global storm counts; it does not forecast path, intensity at landfall, or where any individual storm makes impact.

What this means for you Pre-season documentation and policy review are exactly as important this year as any other. The 38-day window before June 1 is the time to photograph your property, verify your coverage limits, and identify gaps before you need them.
⚖️ Legislative & Regulatory

Citizens Depopulation Continues — What It Means When Your Policy Moves

Citizens Property Insurance Corporation continues its managed depopulation effort, moving policies to private market carriers through its "take-out" program. Tens of thousands of South Florida policyholders have received or will receive notices that their Citizens policy is being assumed by a private carrier.

The new carrier is required to offer coverage at no more than 20% above the Citizens rate, but the terms, exclusions, and claims handling practices vary significantly. Policyholders who accept the offer without reviewing the new policy may find differences in wind deductibles, water damage sub-limits, or loss-of-use provisions that don't become apparent until a claim.

What this means for you If you've received a Citizens take-out notice, compare the declarations page of the new policy side-by-side before accepting. Pay particular attention to the wind deductible (which may now be a percentage of dwelling value rather than a flat dollar amount) and any exclusions added for your specific property type.
📍 Regional Focus · St. Lucie County

St. Lucie Policyholders Face 6% Rate Hike — and Older Roofs Are the Core Issue

St. Lucie County policyholders are absorbing a 6% average rate increase this cycle, the largest of any county in the current OIR filing round. Carriers cite aging housing stock — particularly roofs 15+ years old in Port St. Lucie and Fort Pierce — as the primary driver of underwriting losses and rate pressure.

The practical consequence: homeowners with pre-2010 roofs are seeing both higher premiums and more aggressive depreciation arguments when they file wind or water damage claims. Carriers increasingly cite "functional obsolescence" to reduce actual cash value settlements on older roof systems, even when the damage is clearly storm-related.

What this means for you If you're a St. Lucie homeowner with a roof older than 15 years and you've experienced any storm-related damage, an independent adjuster's scope and estimate is especially important. The difference between replacement cost value and actual cash value on an older roof can run into tens of thousands of dollars.
⚖️ Enforcement

Broward Fraud Arrest Highlights Importance of Choosing a Licensed PA

A Broward County public adjuster was arrested this month on charges related to insurance fraud and unlicensed activity. The case involves allegations of inflated estimates and unauthorized solicitation of homeowners in declared disaster areas, practices that undermine the credibility of the profession and harm policyholders.

The Florida Department of Financial Services licenses public adjusters and maintains a public lookup database. A licensed PA is bonded, subject to continuing education requirements, and bound by the Florida Public Adjuster Code of Ethics.

What this means for you Always verify your PA's license before signing a retainer. You can confirm any Florida public adjuster's license status at the Florida DFS MyProfile portal. A legitimate PA will welcome the question.
📊 Market Conditions · NFIP

NFIP Reauthorization Deadline Approaches — Flood Coverage Uncertainty Continues

The National Flood Insurance Program faces another reauthorization cliff as Congress approaches the current authorization deadline. Short-term extensions have become routine, but each lapse or near-lapse creates uncertainty for property owners in flood zones who require NFIP coverage to satisfy mortgage requirements.

For South Florida property owners, this has downstream effects on closings, refinances, and the availability of NFIP claims adjusters during peak storm season. Private flood market alternatives are expanding but remain more expensive and less standardized for high-risk zones.

What this means for you If your property is in a FEMA-designated flood zone, confirm your policy is active and understand its coverage limits independent of any wind or homeowners policy. NFIP policies cover structure and contents separately — limits may not reflect current replacement costs.
